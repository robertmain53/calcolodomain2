Glossary of Terms

  • Kelvin (K): The base unit of temperature in the International System of Units (SI). It is one of the seven base SI units and is defined by taking the fixed numerical value of the Boltzmann constant.
  • Celsius (°C): A scale and unit of measurement for temperature. It is named after the Swedish astronomer Anders Celsius and is widely used in scientific and everyday contexts.
  • Absolute Zero: The lowest possible temperature, equivalent to 0 K or -273.15 °C, where all molecular motion ceases.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is Kelvin used for?

Kelvin is used in scientific contexts because it is an absolute thermodynamic temperature scale. It's essential in physics, chemistry, and engineering for calculations involving thermal energy, gas laws, and thermodynamic processes.

How do I convert Celsius to Kelvin?

Add 273.15 to the Celsius temperature to get Kelvin. The formula is: \( K = °C + 273.15 \)

Why is Kelvin important?

Kelvin provides a scale that starts at absolute zero, making it crucial for many scientific calculations and research. It eliminates negative values in thermodynamic equations and is the SI base unit for temperature.

Can temperatures in Kelvin be negative?

No, temperatures in Kelvin cannot be negative because the scale begins at absolute zero (0 K), the lowest possible temperature in the universe.
