      <div>
        <h3 class="text-xl font-semibold">Methodology</h3>
        <div class="bg-gray-50 border border-gray-200 rounded-2xl p-4 text-gray-700">
          <p>
            The Beaufort scale is defined by documented ranges. The calculator first normalizes your input to km/h, then looks up the table entry whose range contains that value. No interpolation beyond the listed values is performed so results match published tables.
          </p>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3 class="text-xl font-semibold">Formula</h3>
        <p>
          Example: 10 km/h ÷ 3.6 = 2.78 m/s, which is in the range 1.6–3.3 m/s → Beaufort 2.
        </p>
        <div class="formula-box">
          <p>Normalized m/s = km/h ÷ 3.6. Use the normalized speed to find the corresponding Beaufort range.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3 class="text-xl font-semibold">Standard ranges used</h3>
        <p>
          The table follows common international references such as WMO, the UK Met Office, and major meteorological services. Minor rounding between knots, km/h and m/s does not affect the Beaufort force itself.
        </p>
      </div>
      <div>
        <h3 class="text-xl font-semibold">Notes</h3>
        <ul class="text-gray-700 space-y-2">
          <li>If your marine authority uses slightly different knot spacing, convert to km/h and compare with the table.</li>
          <li>Beaufort describes observable effects, not just a number—read the sea and land column before making decisions.</li>
          <li>For storm warnings always follow your national meteorological service.</li>
        </ul>
