How this sewing calculator works

This sewing calculator combines several tools that sewists commonly need but usually have to open on separate websites: fabric yardage, circle skirt planning, binding and bias tape, and elastic length. It supports both imperial and metric units and adds seam and hem allowances automatically.

1. Fabric yardage for rectangular pieces

The Fabric yardage tab assumes you are cutting identical rectangles from a bolt of fabric with a given width. It first adds seam allowance to your finished dimensions:

Cut width = finished width + 2 × seam allowance

Cut length = finished length + 2 × seam allowance

Then it estimates how many pieces fit across the fabric width and how many rows you need along the length:

Pieces per row = floor(fabric width ÷ cut width)

Number of rows = ceil(piece count ÷ pieces per row)

Total fabric length = rows × cut length

Finally, it adds your extra percentage for shrinkage or safety and converts the result into yards or meters.

2. Circle skirt calculations

For circle skirts, the calculator uses the waist measurement to compute the radius of the inner circle (waist) and adds skirt length and allowances for the outer radius.

For a full circle skirt:

Waist radius = waist ÷ (2π)

Outer radius = waist radius + skirt length + hem allowance + waist seam allowance

Half and quarter circle skirts adjust the waist radius based on the angle of the skirt (180° or 90°). The tool then compares the required diameter with your fabric width to decide whether the skirt fits on a single fold or must be cut in panels.

3. Binding and bias tape

The Binding & bias tab starts from the total edge length you want to bind. It adds a small safety margin and divides by your fabric width to estimate how many strips you need and how much fabric length is required.

Total binding length = edge length × 1.05 (5% extra)

Number of strips = ceil(total binding length ÷ fabric width)

Fabric length = number of strips × strip width

For true bias binding, the estimate is slightly increased to account for diagonal layout and joining losses.

4. Elastic and waistband

The Elastic & waistband tab calculates elastic length based on your body measurement, ease, and overlap for joining the elastic:

Elastic length = body measurement + ease + overlap

It also suggests a cut height for the waistband casing:

Casing cut height = 2 × casing finished height + hem/top allowance

Tips for accurate sewing yardage

  • Round up generously. Fabric shops usually cut in 0.25 or 0.5 yard increments; always round up to the next convenient amount.
  • Consider directional prints and nap. If your fabric has a clear up/down direction or nap (like velvet), you may need extra yardage beyond the geometric estimate.
  • Pre-wash when needed. If you pre-wash fabric, use the shrinkage percentage field or add extra manually.
  • Check pattern layout. Commercial patterns sometimes require more fabric than simple math suggests due to piece shapes and grainline requirements.

Common sewing questions

How much fabric do I need for a simple gathered skirt?

For a basic gathered skirt, a common rule of thumb is 1.5–3 times your waist measurement for the skirt width. You can use the Fabric yardage tab by treating the skirt as one or two large rectangles: width = waist × fullness factor, length = desired skirt length + hem and waistband allowances.

Can I mix inches and centimeters?

For best accuracy, pick one system (imperial or metric) and stick with it. This calculator lets you switch globally between inches/yards and centimeters/meters so all fields stay consistent.

Sewing calculator FAQ

How much fabric do I need for a project?
Use the Fabric yardage tab. Enter your fabric width, number of pieces, finished width and length, and seam allowance. The calculator estimates total fabric length and converts it to yards or meters, including optional extra for shrinkage.
Does this work for quilting?
Yes. Treat quilt blocks or strips as rectangular pieces. For binding, use the Binding & bias tab with your quilt perimeter and desired binding width. The tool will estimate both binding length and fabric needed for strips.
How do I calculate fabric for curtains?
Measure finished curtain width and multiply by your fullness factor (usually 1.5–2.5×). Use that as the piece width, and your finished drop plus hem and header allowances as the piece length. Enter the number of panels you plan to sew and your fabric width to get a yardage estimate.
Is the circle skirt calculator for knit or woven fabrics?
It works for both, but you may choose different ease. For knits, you might reduce the waist measurement slightly for negative ease. For wovens, keep the waist true to body measurement and rely on a zipper or button closure.
