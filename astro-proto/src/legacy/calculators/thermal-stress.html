Thermal stress basics

When a solid body is heated or cooled, it tends to expand or contract. If this thermal expansion is restrained by supports, connections, or neighboring materials, internal forces develop. These forces create thermal stress, which can cause yielding, cracking, or buckling if not accounted for.

Key relationships (1D, linear elastic):

  • Thermal strain (free expansion): \( \varepsilon_{th} = \alpha \,\Delta T \)
  • Hooke’s law (uniaxial): \( \sigma = E\,\varepsilon_{mech} \)
  • Fully constrained bar (net strain ≈ 0): \( \sigma \approx -E\,\alpha\,\Delta T \)
  • Plane stress (thin plate, biaxial): \( \sigma \approx \dfrac{E\,\alpha\,\Delta T}{1-\nu} \)

Variables

  • E – Young’s modulus (Pa, MPa, GPa)
  • α – coefficient of linear thermal expansion (1/°C or 1/K)
  • ΔT – temperature change (T₂ − T₁)
  • ν – Poisson’s ratio (dimensionless)
  • σ – normal stress (Pa, MPa)
  • ε – strain (dimensionless)

How this thermal stress calculator works

This tool assumes a uniform temperature change and linear elastic behavior. It is ideal for quick checks on rods, beams, and plate-like components where 1D or plane-stress approximations are reasonable.

Supported modes

  • Thermal stress: given E, α, and ΔT, compute σ and ε.
  • Thermal strain: given α and ΔT, compute ε (no E needed).
  • Required ΔT: given E, α, and target σ, compute the ΔT that would cause that stress.

Step-by-step example

Problem: A 2 m long steel bar (E = 200 GPa, α = 12×10⁻⁶ /°C) is rigidly fixed at both ends. It is heated from 20 °C to 120 °C. What thermal stress develops?

  1. Temperature change: \( \Delta T = 120 - 20 = 100\,^\circ\text{C} \)
  2. Thermal strain (free): \( \varepsilon_{th} = \alpha \Delta T = 12\times10^{-6} \times 100 = 0.0012 \)
  3. Because the bar is fully restrained, mechanical strain ≈ −0.0012.
  4. Stress: \( \sigma = E \varepsilon_{mech} = 200\times10^9 \times (-0.0012) = -240\times10^6 \,\text{Pa} = -240\,\text{MPa} \)

The negative sign indicates compression. 240 MPa is close to the yield strength of many structural steels, so in practice you would need to check allowable stresses and consider expansion joints.

Assumptions and limitations

  • Uniform temperature through the member (no gradients).
  • Linear elastic material behavior (no plasticity, creep, or cracking modeled).
  • 1D bar or plane-stress plate approximation.
  • Small strains (α·ΔT ≪ 1).

For complex geometries, non-uniform heating, or safety-critical components (e.g., pressure vessels, turbine blades, electronic packages), use this calculator only for preliminary estimates and rely on detailed finite element analysis and applicable design codes for final design.

Frequently asked questions

Is thermal stress always harmful?

Not necessarily. Many structures are designed to tolerate thermal stress within allowable limits. Problems arise when constraints are too rigid, temperature swings are large, or materials with very different α values are bonded together (e.g., glass–metal joints, bimetallic strips, electronic packages).

How can I reduce thermal stress?

  • Allow movement with expansion joints or sliding supports.
  • Use materials with lower α or better high-temperature strength.
  • Reduce temperature gradients (insulation, controlled heating/cooling rates).
  • Use flexible connections or compliant layers between dissimilar materials.

What about thermal shock?

Thermal shock occurs when a component experiences a rapid, non-uniform temperature change, creating steep thermal gradients and high local stresses. This calculator does not model thermal shock; it assumes uniform ΔT. Thermal shock analysis typically requires transient heat transfer and stress analysis, often via FEA.
