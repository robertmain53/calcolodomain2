How to read nutrition labels for sodium

On most labels, sodium is listed in milligrams (mg) per serving. Enter that number in the “Sodium per serving” field, and the number of servings you ate.

If the label lists salt instead of sodium, 1 g of salt ≈ 400 mg of sodium (since table salt is ~40% sodium by weight).

Corrected Sodium in Hyperglycemia

Hyperglycemia causes a dilutional decrease in measured serum sodium. This calculator estimates the “corrected” sodium as if blood glucose were normal. It is commonly used in diabetes and critical care settings.

Understanding sodium intake and corrected sodium

Sodium is an essential electrolyte that helps regulate fluid balance, blood pressure, and nerve and muscle function. However, too much sodium can raise blood pressure and increase the risk of heart disease, stroke, and kidney disease.

In clinical settings, sodium is also a key lab value. Conditions like hyponatremia (low sodium) and hypernatremia (high sodium) can be life-threatening, and hyperglycemia can make sodium appear lower than it truly is.

Typical daily sodium targets

  • General population: Aim for less than 2,300 mg/day.
  • High blood pressure, heart or kidney disease, diabetes: Many guidelines suggest around 1,500 mg/day, unless your clinician advises otherwise.
  • Very low sodium diets: Below 1,200 mg/day are sometimes used short-term under medical supervision.

The intake calculator on this page helps you see how your usual foods add up compared with these targets.

Corrected sodium in hyperglycemia

When blood glucose is very high, water shifts from cells into the bloodstream, diluting serum sodium. The measured sodium may look dangerously low even though part of the drop is due to this dilutional effect.

The corrected sodium formula estimates what the sodium concentration would be if glucose were normal (100 mg/dL). This is particularly useful in patients with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS).

Limitations and safety

  • The correction formula is an approximation and may not fit every patient.
  • Do not use this calculator to decide IV fluid rates or sodium correction targets on your own.
  • Rapid changes in serum sodium can cause serious neurologic injury; management must be guided by a clinician.

This tool does not store or transmit your inputs. It is intended for education and general planning only and is not a substitute for professional medical advice, diagnosis, or treatment.
