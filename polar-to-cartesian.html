Formulas

\[ x = r \cdot \cos(\theta) \]

\[ y = r \cdot \sin(\theta) \]

Where r is the radial distance from the origin, and θ (theta) is the angle measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis.

How It Works

Polar coordinates represent points in a 2D plane using a distance from the origin (r) and an angle (θ). The conversion to Cartesian coordinates involves using trigonometric functions to project the radial distance onto the x and y axes.

Example Calculation

To convert polar coordinates (r=5, θ=45°) to Cartesian coordinates:

  1. Calculate x: x = 5 × cos(45°) ≈ 3.54
  2. Calculate y: y = 5 × sin(45°) ≈ 3.54
  3. Result: (5, 45°) in polar = (3.54, 3.54) in Cartesian

Frequently Asked Questions

What are polar coordinates?

Polar coordinates represent a point in a 2D plane using a distance from the origin (r) and an angle (θ) from the positive x-axis. This system is particularly useful for circular or rotational problems.

When should I use polar coordinates?

Use polar coordinates when dealing with circular patterns, rotational motion, or problems with radial symmetry. They simplify calculations involving circles, spirals, and angular relationships.

What is the angle θ measured in?

Our calculator accepts θ in degrees, measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis. The angle can also be expressed in radians by multiplying degrees by π/180.

Can r be negative?

Yes, a negative r indicates the point is in the opposite direction from the angle θ. For example, (r=-5, θ=45°) is equivalent to (r=5, θ=225°).

Data Source and Methodology

All calculations are based on standard trigonometric formulas for coordinate system conversions. The sine and cosine functions are used to project the radial distance onto the Cartesian axes.
