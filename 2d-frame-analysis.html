Understanding 2D Frame Analysis

2D frame analysis is a fundamental technique in structural engineering for determining internal forces, moments, and displacements in planar structural systems. This calculator focuses on simply-supported beam elements, which form the basis for more complex frame analysis.

Key Concepts

  • Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL): A load that is spread evenly along the length of a structural member
  • Bending Moment: Internal moment that causes a beam to bend
  • Shear Force: Internal force that acts perpendicular to the beam axis
  • Deflection: Vertical displacement of the beam under load
  • Elastic Modulus (E): Material property indicating stiffness
  • Moment of Inertia (I): Geometric property indicating resistance to bending

Typical Material Properties

Elastic Modulus (E)

  • Steel: 200,000 MPa (200 GPa)
  • Concrete: 20,000-40,000 MPa (20-40 GPa)
  • Aluminum: 69,000 MPa (69 GPa)
  • Wood: 8,000-15,000 MPa (8-15 GPa)

How It Works: Worked Example

Consider a simply-supported steel beam with the following properties:

  • Span length (L) = 6 meters
  • Distributed load (w) = 20 kN/m
  • Elastic modulus (E) = 200,000 MPa (steel)
  • Moment of inertia (I) = 0.0001 m⁴

Step 1: Calculate Maximum Moment

M_max = (wL²) / 8 = (20 × 6²) / 8 = 90 kN·m

Step 2: Calculate Maximum Shear

V_max = (wL) / 2 = (20 × 6) / 2 = 60 kN

Step 3: Calculate Maximum Deflection

δ_max = (5wL⁴) / (384EI) = (5 × 20 × 10³ × 6⁴) / (384 × 200,000 × 10⁶ × 0.0001)

δ_max = 16.4 mm

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is 2D frame analysis used for?

2D frame analysis is used in structural engineering to determine internal forces, moments, and displacements in planar structures like beams, frames, and trusses. It's essential for ensuring structural safety and meeting building codes.

What assumptions does this calculator make?

This calculator assumes: (1) Linear elastic material behavior, (2) Simply-supported boundary conditions, (3) Uniformly distributed load, (4) Small deflections, (5) Euler-Bernoulli beam theory applies (plane sections remain plane).

How do I calculate moment of inertia (I)?

For rectangular cross-sections: I = bh³/12 where b is width and h is height. For standard steel sections (I-beams, channels), use published section properties from steel handbooks or manufacturer data.

Can this calculator handle point loads or multiple loads?

This calculator is specifically designed for uniformly distributed loads on simply-supported beams. For point loads or combined loading, use specialized structural analysis software or hand calculations using superposition.

What deflection limits should I use?

Building codes typically limit deflection to L/240 to L/360 for floors and roofs under live loads. For a 6m beam, this would be 25mm to 17mm maximum deflection. Always consult applicable building codes.

Is this suitable for real-world design?

This calculator provides preliminary analysis for educational purposes and conceptual design. For final structural design, use professional software and have work reviewed by a licensed structural engineer.
