The Formula Explained

From standard form \( f(x)=ax^2+bx+c,\, a\neq 0 \):
\( h=-\dfrac{b}{2a},\quad k=f(h)=a h^2+b h+c \).

Vertex form \( f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k \) gives the vertex directly as \( (h,k) \).

Axis of symmetry: \( x=h \). Direction: up if \( a>0 \), down if \( a<0 \).

For \( f(x)=a(x-h)^2+k \): focus \( \left(h,\,k+\dfrac{1}{4a}\right) \), directrix \( y=k-\dfrac{1}{4a} \).

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Given: \( f(x)=2x^2-8x+3 \).

  1. Compute \( h=-\frac{b}{2a}=-\frac{-8}{2\cdot 2}=2 \).
  2. Evaluate \( k=f(2)=2(4)-8(2)+3=8-16+3=-5 \).
  3. Vertex is \( (2,-5) \); axis is \( x=2 \); since \( a=2>0 \), the vertex is a minimum.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a = 0 in a quadratic?

No. If a = 0, the function is linear and has no vertex (as a turning point).

How do I convert standard form to vertex form?

Complete the square: \( ax^2+bx+c = a[(x-\frac{-b}{2a})^2 - (\frac{b}{2a})^2] + c \), then group constants to get \( a(x-h)^2+k \).

What if my inputs produce very large values?

The plot auto-scales, but for readability try rescaling (e.g., divide coefficients) — the vertex is invariant under uniform scaling of the function.

Can I get focus and directrix?

Yes—when using vertex form. The calculator shows them in the detailed readout if desired.

Does this handle negative or fractional inputs?

Yes. All fields accept decimals and negatives.
