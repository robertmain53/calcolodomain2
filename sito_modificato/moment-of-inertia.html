What is moment of inertia?

Moment of inertia describes how strongly a shape or body resists bending or changes in rotational speed, depending on how its area or mass is distributed relative to an axis.

  • Area moment of inertia (second moment of area) – used in beam bending and deflection. Units: m⁴, cm⁴, in⁴, mm⁴.
  • Mass moment of inertia – used in rotational dynamics (e.g., τ = I·α). Units: kg·m², slug·ft², etc.

General definitions

Area moment of inertia about an axis \(x\):

\[ I_x = \int_A y^2 \, dA \]

Mass moment of inertia about an axis \(z\):

\[ I_z = \int_V r^2 \, dm \]

where \(y\) or \(r\) is the perpendicular distance from the axis.

Formulas used in this calculator

1. Rectangle (area moment, centroidal axes)

Rectangle of width \(b\) and height \(h\), axes through the centroid:

\[ I_x = \frac{b h^3}{12}, \qquad I_y = \frac{h b^3}{12} \]

\(I_x\): about horizontal axis (parallel to base), \(I_y\): about vertical axis.

2. Solid circle (area moment, centroidal axis)

Circle of radius \(R\), about a diameter or centroidal axis in the plane:

\[ I = \frac{\pi R^4}{4} \]

3. Hollow circle / tube (area moment)

Outer radius \(R_o\), inner radius \(R_i\):

\[ I = \frac{\pi}{4} \left(R_o^4 - R_i^4\right) \]

4. Thin rectangular plate about an edge (area moment)

Width \(b\), height \(h\), axis along one edge (using parallel‑axis theorem):

\[ I_{\text{edge}} = I_c + A d^2 = \frac{b h^3}{12} + b h \left(\frac{h}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{b h^3}{3} \]

5. Thin rod (mass moment)

  • Rod of length \(L\), mass \(m\), axis through center, perpendicular to rod:

\[ I_{\text{center}} = \frac{1}{12} m L^2 \]

  • Rod of length \(L\), mass \(m\), axis through one end, perpendicular to rod:

\[ I_{\text{end}} = \frac{1}{3} m L^2 \]

6. Solid disc / cylinder (mass moment)

Disc or solid cylinder of mass \(m\), radius \(R\), axis through center, perpendicular to face:

\[ I = \frac{1}{2} m R^2 \]

7. Solid sphere (mass moment)

Sphere of mass \(m\), radius \(R\), axis through center (any diameter):

\[ I = \frac{2}{5} m R^2 \]

8. Parallel‑axis theorem

To shift from a centroidal axis to a parallel axis at distance \(d\):

Area moment:

\[ I = I_c + A d^2 \]

Mass moment:

\[ I = I_c + m d^2 \]

Worked example: area moment of inertia of a rectangular beam

Suppose you have a rectangular steel beam with:

  • Width \(b = 200 \text{ mm}\)
  • Height \(h = 400 \text{ mm}\)

We want \(I_x\) about the horizontal centroidal axis (strong axis).

  1. Convert units if needed (here we stay in mm).
  2. Use \(I_x = \dfrac{b h^3}{12}\).
  3. Compute: \[ I_x = \frac{200 \times 400^3}{12} = \frac{200 \times 64{,}000{,}000}{12} = \frac{12{,}800{,}000{,}000}{12} \approx 1.067 \times 10^9 \,\text{mm}^4. \]

In SI base units, \(1 \text{ mm}^4 = 10^{-12} \text{ m}^4\), so:

\[ I_x \approx 1.067 \times 10^9 \times 10^{-12} = 1.067 \times 10^{-3} \,\text{m}^4. \]

Common pitfalls and tips

  • Be consistent with units. Do not mix mm and m or in and ft without converting.
  • Choose the correct axis. For beams, the axis perpendicular to the bending plane is usually needed.
  • Use the right formula. Area moments and mass moments are not interchangeable.
  • For composite sections, compute \(I\) for each simple shape about a common axis and sum them, using the parallel‑axis theorem as needed.

Moment of inertia – FAQ

What is moment of inertia?

Moment of inertia measures how area or mass is distributed relative to an axis. A larger moment of inertia means the body is harder to bend (for beams) or harder to spin up or slow down (for rotating machinery).

What is the difference between area and mass moment of inertia?

Area moment of inertia (m⁴, in⁴) is purely geometric and appears in beam formulas for deflection and bending stress. Mass moment of inertia (kg·m², slug·ft²) depends on mass and is used in rotational dynamics, for example in τ = I·α and rotational kinetic energy \(E = \tfrac{1}{2} I \omega^2\).

Can I use this calculator for composite sections?

Yes, but you must break the composite section into basic shapes (rectangles, circles, etc.), compute each shape’s moment of inertia about a common axis (using the parallel‑axis theorem), and then sum the results. This calculator gives you the building blocks for each simple shape.

Which units should I choose?

For structural work, mm and mm⁴ or in and in⁴ are common. For dynamics, use SI units (m, kg, kg·m²) whenever possible. Our tool keeps your units consistent and reports the final units explicitly in the result.
