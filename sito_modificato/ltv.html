What is Loan-to-Value (LTV)?

Loan-to-Value (LTV) is a percentage that compares the amount of a loan to the value of the asset used as collateral, typically a property. It is one of the key indicators lenders use to measure risk when they assess a mortgage or secured loan.

LTV formula

\[ \text{LTV (\%)} = \frac{\text{Loan amount}}{\text{Property value}} \times 100 \]

Example: a loan of 180,000 on a property worth 240,000 gives \[ \text{LTV} = \frac{180{,}000}{240{,}000} \times 100 = 75\%. \]

How to use the LTV calculator

Interpreting your LTV result

Lenders typically group LTV into ranges, each with different conditions:

  • ≤ 60% – very low LTV
    Strong equity position, best risk profile. Often eligible for the most competitive interest rates.
  • 60–80% – standard LTV
    Typical for many mortgages. Rates are still generally good, but slightly higher than very low LTV.
  • 80–90% – higher LTV
    Higher risk for the lender. You may face stricter underwriting, higher rates, or insurance requirements.
  • > 90% – very high LTV
    Maximum-risk band. Only some lenders or special products allow this, often with higher costs and tighter conditions.

Why lenders care about LTV

LTV affects:

  • Approval chances – very high LTV can lead to rejection or require a guarantor.
  • Interest rate – lower LTV usually means lower rates.
  • Required insurance – in many markets, LTV above a threshold triggers mortgage insurance or extra guarantees.
  • Maximum loan amount – lenders often publish maximum LTV limits by product and property type.

Worked examples

Example 1 – You know the loan amount

Suppose you buy a home for 250,000 and need a loan of 200,000.

  • Property value = 250,000
  • Loan amount = 200,000

\[ \text{LTV} = \frac{200{,}000}{250{,}000} \times 100 = 80\%. \]

Your LTV is 80%. You are at the upper end of the “standard” band: many lenders still accept this, but a slightly larger down payment could unlock better rates.

Example 2 – You know the down payment

You want to buy a property worth 300,000 and can put down 60,000 (20%).

  • Property value = 300,000
  • Down payment = 60,000
  • Loan amount = 300,000 − 60,000 = 240,000

\[ \text{LTV} = \frac{240{,}000}{300{,}000} \times 100 = 80\%. \]

Again, your LTV is 80%. Increasing your down payment to 75,000 (25%) would reduce LTV to 75% and likely improve your borrowing conditions.

How to improve your LTV

  • Increase your down payment – save more or use equity from another property.
  • Choose a cheaper property – a lower price with the same savings reduces LTV.
  • Renegotiate after value increases – if the property value rises and you have repaid principal, your LTV falls.
  • Avoid additional borrowing secured on the same property – extra loans raise LTV again.

Limitations and notes

  • The calculator uses the values you enter and does not replace a professional appraisal.
  • Lenders may use slightly different valuation criteria or include other secured debts on the same property.
  • LTV is only one factor; income, credit history, and regulations also matter.

Loan-to-Value (LTV) – FAQ

What is a good LTV ratio for a mortgage?

Many lenders consider ≤ 80% LTV as “good”: you bring at least 20% equity. This often gives access to better interest rates and may avoid mortgage insurance or additional guarantees. Very low LTV (for example 50–60%) is even safer and can unlock the best conditions.

Is LTV based on purchase price or appraised value?

Lenders typically use the lower of the purchase price and the appraised market value. If you buy below market value, your effective LTV may be lower than you expect; if you overpay relative to the appraisal, your LTV may be higher and the bank may reduce the loan amount.

Does LTV affect refinancing as well?

Yes. When you refinance, the bank recalculates LTV using the current loan balance and current property value. If your LTV has fallen thanks to repayments or price increases, you may qualify for better rates or be able to remove mortgage insurance.

Is a higher LTV always bad?

Not necessarily. A higher LTV lets you buy with a smaller down payment, which can be useful if you have strong income but limited savings. However, it usually means higher monthly payments, higher interest costs over time, and less protection if property prices fall.
