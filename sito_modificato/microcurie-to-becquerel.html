Conversion formula between microcurie and becquerel

The becquerel (Bq) is the SI unit of radioactivity and represents one nuclear decay per second. The curie (Ci) is an older unit based on the activity of 1 gram of radium‑226.

Exact relationships:

  • \(1 \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}\)
  • \(1 \text{ µCi} = 10^{-6} \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{4} \text{ Bq}\)
  • \(1 \text{ Bq} \approx 2.7027027 \times 10^{-5} \text{ µCi}\)

From microcurie to becquerel (µCi → Bq)

\( \text{Bq} = \text{µCi} \times 3.7 \times 10^{4} \)

Example: Convert 2.5 µCi to Bq.

\( \text{Bq} = 2.5 \times 3.7 \times 10^{4} = 9.25 \times 10^{4} \text{ Bq} = 92{,}500 \text{ Bq} \)

From becquerel to microcurie (Bq → µCi)

\( \text{µCi} = \dfrac{\text{Bq}}{3.7 \times 10^{4}} \)

Example: Convert 1,000,000 Bq to µCi.

\( \text{µCi} = \dfrac{1{,}000{,}000}{3.7 \times 10^{4}} \approx 27.027 \text{ µCi} \)

What do microcurie and becquerel measure?

Both units describe radioactive activity – how many nuclear decays occur in a given time.

  • Becquerel (Bq) – SI unit, defined as one decay per second.
  • Curie (Ci) – legacy unit, defined as \(3.7 \times 10^{10}\) decays per second.
  • Microcurie (µCi) – one millionth of a curie: \(1 \text{ µCi} = 10^{-6} \text{ Ci}\).

Note: Activity (Bq or µCi) does not directly indicate dose to a person (measured in sievert, Sv). Dose depends on radiation type, energy, exposure geometry, and shielding.

Frequently asked questions

How accurate is this microcurie to becquerel converter?

The calculator uses the exact factor \(1 \text{ Ci} = 3.7 \times 10^{10} \text{ Bq}\) and performs calculations in double‑precision floating point. You can choose the number of decimal places for display; the internal computation is more precise than the rounded output.

Can I enter values in scientific notation?

Yes. Both input fields accept scientific notation such as 1e6, 3.7E4, or 2.5e-3. This is especially useful for very large or very small activities.

When should I use µCi vs Bq?

Use Bq for SI‑compliant scientific work, regulatory reporting, and most modern publications. Use µCi when working with older literature, certain nuclear medicine protocols, or equipment that still reports in curies or microcuries.
