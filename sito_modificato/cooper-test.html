What is the Cooper test?

The Cooper test is a classic 12‑minute running test created by Dr. Kenneth H. Cooper in 1968 to assess aerobic fitness and estimate VO₂ max (maximal oxygen uptake). It is widely used by coaches, military organizations, and sports scientists.

The protocol is simple: after a proper warm‑up, you run on a track or flat surface for exactly 12 minutes, trying to cover the maximum possible distance. The distance you cover is then converted into an estimate of your VO₂ max and a fitness rating.

Cooper test VO₂ max formula

For adults, a commonly used equation is:

VO₂ max (ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹)

$$VO_2\text{ max} = \frac{d\_{\text{meters}} - 504.9}{44.73}$$

where \(d\_{\text{meters}}\) is the distance you ran in 12 minutes, measured in meters.

If you also know your body weight, you can estimate absolute VO₂ in liters per minute:

$$VO_2\ (\text{L/min}) = \frac{VO_2\text{ max (ml·kg}^{-1}\text{·min}^{-1}) \times \text{weight (kg)}}{1000}$$

How to perform the Cooper test correctly

  1. Medical clearance (if needed) – If you have heart, lung, or metabolic disease, or are very deconditioned, talk to your doctor first.
  2. Choose the surface – Ideally a 400 m track. Otherwise, use a flat, measured route or GPS watch.
  3. Warm up 10–15 minutes – Easy jog + dynamic drills (leg swings, skips, short strides).
  4. Pace yourself – Start slightly conservatively and build up. It should feel hard but sustainable for 12 minutes.
  5. Record the distance – Note total meters or laps + partial lap at the 12‑minute mark.
  6. Cool down – 5–10 minutes of easy jogging or walking and light stretching.

Interpreting your Cooper test result

  • VO₂ max – Higher values indicate better aerobic capacity and endurance potential.
  • Fitness rating – Puts your result into context for your age and sex.
  • Equivalent paces – Help you translate the test into training speeds (e.g. tempo or interval pace).
  • Progress over time – Use the comparison mode to see how your VO₂ max changes across training blocks.

Limitations and safety

  • The Cooper test is a maximal effort and may not be suitable for everyone.
  • Environmental factors (heat, wind, altitude) and pacing strategy can affect your result.
  • VO₂ max is estimated, not directly measured in a lab with gas analysis.
  • Always stop immediately if you feel chest pain, dizziness, or unusual shortness of breath.

FAQs

Is the Cooper test accurate for VO₂ max?

For groups of healthy adults, the Cooper test correlates reasonably well with laboratory VO₂ max measurements. For individuals, the estimate can be off by several ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹, but it is very useful for tracking relative changes over time.

Can I walk during the Cooper test?

Yes, especially if you are a beginner. However, the more you have to walk, the more the result reflects your current conditioning rather than your potential VO₂ max. Use it as a baseline and repeat after a few weeks of training.

How often should I repeat it?

Every 6–8 weeks is a good compromise: often enough to see progress, but not so often that you disrupt your training with frequent maximal efforts.
