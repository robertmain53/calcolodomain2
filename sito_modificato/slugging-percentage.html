What is slugging percentage?

Slugging percentage (SLG) is a baseball and softball statistic that measures how many total bases a hitter earns per at-bat. It captures a player’s power, not just how often they get hits.

Unlike batting average, which treats all hits the same, slugging percentage gives more weight to doubles, triples, and home runs.

Slugging percentage formula

SLG = Total Bases ÷ At-Bats

Total Bases (TB) = 1 × 1B + 2 × 2B + 3 × 3B + 4 × HR

SLG = (1B + 2·2B + 3·3B + 4·HR) ÷ AB

Key points:

  • AB (at-bats) excludes walks, hit-by-pitch, and sacrifices.
  • 1B, 2B, 3B, HR are counts of singles, doubles, triples, and home runs.
  • SLG is usually shown to three decimal places, e.g. .475.

Worked example

Suppose a player has the following season line:

  • AB = 400
  • 1B = 60
  • 2B = 25
  • 3B = 5
  • HR = 20

First, compute total bases:

TB = 1×60 + 2×25 + 3×5 + 4×20
TB = 60 + 50 + 15 + 80 = 205

Then divide by at-bats:

SLG = 205 ÷ 400 = 0.5125 ≈ 0.513

We would say this hitter has a .513 slugging percentage, which is strong power production.

What is a good slugging percentage?

Context matters (league, era, ballpark), but for modern MLB a rough guide is:

Slugging % (SLG) Interpretation
Below .400 Below-average power
.400 – .449 Modest / slightly below average
.450 – .499 Around league average
.500 – .549 Good power hitter
.550 – .599 Excellent power
.600+ Elite / MVP-level power season

Slugging percentage vs. batting average vs. OPS

  • Batting average (AVG) = H ÷ AB. Every hit counts the same.
  • Slugging percentage (SLG) = TB ÷ AB. Rewards extra-base hits.
  • On-base percentage (OBP) includes walks and HBP.
  • OPS = OBP + SLG, combining getting on base and hitting for power.

A player can have a high AVG but low SLG (lots of singles) or a modest AVG but high SLG (lower contact, huge power).

Common questions about slugging percentage

Does slugging percentage include walks?

No. Walks, hit-by-pitches, and sacrifice bunts/flies do not count as at-bats, so they are not part of the slugging percentage calculation. They do matter for on-base percentage, but not for SLG.

Can slugging percentage be greater than 1.000?

Yes, especially in small samples. If a player has more than one base per at-bat on average (for example, several home runs in a handful of at-bats), SLG can exceed 1.000. Over a full season, SLG above 1.000 is essentially impossible.

Why is it called a “percentage” if it’s written like .500?

Historically, many baseball stats are written as .XYZ but called “percentages”. You can think of SLG as “bases per at-bat”. A .500 slugging percentage means the hitter averages half a base per at-bat.
