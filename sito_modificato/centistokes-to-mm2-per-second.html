How to convert centistokes to mm²/s

Centistokes (cSt) and square millimeters per second (mm²/s) are two ways of writing the same physical unit of kinematic viscosity. In SI units:

1 stoke (St) = 1 cm²/s
1 centistoke (cSt) = 0.01 St = 0.01 cm²/s

1 cm² = (10 mm)² = 100 mm²
0.01 cm²/s = 0.01 × 100 mm²/s = 1 mm²/s

⇒ 1 cSt = 1 mm²/s

Therefore the conversion is trivial:

νmm²/s = νcSt
νcSt = νmm²/s

The only reason to “convert” is to match the unit system used in your standards, datasheets, or simulation software.

Worked examples

  • Example 1: 32 cSt oil at 40 °C
    32 cSt = 32 mm²/s
  • Example 2: 68 mm²/s hydraulic oil
    68 mm²/s = 68 cSt
  • Example 3: Very viscous fluid, 450 cSt
    450 cSt = 450 mm²/s

From dynamic viscosity (mPa·s, cP) to cSt / mm²/s

Often you know the dynamic viscosity μ (in mPa·s or cP) and the density ρ. Kinematic viscosity ν is:

ν = μ / ρ

In practical lab units:
ν (cSt or mm²/s) = μ (mPa·s or cP) ÷ ρ (g/cm³)

Example: A fluid has μ = 10 mPa·s and ρ = 0.80 g/cm³.

ν = 10 / 0.80 = 12.5 cSt = 12.5 mm²/s

Typical kinematic viscosity ranges

  • Water at 20 °C: ≈ 1 cSt (1 mm²/s)
  • Light hydraulic oil: 10–32 cSt
  • Gear oils: 68–460 cSt (and higher)
  • Very thick oils / syrups: > 1,000 cSt

FAQ

Is there any conversion factor between cSt and mm²/s?

No numerical factor is needed. The factor is exactly 1: 1 cSt = 1 mm²/s. Our calculator simply mirrors the value with controlled rounding and formatting.

Why do some datasheets use cSt and others mm²/s?

Centistokes come from the older CGS system (stokes), while mm²/s is the pure SI expression. Many lubrication and automotive standards still use cSt, but ISO viscosity grades are fundamentally based on mm²/s at 40 °C and 100 °C. In practice, engineers treat them interchangeably.

Does temperature affect the conversion?

Temperature strongly affects the value of kinematic viscosity, but not the relationship between units. At any temperature, the numerical value in cSt equals the value in mm²/s.
