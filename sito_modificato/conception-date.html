How this conception date calculator works

This tool estimates when you likely conceived by working backward from standard pregnancy dating methods. Pregnancy length is usually counted as 280 days (40 weeks) from the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP), even though conception typically happens about 2 weeks after LMP.

1. From due date

If you already have an estimated due date (EDD) from your clinician or an early ultrasound, the calculator:

  • Subtracts 280 days from the due date to estimate your LMP.
  • Adds an estimated ovulation offset (usually 14 days, adjusted for your cycle length) to estimate conception.

Step 1 – Estimate LMP:

LMP ≈ Due date − 280 days

Step 2 – Estimate ovulation:

Ovulation day ≈ LMP + (Cycle length − 14)

Step 3 – Estimate conception:

Conception ≈ Ovulation day

2. From last menstrual period (LMP)

When you enter the first day of your last period and your typical cycle length, we assume:

  • Ovulation occurs about cycle length − 14 days after LMP.
  • Conception occurs on the ovulation day or within a few days around it.

3. From ultrasound date and gestational age

Early ultrasounds (especially in the first trimester) are often the most accurate way to date a pregnancy. If you know the ultrasound date and the gestational age reported (for example, 8 weeks 3 days), the calculator:

  1. Subtracts the gestational age from the ultrasound date to estimate LMP.
  2. Adds 14 days to estimate conception (for a typical 28-day cycle).

Gestational age in days:

GAdays = 7 × weeks + extra days

Estimated LMP:

LMP ≈ Ultrasound date − GAdays

Estimated conception:

Conception ≈ LMP + 14 days

Understanding your fertile window

Sperm can survive in the reproductive tract for up to 5 days, and the egg can be fertilized for about 12–24 hours after ovulation. Because of this, conception can occur within a window of several days.

This calculator therefore shows:

  • Likely conception date: the most probable day based on your inputs.
  • Fertile window: usually 5 days before through 1 day after the estimated ovulation day.

Limitations and accuracy

  • Cycle length and ovulation day vary from person to person and even from month to month.
  • Implantation can occur several days after fertilization, which does not change conception date but may affect early symptoms and test timing.
  • Even early ultrasounds have a margin of error of a few days.

Because of these factors, no online calculator can give an exact conception timestamp. The results are best used as an educational estimate and starting point for discussion with your healthcare provider.

Frequently asked questions

Can this calculator be used for paternity questions?

It can help you understand the likely window when conception occurred, but it cannot reliably determine paternity. If there were multiple possible partners during that window, only DNA testing can answer paternity questions with confidence.

What if my cycles are irregular?

If your cycles vary a lot, any conception estimate based on LMP or cycle length becomes less precise. In that case, an early ultrasound is usually more reliable for dating the pregnancy.

Why is pregnancy counted from my period instead of conception?

Historically, the first day of the last menstrual period is an easier date to remember and record than the exact day of ovulation. Medical guidelines therefore define gestational age as the number of weeks since LMP, even though conception happens about two weeks later.

When should I talk to a healthcare professional?

Always contact your clinician if you are pregnant or think you might be pregnant, especially if you have pain, bleeding, or concerns about your dates. This calculator is for general information only and is not a substitute for medical advice, diagnosis, or care.
