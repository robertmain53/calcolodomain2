What is the Schofield equation?

The Schofield equation is a set of predictive equations that estimate Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) from body weight, age and sex. BMR is the energy your body uses at rest to maintain vital functions such as breathing, circulation and temperature regulation.

Schofield and colleagues derived these equations from a large dataset of indirect calorimetry measurements and they are widely used in clinical nutrition, dietetics and public health guidelines.

Schofield equations by age and sex

This calculator uses the standard weight-based Schofield equations (weight in kilograms). For each age band, a different linear equation is used:

Men

  • 3–10 years: \( \text{BMR} = 22.7 \times W + 495 \)
  • 10–18 years: \( \text{BMR} = 17.5 \times W + 651 \)
  • 18–30 years: \( \text{BMR} = 15.3 \times W + 679 \)
  • 30–60 years: \( \text{BMR} = 11.6 \times W + 879 \)
  • > 60 years: \( \text{BMR} = 13.5 \times W + 487 \)

where \( W \) is body weight in kilograms and BMR is in kcal/day.

Women

  • 3–10 years: \( \text{BMR} = 22.5 \times W + 499 \)
  • 10–18 years: \( \text{BMR} = 12.2 \times W + 746 \)
  • 18–30 years: \( \text{BMR} = 14.7 \times W + 496 \)
  • 30–60 years: \( \text{BMR} = 8.7 \times W + 829 \)
  • > 60 years: \( \text{BMR} = 10.5 \times W + 596 \)

where \( W \) is body weight in kilograms and BMR is in kcal/day.

How this Schofield calculator works

  1. You enter sex, age and weight (kg or lb).
  2. The tool converts weight in pounds to kilograms if needed.
  3. Based on age and sex, it automatically selects the correct Schofield equation.
  4. It calculates BMR in kcal/day and converts it to kJ/day.
  5. If you choose an activity level, it multiplies BMR by the corresponding factor to estimate total daily energy expenditure (TDEE).

From BMR to daily energy needs

To estimate total daily energy needs, BMR is multiplied by an activity factor:

  • Bed rest / ICU: ~1.2 × BMR
  • Sedentary: ~1.3 × BMR
  • Lightly active: ~1.5 × BMR
  • Moderately active: ~1.7 × BMR
  • Very active: ~2.0 × BMR (or higher)

These factors are approximate and may be adapted in clinical practice depending on disease state, stress factors and treatment goals.

Clinical use and limitations

The Schofield equations are commonly used in:

  • Hospital and outpatient nutrition assessment
  • Diet planning for weight maintenance, loss or gain
  • Public health recommendations for energy intake

However, they provide population-average estimates and may be less accurate for:

  • Very obese or very underweight individuals
  • Critically ill or highly catabolic patients
  • People with unusually high muscle mass or edema
  • Infants and very young children (special pediatric equations are preferred)

When available, indirect calorimetry remains the gold standard for measuring energy expenditure.

Example calculation

Consider a 35-year-old man weighing 80 kg.

  • Age band: 30–60 years (male)
  • Equation: \( \text{BMR} = 11.6 \times W + 879 \)
  • Calculation: \( 11.6 \times 80 + 879 = 1{,}807 \,\text{kcal/day (approx.)} \)

If he is moderately active (factor 1.7), estimated daily energy needs:

\( \text{TDEE} \approx 1{,}807 \times 1.7 \approx 3{,}072 \,\text{kcal/day} \)

Safety note

This calculator is intended for educational and general planning purposes. It does not replace medical advice. For patients with complex medical conditions, always consult a registered dietitian, physician or clinical nutrition specialist.
