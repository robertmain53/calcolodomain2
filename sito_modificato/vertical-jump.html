What is a vertical jump?

A vertical jump (or vertical leap) is a straight-up jump from the ground. In sports science and strength and conditioning, the vertical jump test is a standard way to assess lower-body power and explosive strength. It is commonly used in basketball, volleyball, football codes and many talent identification programs.

Types of vertical jump tests

  • Standing vertical jump (countermovement jump): jump from a standing position with a quick dip or countermovement.
  • Squat jump: jump starting from a static semi-squat without a prior countermovement.
  • Running or approach jump: jump performed after a run-up; useful in sport practice, but less standardised for testing.

For most testing protocols and comparison with published norms, the standing vertical jump or a standardised countermovement jump is used.

How vertical jump height is measured

1. Wall or Vertec reach method

  1. Measure your standing reach by standing side-on to a wall or device and reaching as high as possible with your arm.
  2. Mark this point (chalk on the fingers or a measuring device).
  3. Jump as high as you can and touch the highest possible point.
  4. The difference between jump reach and standing reach is your vertical jump height.

This is the most accessible method and is the basis for many field tests and normative tables.

2. Flight time method

If you have a jump mat, force plate, infrared system or a reliable high-speed video/app, you can measure the time in the air (flight time). From basic physics, jump height \(h\) can be estimated as:

Jump height from flight time

\( h = \dfrac{g \cdot t^2}{8} \)

where:

  • \(h\) = jump height (metres)
  • \(t\) = flight time (seconds)
  • \(g\) ≈ 9.81 m/s² (acceleration due to gravity)

The calculator uses this equation, then converts the result into centimetres and inches for practical use.

Estimating power from your vertical jump

Several research groups have proposed equations to estimate peak power output from vertical jump height and body mass. One widely used option is the Sayers equation for peak anaerobic power.

Sayers peak power equation

Peak Power (W) = 60.7 × jump height (cm) + 45.3 × body mass (kg) − 2055

This equation was developed and cross-validated in groups of trained and untrained athletes and has become a common field estimate of lower-limb power. Remember that it is still an estimate: use it to track progression across repeated tests rather than as an absolute laboratory value.

Best practices for a reliable vertical jump test

  • Warm up with light cardio and dynamic movements for 5–10 minutes.
  • Practice the technique with a few submaximal jumps before testing.
  • Keep technique consistent between trials (same arm swing, foot position and depth of countermovement).
  • Take several maximal attempts (for example 3–5) and record the best result.
  • Rest 30–60 seconds between jumps to minimise fatigue effects.
  • Use the same method, surface and equipment each time you re-test.

Safety and limitations

Maximal jumping loads the ankles, knees, hips and lower back. If you are recovering from injury, have joint pain, or live with medical conditions that limit vigorous exercise, consult a qualified professional before performing a vertical jump test.

This calculator is intended for athletes, coaches and fitness enthusiasts who want to better understand their performance. It does not replace a full clinical or performance assessment.
