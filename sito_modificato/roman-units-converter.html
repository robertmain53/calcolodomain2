Roman numerals vs. Roman units – two different systems

Modern websites often focus either on Roman numerals or on Roman measures. This page brings both worlds together: in the first tab you can convert numerals such as MMXXV to their decimal counterparts, while the second tab focuses on historical length, volume and weight units used throughout the Roman Empire.

Key assumptions behind the Roman units

Ancient standards were not perfectly uniform across provinces or periods. To make the converter practical and consistent, we adopt widely cited modern approximations:

  • Roman foot (pes): 0.296 m
  • Roman mile (mille passus): 1,000 paces = 1,480 m
  • Amphora quadrantal: about 26 L
  • Sextarius: 1/48 of an amphora (≈ 0.54 L)
  • Roman pound (libra): about 329 g

These values are close enough for courses in history, archaeology and engineering, and they keep internal ratios between Roman units exactly consistent.

How Roman numerals are interpreted

The numerals converter follows standard textbook rules:

  • Valid symbols: I, V, X, L, C, D, M.
  • Subtractive notation allowed only for I before V or X, X before L or C, C before D or M.
  • Repeated symbols limited to three in a row (e.g. III is valid, IIII is flagged).
  • Supported range: 1 to 3999 (I to MMMCMXCIX).

If you enter a malformed numeral, the tool will show an error message rather than silently returning an incorrect value.

FAQ: Roman units and this converter

Can I rely on these numbers in scientific work?

For high-level comparisons, teaching and exploratory calculations, yes. For original research on Roman metrology you should always quote the specific reconstruction (museum artefact, inscription or scholarly article) that motivates the values you use.

Why does the Roman mile differ from the modern mile?

The modern statute mile is about 1.609 km; the Roman mile is shorter at around 1.48 km because it is built from Roman feet, which are slightly shorter than modern imperial feet, and uses 1,000 paces as its definition.

How precise are the litre and gram conversions?

Internally the calculator keeps more decimal digits than it displays. The main source of uncertainty is historical, not numerical: different authors give slightly different reconstructions. Those differences are typically within a few percent.
