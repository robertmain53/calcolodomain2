Safety first: working with lye

  • Always wear gloves, goggles, and long sleeves.
  • Add lye to water, never water to lye.
  • Work in a well-ventilated area and keep children and pets away.
  • Use a reliable digital scale; do not measure by volume.

How this lye calculator works

This tool uses standard SAP (saponification) values to calculate how much sodium hydroxide (NaOH) or potassium hydroxide (KOH) you need to fully saponify the oils in your recipe, then applies your chosen superfat and water settings.

1. SAP values and basic formula

Each oil has a SAP value: the amount of lye needed to turn 1 gram of that oil into soap. For NaOH, SAP values are typically around 0.13–0.19 g NaOH per gram of oil.

Step 1 – Theoretical lye (no superfat):

\[ \text{Lye}_{\text{theoretical}} = \sum (\text{Oil weight}_i \times \text{SAP}_i) \]

Step 2 – Apply superfat:

\[ \text{Lye}_{\text{actual}} = \text{Lye}_{\text{theoretical}} \times (1 - \text{Superfat}/100) \]

For KOH, SAP values are higher. Many references multiply NaOH SAP by about 1.403 to get the KOH equivalent. This calculator stores both NaOH and KOH SAP values for common oils.

2. Water calculation

You can choose between two common approaches:

  • Water : lye ratio – e.g. 2.5:1 means: \[ \text{Water} = \text{Lye}_{\text{actual}} \times 2.5 \]
  • Lye concentration – percentage of lye in the lye solution: \[ \text{Lye concentration} = \frac{\text{Lye}}{\text{Water} + \text{Lye}} \times 100 \] Rearranged to find water: \[ \text{Water} = \text{Lye} \times \left(\frac{100}{C} - 1\right) \] where \(C\) is the lye concentration in %.

3. Working by oil weights vs. batch size

  • Oil weights mode – you directly enter the weight of each oil. The calculator sums them and computes lye and water.
  • Batch size mode – you enter the total oil weight and percentages for each oil. The calculator converts percentages to weights, then proceeds as above.

Recommended starting points

  • Superfat: 5% for general body bars, 3% for laundry soap, 7–8% for extra conditioning facial bars.
  • Water : lye ratio: 2.3–2.7 for most cold process recipes.
  • Lye concentration: 30–33% for a good balance of workability and cure time.

Frequently asked questions

Can I switch between grams and ounces?

Yes. The calculator internally works in grams and converts to ounces when you select the US unit system. You can switch units at any time; just recalculate to update the display.

Does this calculator handle dual-lye recipes?

At the moment, this tool assumes a single lye type per batch (either NaOH or KOH). For advanced dual-lye recipes (e.g. 60% KOH / 40% NaOH for cream soap), you can run two separate calculations and combine the lye amounts manually.

How accurate are the results?

The formulas and SAP values are consistent with what experienced soap makers use. The main sources of error in real life are inaccurate scales, mis-typed numbers, and variations in oil composition. Always double-check your inputs and consider running a small test batch when trying a new recipe.

Can I use this for hot process soap?

Yes. The lye and water calculations are the same for cold and hot process. For hot process, many makers use slightly lower water or higher lye concentration to speed up cook time, but the underlying math is identical.
