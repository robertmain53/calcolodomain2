Creatinine clearance and the Cockcroft–Gault equation

Creatinine clearance (CrCl) is an estimate of how much blood plasma is cleared of creatinine per minute. It is widely used to adjust doses of renally cleared medications, because most drug trials and labels historically referenced the Cockcroft–Gault equation.

Cockcroft–Gault formula

For serum creatinine in mg/dL and weight in kg:

$$CrCl\,(mL/min) = \\frac{(140 - \\text{age}) \\times \\text{weight (kg)} \\times F}{72 \\times S_{Cr}(mg/dL)}$$

where \(F = 1.0\) for males and \(F = 0.85\) for females.

This calculator automatically converts serum creatinine from µmol/L to mg/dL and supports different weight choices (actual, ideal, adjusted) to better reflect kidney function in underweight and obese patients.

Weight choices: ABW, IBW and adjusted body weight

The original Cockcroft–Gault paper used actual body weight (ABW), but this can overestimate kidney function in obesity and underestimate it in underweight patients. Many institutions therefore recommend:

  • IBW (ideal body weight) for normal‑weight patients.
  • Adjusted body weight (AdjBW) for obese patients (e.g., BMI ≥ 30 kg/m²).
  • Actual body weight for underweight patients (BMI < 18.5 kg/m²).

Ideal body weight (Devine formula)

Male: \( IBW = 50 + 2.3 \\times (\\text{height in inches} - 60) \) kg
Female: \( IBW = 45.5 + 2.3 \\times (\\text{height in inches} - 60) \) kg

Adjusted body weight (AdjBW)

\( AdjBW = IBW + 0.4 \\times (ABW - IBW) \)   (used when ABW > IBW)

The “Auto” mode in this tool follows a simple rule:

  • BMI < 18.5: use ABW.
  • 18.5 ≤ BMI < 30: use IBW.
  • BMI ≥ 30: use AdjBW.

This is a pragmatic approach and may differ from your local protocol; always follow institutional guidance where available.

Unit conversions used

When you select serum creatinine in µmol/L, the calculator converts it to mg/dL internally using:

\( S_{Cr}(mg/dL) = \\dfrac{S_{Cr}(\\mu mol/L)}{88.4} \)

Limitations and clinical caveats

  • Not validated in children, pregnancy, or AKI: Cockcroft–Gault is for adults with relatively stable kidney function.
  • Extremes of muscle mass: Very low or very high muscle mass (e.g., cachexia, bodybuilders, amputees) can make creatinine‑based estimates unreliable.
  • Rounding low creatinine: Some protocols round very low serum creatinine (e.g., < 0.6 mg/dL) to avoid overestimation, but practices vary.
  • Dialysis: This equation is not appropriate for patients on dialysis; use drug‑specific guidance.

Creatinine clearance is only one piece of the puzzle. Always interpret it in the context of the patient’s overall clinical picture, trends in kidney function, and specific drug recommendations.

Frequently asked questions

Should I use Cockcroft–Gault or eGFR for drug dosing?

Many drug labels and pivotal trials still specify renal dose adjustments based on Cockcroft–Gault creatinine clearance. For those medications, using Cockcroft–Gault keeps you aligned with the evidence base. For others, local guidelines may allow eGFR (CKD‑EPI) instead. Check your formulary or institutional policy.

How often should I recalculate creatinine clearance?

In stable outpatients, recalculation is often done when labs are repeated or when starting or changing renally cleared drugs. In hospitalized or critically ill patients, kidney function can change quickly, so more frequent reassessment is needed—sometimes daily or even more often.

What if the result is very different from the lab’s eGFR?

Differences are common because Cockcroft–Gault and eGFR equations use different assumptions and normalizations. Large discrepancies should prompt a review of the inputs (age, weight, units) and consideration of factors such as muscle mass, diet, and acute illness. When in doubt, discuss with a nephrologist or clinical pharmacist.
