How this mortgage calculator works

This tool models a standard fixed-rate, fully amortizing mortgage. It calculates your monthly principal and interest payment, then adds estimates for property taxes, homeowners insurance, private mortgage insurance (PMI), and HOA dues if you enter them.

You can also add recurring extra monthly payments and an annual lump-sum payment. These are applied directly to principal, which shortens your payoff time and reduces total interest.

Core mortgage payment formula

For a fixed-rate mortgage, the principal and interest portion of your monthly payment is:

Monthly payment (principal & interest)

Let:

  • P = loan amount (principal)
  • r = monthly interest rate = annual rate ÷ 12
  • n = total number of monthly payments (years × 12)

Then the monthly payment is:

\( M = P \cdot \dfrac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1} \)

Each month, interest is calculated on the remaining balance, and the rest of your payment reduces principal. Over time, the interest portion shrinks and the principal portion grows.

What is included in a mortgage payment?

A typical monthly mortgage payment can include:

  • Principal – the amount that pays down your loan balance.
  • Interest – the cost of borrowing from the lender.
  • Property taxes – often collected monthly and held in escrow.
  • Homeowners insurance – also often escrowed.
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI) – usually required when your down payment is < 20%.
  • HOA dues – if your property is in a homeowners association.

Understanding loan-to-value (LTV) and PMI

The loan-to-value ratio (LTV) is your loan amount divided by the home value. Many conventional lenders require PMI when LTV is above 80% (less than 20% down). This calculator:

  • Estimates PMI as an annual percentage of the current loan balance.
  • Stops PMI automatically once your LTV reaches 80% based on the original purchase price.

How extra payments save you money

Extra payments that go to principal reduce your balance faster. This has two effects:

  • You pay off the mortgage earlier (fewer total payments).
  • You pay less total interest over the life of the loan.

Even a modest extra monthly amount (for example, $100) can save tens of thousands of dollars in interest on a 30-year mortgage, depending on the loan size and rate.

Key mortgage concepts in plain language

What is a mortgage?

A mortgage is a loan used to buy real estate, where the property itself serves as collateral. If you fail to make payments as agreed, the lender can foreclose and take the property to recover what is owed.

Fixed-rate vs. adjustable-rate mortgages (ARM)

  • Fixed-rate mortgage: The interest rate stays the same for the entire term. Payments are predictable.
  • Adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM): The rate can change after an initial fixed period, based on a benchmark index plus a margin.

This calculator focuses on fixed-rate loans, which are easier to model and are common for long-term home financing.

Choosing a loan term: 15 vs. 30 years

  • Shorter term (e.g., 15 years): Higher monthly payment, lower interest rate, much less total interest.
  • Longer term (e.g., 30 years): Lower monthly payment, higher rate, more total interest, more flexibility.

A good rule of thumb is to pick the shortest term you can comfortably afford while still meeting other goals (emergency fund, retirement savings, etc.).

Practical tips before you get a mortgage

  • Check your credit reports and scores; better credit usually means a lower rate.
  • Estimate all housing costs (taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance), not just the mortgage.
  • Keep an emergency fund so you can handle income shocks or unexpected repairs.
  • Compare offers from multiple lenders and pay attention to both rate and closing costs.

Limitations and disclaimers

This calculator is for educational and planning purposes only. It does not provide legal, tax, or financial advice, and it does not guarantee loan approval or specific terms. Actual mortgage offers will depend on your lender, credit profile, property type, and local laws.

Mortgage Calculator FAQ

How accurate is this mortgage calculator? ▶
The calculator uses the same standard amortization formulas that lenders use for fixed-rate mortgages. Results should closely match lender quotes for principal and interest. Taxes, insurance, and PMI are estimates; your actual amounts may differ based on your location, insurer, and loan program.
Does this calculator support biweekly payments? ▶
Not directly. However, you can approximate a biweekly strategy by entering an extra monthly payment equal to about half of your regular monthly principal & interest payment. That roughly simulates making 13 full payments per year instead of 12.
Can I use this for FHA, VA, or USDA loans? ▶
You can use it to approximate payments by entering the correct loan amount, interest rate, and any required mortgage insurance as PMI. However, government-backed loans have specific rules and fees (such as upfront funding fees or mortgage insurance premiums) that are not fully modeled here. Always confirm details with your lender or a housing counselor.
What if my taxes or insurance change over time? ▶
Property taxes and insurance premiums often change from year to year. This calculator assumes they stay constant for simplicity. In reality, your escrow portion may rise or fall. Revisit the calculator periodically with updated numbers to keep your budget current.
