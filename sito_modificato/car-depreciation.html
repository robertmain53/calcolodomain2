How this car depreciation calculator works

This tool estimates how your car’s value declines over time using either a declining-balance (percentage-based) method or a straight-line method. It gives you a year‑by‑year schedule of:

  • Estimated value at the end of each year
  • Annual depreciation in dollars and percentage
  • Total loss compared with the original purchase price
  • Projected mileage each year

1. Declining-balance depreciation (percentage of current value)

This method assumes the car loses a fixed percentage of its current value each year, which matches how real-world used car prices usually behave.

Year 0 value: \( V_0 = \text{starting value} \)
Year n value: \( V_n = V_0 \times (1 - r)^n \)
where \( r \) is the annual depreciation rate (e.g. 0.18 for 18%).

If you enter a current market value, the calculator uses that as \( V_0 \). Otherwise, it estimates the current value from the purchase price and years already elapsed.

2. Straight-line depreciation (equal amount each year)

Straight-line depreciation spreads the loss evenly over the car’s useful life, down to a chosen salvage value.

Annual depreciation amount:
\( D = \dfrac{\text{Purchase price} - \text{Salvage value}}{\text{Useful life (years)}} \)

Year n value:
\( V_n = \max(\text{Purchase price} - n \times D,\ \text{Salvage value}) \)

This method is commonly used in accounting and tax schedules, but real market prices often fall faster in early years than straight-line suggests.

3. Mileage and age adjustments

The calculator also tracks your current mileage and expected annual mileage. While the core depreciation curve is driven by age and percentage loss, mileage helps you sanity‑check the result:

  • Very high mileage for age usually means more depreciation than average.
  • Very low mileage for age usually means less depreciation than average.

You can adjust the annual depreciation rate up or down to reflect how your usage compares with typical drivers.

Typical car depreciation patterns

Actual depreciation varies by brand, model, fuel type, and market conditions, but a common pattern is:

  • Year 1: 15–25% loss from the purchase price (biggest hit).
  • Years 2–5: roughly 10–15% per year.
  • After 5 years: many cars are worth 40–50% of original price.
  • After 10 years: often 15–25% of original price, depending on mileage and condition.

Use these ranges as a starting point for the annual rate, then refine based on your car’s segment:

  • Luxury & large SUVs: often depreciate faster.
  • Reliable economy cars & hybrids: often hold value better.
  • Limited‑production or enthusiast models: can depreciate slowly or even appreciate.

Straight-line vs. declining-balance: which should you use?

For personal planning (buy vs. keep vs. sell):

  • Choose declining-balance for a realistic market-style curve.
  • Use a rate like 18% and adjust based on your car’s type and mileage.

For simple accounting or budgeting:

  • Choose straight-line if you want the same dollar amount each year.
  • Set a reasonable useful life (e.g. 8–10 years) and a salvage value (e.g. 10–20% of purchase price).

Limitations and good practices

This calculator gives an estimate, not an official valuation. Real prices depend on:

  • Local used car market and economic conditions
  • Accident history, service records, and number of owners
  • Color, trim level, options, and transmission type
  • Fuel type (EV, hybrid, diesel, petrol) and regulatory changes

For an accurate selling or insurance value, always cross‑check with live listings and professional valuation tools, then use this calculator to explore what‑if scenarios over time.

Car depreciation FAQs

How do you calculate car depreciation per year?

You can either subtract a fixed dollar amount each year (straight-line) or apply a fixed percentage to the current value (declining-balance). The calculator supports both and shows the resulting value for each year.

Is car depreciation linear?

No. Most cars lose value faster in the first few years, then the curve flattens. That’s why a percentage-based (declining-balance) model usually matches reality better than a straight line, especially in the first 5 years.

How can I reduce my car’s depreciation?

Choose models with strong reliability and demand, avoid excessive mileage, keep up with maintenance, avoid accidents, and keep the car in clean, original condition. Buying used (2–3 years old) instead of new also avoids the steepest part of the curve.
