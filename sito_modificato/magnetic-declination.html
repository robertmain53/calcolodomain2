What is magnetic declination?

Magnetic declination is the angle between true north (geographic north) and magnetic north (the direction a compass needle points). It is:

  • East (positive) when magnetic north lies east of true north.
  • West (negative) when magnetic north lies west of true north.

Declination depends on your location and slowly changes over time as Earth’s magnetic field evolves. That’s why modern maps include a declination diagram and an annual rate of change.

True north vs magnetic north vs grid north

  • True north – direction toward Earth’s geographic North Pole.
  • Magnetic north – direction of the horizontal component of Earth’s magnetic field; where a compass points.
  • Grid north – direction of the vertical grid lines on a map projection (e.g., UTM). It can differ slightly from true north; the difference is called grid convergence.

Formulas used by the magnetic declination calculator

This tool focuses on the navigation math once you know the declination. It does not attempt to replicate NOAA’s geomagnetic models (like WMM or IGRF), which require spherical harmonics and up‑to‑date coefficients.

1. Sign convention

We use the common convention:

East declination = positive (+)

West declination = negative (−)

2. True bearing ↔ magnetic bearing

Let:

  • \( D \) = magnetic declination (°), east positive, west negative
  • \( T \) = true bearing (°)
  • \( M \) = magnetic bearing (°)

True → Magnetic (for using a compass to follow a map bearing):

\( M = T - D \)

Then wrap to 0–360°.

Magnetic → True (for plotting a compass bearing on a map):

\( T = M + D \)

Then wrap to 0–360°.

A popular memory aid is:

  • “East is least, West is best” for True → Magnetic:
    • East declination → subtract (bearing gets smaller).
    • West declination → add (bearing gets bigger).
  • Reverse the sign for Magnetic → True.

3. Including grid north (optional)

If your map uses a grid (e.g., UTM), it will show the angle between true north and grid north, called grid convergence, usually labeled as “grid north is X° east/west of true north”.

Let:

  • \( C \) = grid convergence (°), east positive, west negative (true → grid)
  • \( G \) = grid bearing (°)

True ↔ Grid:

\( G = T + C \)

\( T = G - C \)

Grid ↔ Magnetic (via true north):

\( G = M + D + C \)

\( M = G - D - C \)

The calculator applies these formulas automatically when you enable the grid options and enter a grid convergence.

Worked examples

Example 1 – True to magnetic, east declination

You have a map bearing of T = 60° and a local declination of +10° east. What compass bearing should you follow?

Given: \( T = 60^\circ \), \( D = +10^\circ \)

\( M = T - D = 60^\circ - 10^\circ = 50^\circ \)

Set your compass to 50° magnetic to follow a 60° true bearing.

Example 2 – Magnetic to true, west declination

Your compass reads M = 210° and the declination is 7° west (i.e., \( D = -7^\circ \)). What is the true bearing?

Given: \( M = 210^\circ \), \( D = -7^\circ \)

\( T = M + D = 210^\circ + (-7^\circ) = 203^\circ \)

The direction on the map is 203° true.

Best practices for using magnetic declination in the field

  • Always check the map’s declination diagram. It shows declination at the map’s publication date and the annual rate of change.
  • Update for the current year. Multiply the annual change by the number of years since the map date and adjust the declination accordingly.
  • Be consistent with sign conventions. Decide whether east is positive and west is negative (as this calculator does) and stick with it.
  • Normalize bearings. After any calculation, wrap the result into the 0–360° range.
  • Practice with a few examples. Run several known cases through the calculator until the “East is least, West is best” rule feels natural.

Limitations and data sources

This calculator does not compute declination from latitude/longitude and date. Instead, it provides a robust, transparent way to apply declination and grid convergence once you know them.

For authoritative declination values, use:

  • NOAA’s geomagnetic calculators (World Magnetic Model).
  • USGS or your national geological survey.
  • Official topographic maps, which include a declination diagram and annual change.

After you obtain the declination, this tool helps you avoid arithmetic mistakes and keeps your navigation consistent between map, compass, GPS, and GIS.

Magnetic declination – FAQ

What is magnetic declination?

Magnetic declination is the angle between true north (geographic north) and magnetic north (the direction a compass needle points). It is positive (east) when magnetic north lies east of true north, and negative (west) when it lies west. You must account for declination whenever you convert between map bearings and compass bearings.

How do I convert a true bearing to a magnetic bearing?

Use the rule “East is least, West is best” for true → magnetic:

  • If declination is east, subtract it from the true bearing.
  • If declination is west, add it to the true bearing.

Then wrap the result into the 0–360° range. The calculator does this automatically and shows the steps.

How often does magnetic declination change?

Declination changes slowly over time as Earth’s magnetic field evolves. In many regions it changes by a few minutes of arc per year; in some areas it can change by more than 0.1° per year. For casual hiking, updating every few years is usually enough. For surveying or aviation, use current values from official sources.

What is the difference between true north, magnetic north, and grid north?

True north points to the geographic North Pole. Magnetic north is where a compass needle points, toward Earth’s magnetic field. Grid north is the direction of the vertical grid lines on a projected map (like UTM). Grid north can differ from true north by a small angle called grid convergence.

Do I need internet access to use this magnetic declination calculator?

You need internet only to look up your local declination from an authoritative source (NOAA, USGS, national mapping agency, or an up‑to‑date online service). Once you know the declination, you can use this calculator offline to convert between true, magnetic, and grid bearings without doing the math by hand.
