Result

Clausius–Clapeyron equation (integrated form)

\[ \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) \]

What is the Clausius–Clapeyron equation?

The Clausius–Clapeyron equation describes how the saturation vapor pressure of a substance changes with temperature along a phase boundary (for example, liquid–vapor). In its commonly used integrated form (assuming ideal gas behavior and constant enthalpy of vaporization), it is

\[ \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) \]

where:

  • \(P_1, P_2\) = saturation vapor pressures at temperatures \(T_1, T_2\)
  • \(T_1, T_2\) = absolute temperatures in kelvin (K)
  • \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\) = molar enthalpy of vaporization (J·mol⁻¹ or kJ·mol⁻¹)
  • \(R\) = universal gas constant (8.314 J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ or 0.008314 kJ·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹)

Rearranged forms used in this calculator

1. Solve for unknown vapor pressure \(P_2\)

Starting from \(\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)\), exponentiate both sides:

\[ \frac{P_2}{P_1} = \exp\left[-\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)\right] \] \[ P_2 = P_1 \cdot \exp\left[-\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)\right] \]

2. Solve for unknown temperature \(T_2\)

Rearrange for \(1/T_2\):

\[ \ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) \] \[ \frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{R}{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) \] \[ T_2 = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{T_1} - \dfrac{R}{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}\ln\left(\dfrac{P_2}{P_1}\right)} \]

3. Solve for enthalpy of vaporization \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\)

Rearrange for \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\):

\[ \Delta H_{\text{vap}} = -\,R\,\dfrac{\ln\left(\dfrac{P_2}{P_1}\right)}{\dfrac{1}{T_2} - \dfrac{1}{T_1}} \]

Typical use cases

  • Estimate vapor pressure at a new temperature from a known reference point and tabulated \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\).
  • Estimate boiling point at reduced or elevated pressure (e.g., vacuum distillation, high-altitude boiling).
  • Determine \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\) from two experimental vapor-pressure measurements.

Assumptions and limitations

  • Vapor behaves as an ideal gas.
  • \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\) is approximately constant over the temperature range considered.
  • Applies along a single phase boundary (usually liquid–vapor) away from the critical point.
  • Accuracy decreases over very wide temperature ranges or at high pressures where non-ideal effects are important.

Worked example: water vapor pressure

Suppose the saturation pressure of water at \(T_1 = 373.15\ \text{K}\) (100 °C) is \(P_1 = 1.000\ \text{atm}\), and we want the saturation pressure at \(T_2 = 353.15\ \text{K}\) (80 °C). Take \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}} = 40.7\ \text{kJ·mol}^{-1}\) and \(R = 0.008314\ \text{kJ·mol}^{-1}\text{K}^{-1}\).

  1. Compute the temperature term: \[ \frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1} = \frac{1}{353.15} - \frac{1}{373.15} \approx 2.832\times10^{-3} - 2.679\times10^{-3} = 1.53\times10^{-4}\ \text{K}^{-1} \]
  2. Compute \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}/R\): \[ \frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R} = \frac{40.7}{0.008314} \approx 4.89\times10^{3} \]
  3. Compute the exponent: \[ -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right) \approx -4.89\times10^{3} \cdot 1.53\times10^{-4} \approx -0.75 \]
  4. Compute the pressure ratio and \(P_2\): \[ \frac{P_2}{P_1} = e^{-0.75} \approx 0.47 \quad\Rightarrow\quad P_2 \approx 0.47\ \text{atm} \]

Tabulated values give a saturation pressure of water at 80 °C of about 0.47 atm, so the Clausius–Clapeyron approximation is quite good in this range.

FAQ

What does the Clausius–Clapeyron equation calculate?

It relates saturation vapor pressure and temperature along a phase boundary. In practice, it is used to estimate how vapor pressure changes with temperature, or to determine the enthalpy of phase change from experimental data.

Which units should I use?

  • Temperatures: always in kelvin (K).
  • Pressures: any consistent unit (atm, bar, Pa, kPa, mmHg, etc.).
  • \(\Delta H_{\text{vap}}\): J/mol or kJ/mol, matching the choice of \(R\).

Can I use this for sublimation or fusion (melting)?

Yes. The same mathematical form applies to any first-order phase transition (fusion, vaporization, sublimation) if you use the appropriate enthalpy of phase change (\(\Delta H_{\text{fus}}\), \(\Delta H_{\text{sub}}\), etc.).

Why does the calculator use natural logarithms?

The Clausius–Clapeyron equation is derived using natural logarithms (ln). If you see base‑10 logarithms in tables or plots, they have been converted using \(\log_{10} x = \ln x / \ln 10\).
