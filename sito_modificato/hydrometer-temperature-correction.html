Data Source and Methodology

Authoritative data source: Kell, G. S. (1975), “Density, Thermal Expansivity, and Compressibility of Liquid Water from 0° to 150 °C,” Journal of Chemical & Engineering Data, 20(1), 97–105. DOI: 10.1021/je60064a005 . For broader context on water property standards see IAPWS-95 formulation ( iapws.org ).

All calculations are strictly based on the formulas and data provided by this source.

The Formula Explained

Corrected specific gravity:

$$ SG_{corr} = SG_{obs} \times \frac{\rho_w(T_{obs})}{\rho_w(T_{cal})} $$

Water density (kg/m^3) as a function of °C (Kell, 1975):

$$ \rho_w(T) = 999.842594 + 6.793952\times10^{-2}T - 9.095290\times10^{-3}T^2 + 1.001685\times10^{-4}T^3 - 1.120083\times10^{-6}T^4 + 6.536332\times10^{-9}T^5 $$

Unit conversions:

$$ T_{^\circ C} = (T_{^\circ F} - 32)\times\frac{5}{9} \quad ; \quad T_{^\circ F} = T_{^\circ C}\times\frac{9}{5} + 32 $$

SG ↔ Plato conversions (brewing standard approximations):

$$ P \approx 135.997\,SG^3 - 630.272\,SG^2 + 1111.14\,SG - 616.868 $$

$$ SG \approx 1 + \frac{P}{258.6 - 227.1\times\left(\frac{P}{258.2}\right)} $$

Glossary of Variables

  • SG_obs : Observed specific gravity on the hydrometer at the measured sample temperature.
  • T_obs : Temperature of the liquid when the observation was made.
  • T_cal : Hydrometer calibration temperature (usually 20 °C or 60 °F).
  • ρw(T) : Density of pure water at temperature T (°C) in kg/m³.
  • SG_corr : Temperature-corrected specific gravity referenced to the hydrometer’s calibration.
  • P (°P) : Degrees Plato (approximately equal to Brix for brewing), convertible to/from SG.

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Suppose your hydrometer reads 1.050 at a sample temperature of 30 °C , and your hydrometer is calibrated at 20 °C .

  1. Compute water densities: ρw(30 °C) ≈ 995.650 kg/m³; ρw(20 °C) ≈ 998.204 kg/m³.
  2. Find the ratio: r = ρw(30)/ρw(20) ≈ 0.99744.
  3. Apply the formula: SG_corr = 1.050 × 0.99744 ≈ 1.0475 .
  4. Convert to Plato: P ≈ 11.7 °P .

Thus, cooling the sample to the calibration temperature would have yielded approximately SG 1.0475 instead of 1.050.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Do I need to correct if my sample matches the calibration temperature?

No. When T_obs equals T_cal, the ratio is 1 and the observed reading already equals the corrected value.

Can I enter readings in Brix?

Yes. Select “Plato / Brix” and enter your Brix value. The tool converts it to SG internally, applies the correction, then reports both SG and °P.

Is this valid for seawater or highly sugared solutions?

The temperature correction is based on pure-water density ratios, which is the accepted practice for hydrometer correction in brewing and winemaking. Extremely high concentrations or non-aqueous solutions may require specialized instruments.

What accuracy can I expect?

The Kell polynomial is highly accurate from 0–100 °C. Overall accuracy also depends on your thermometer, hydrometer calibration, and reading technique (meniscus/parallax).

Why is the corrected SG lower at higher temperatures?

Warm water is less dense, so the hydrometer sinks slightly more and reads higher than it should. The correction scales it back down.

Does this calculator account for thermal expansion of the hydrometer stem?

The dominant effect is water density change; the stem’s thermal expansion is negligible compared to that and typically ignored in standard brewing practice.
