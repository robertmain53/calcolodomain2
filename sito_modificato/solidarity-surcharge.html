What is the solidarity surcharge (Solidaritätszuschlag)?

The solidarity surcharge (German: Solidaritätszuschlag, often shortened to Soli) is an additional German tax levied on top of:

  • personal income tax (Einkommensteuer), and
  • corporate income tax (Körperschaftsteuer).

It was introduced in 1991 to help finance the costs of German reunification and other federal projects. The statutory rate is 5.5% of the income or corporate tax, but since 2021 most individual taxpayers are fully or largely exempt.

How the calculator works

This calculator follows the current structure of the German solidarity surcharge in a simplified way:

  1. It estimates your income tax (for individuals) or corporate tax (for companies).
  2. It applies the tax-free threshold (Freigrenze) and phase-in zone for individuals.
  3. It applies the full 5.5% rate for corporations.

Core formula (full liability):

\[ \text{Solidarity surcharge} = 5.5\% \times \text{Income tax or Corporate tax} \]

For individuals, the actual amount may be lower due to the tax-free threshold and phase-in zone. The calculator approximates these rules for recent years.

1. Individuals (employees, self-employed)

For individuals, the solidarity surcharge is based on your income tax liability after credits. Since the 2021 reform:

  • Most low and middle incomes pay no Soli at all.
  • There is a phase-in zone where Soli gradually increases.
  • Only high earners pay the full 5.5% rate on their income tax.

The exact thresholds are defined in German tax law and change over time. This tool uses typical values and approximations for recent years to give you a realistic estimate, but it is not a certified tax program.

2. Corporations

Corporations (e.g. GmbH, AG) generally still pay the full solidarity surcharge on their corporate income tax:

\[ \text{Soli (corporation)} = 5.5\% \times \text{Körperschaftsteuer} \]

The calculator lets you enter your taxable profit and corporate tax rate (default 15%) and then applies the 5.5% surcharge.

  4. If it is in the phase-in zone, you pay a reduced Soli.
  5. Only above the upper threshold is the full 5.5% applied.

Assume:

  • Taxpayer type: Corporation
  • Taxable profit: €250,000
  • Corporate tax rate: 15%

Then:

  1. Corporate tax = 15% × €250,000 = €37,500

Formulas used in the calculator

1. Income tax estimation (simplified)

German income tax is calculated using progressive formulas defined in §32a EStG. For this calculator we use a simplified progressive model that closely approximates the official tax tables for recent years:

Simplified progressive structure:

  • 0% on income up to the basic allowance (Grundfreibetrag)
  • Progressively increasing rates on the middle income ranges
  • Top marginal rate of about 42–45% on high incomes

This is sufficient for estimating the solidarity surcharge, but your actual tax may differ slightly due to deductions, allowances, and special rules.

2. Solidarity surcharge for individuals

In law, the solidarity surcharge for individuals is defined using a tax-free threshold and a phase-in formula. Conceptually:

If income tax ≤ threshold: \[ \text{Soli} = 0 \]

If income tax is in phase-in zone: \[ \text{Soli} = \text{reduced percentage} \times \text{Income tax} \]

Above the upper limit: \[ \text{Soli} = 5.5\% \times \text{Income tax} \]

The calculator implements a smooth phase-in between the exemption threshold and the full rate to mimic the official rules.

FAQ about the German solidarity surcharge

Is the solidarity surcharge still in force?

Yes. The solidarity surcharge still exists in German tax law. However, since the 2021 reform, the vast majority of individual taxpayers no longer pay it, or pay only a small amount. Corporations generally continue to pay the full 5.5% on their corporate tax.

Does the solidarity surcharge apply to trade tax (Gewerbesteuer)?

No. The solidarity surcharge is levied on income tax and corporate tax, not on trade tax. However, trade tax itself can be substantial and is a separate municipal tax.

Is Soli included in my payslip?

If you are an employee and still liable for the solidarity surcharge, it will appear as a separate line on your monthly payslip and annual Lohnsteuerbescheinigung. If you are exempt, it will simply not be shown.

Can non-residents be subject to the solidarity surcharge?

Yes. If you have German-source income that is subject to German income tax or corporate tax (for example, rental income from a German property), the solidarity surcharge may also apply to that tax. The exact treatment depends on your residency status and any applicable double tax treaties.
