How this concrete slab calculator works

This tool estimates how much concrete you need for one or more rectangular slabs, then converts that volume into ready‑mix cubic yards, bag counts, approximate weight, and optional cost. It also gives a quick estimate of rebar or mesh layout based on your chosen spacing.

Volume formula for a concrete slab

Volume = Length × Width × Thickness

For multiple sections, the calculator sums the volume of each rectangle:

Total volume = Σ (Lengthi × Widthi × Thicknessi)

Internally, all dimensions are converted to feet (imperial) or meters (metric), then to:

  • Cubic feet and cubic yards (1 yd³ = 27 ft³)
  • Cubic meters (1 m³ ≈ 1.3079 yd³)

Waste allowance

No jobsite is perfect. Forms are not perfectly level, subgrade may be uneven, and some concrete is lost during placement and finishing. To account for this, the calculator multiplies the net volume by:

Volumewaste = Volumenet × (1 + Waste% / 100)

For small DIY projects, 10% waste is common. Experienced crews on larger slabs may use 5–7%.

Bag counts

Bagged concrete yields are approximate and depend on compaction and water content. Typical yields:

  • 80 lb bag ≈ 0.60 ft³
  • 60 lb bag ≈ 0.45 ft³
  • 50 lb bag ≈ 0.50 ft³
  • 25 kg bag ≈ 0.025 m³

The calculator divides the total volume (with waste) by the selected bag yield and rounds up to the next whole bag.

Concrete weight

Normal‑weight concrete has a density around 145–155 lb/ft³ (≈ 2400 kg/m³). The default is 150 lb/ft³, but you can adjust it if you are using lightweight or heavyweight concrete.

Weight = Volumeft³ × Density

Rebar and mesh estimate

When rebar is enabled, the calculator assumes a simple grid of bars in both directions at the same spacing. It estimates:

  • Number of bars in each direction based on slab dimensions and spacing
  • Total bar length for all sections combined
  • Approximate number of stock bars, including your rebar waste percentage

This is a planning aid only. For structural slabs, always follow an engineer’s reinforcement design and local codes.

Typical slab thickness guidelines

  • Sidewalks & patios: usually 4 in (100 mm)
  • Garage floors: 4–5 in (100–125 mm)
  • Driveways: 4–6 in (100–150 mm), depending on soil and vehicle loads
  • Heavy loads / structural slabs: engineered thickness and reinforcement required

Always check local building codes and, for critical structures, consult a licensed engineer.

When to use ready‑mix vs. bagged concrete

  • Ready‑mix truck: usually best when you need more than about 1–1.5 yd³ (roughly 40–60 bags of 80 lb concrete). Faster placement and more consistent quality.
  • Bagged concrete: convenient for small slabs, steps, and repairs, or where truck access is limited.

FAQ

How accurate is this concrete slab calculator?

The math is exact for the dimensions you enter, but real‑world conditions (subgrade, formwork, slump, crew technique) introduce variation. Use the waste percentage slider and consider rounding up to the next 0.5–1.0 yd³ for larger pours.

Do I need rebar or wire mesh in my slab?

Lightly loaded slabs on good soil may only need welded wire mesh or fiber reinforcement. Driveways, garages, and structural slabs often require rebar. Requirements depend on soil, climate (freeze–thaw), slab thickness, and local codes—always follow an engineer’s or building official’s guidance.

Can I mix units (feet and inches) in this calculator?

Yes. In imperial mode you can enter decimal feet (e.g. 12.5 ft) or convert inches to feet (e.g. 6 in = 0.5 ft). In metric mode, enter meters or convert centimeters to meters (e.g. 10 cm = 0.10 m). The calculator expects a single unit per field, so convert before entering.
