How to convert Mach to kilometers per hour

Mach is a dimensionless speed defined as the ratio between an object's speed and the local speed of sound:

Definition of Mach:

\( \text{Mach} = \dfrac{v}{a} \)

Conversion to km/h:

\( v_{\text{km/h}} = \text{Mach} \times a_{\text{m/s}} \times 3.6 \)

Here a is the local speed of sound, which depends mainly on air temperature. At standard sea‑level conditions, \( a \approx 340.3\ \text{m/s} \), which is about 1,225 km/h.

Speed of sound vs. temperature

For dry air treated as an ideal gas, the speed of sound is:

\( a = \sqrt{\gamma \, R \, T} \)

  • \( \gamma \approx 1.4 \) (ratio of specific heats for air)
  • \( R \approx 287\ \text{J/(kg·K)} \) (specific gas constant for air)
  • \( T \) is absolute temperature in kelvin (K)

That is why Mach 1 is slower at high altitude (colder air) than near sea level. The calculator lets you choose between:

  • Standard sea level (T = 15 °C, a ≈ 340.3 m/s, 1,225 km/h)
  • Typical cruise altitude (~11 km) (T ≈ −56.5 °C, a ≈ 295 m/s, 1,062 km/h)
  • Custom temperature (you specify °C, K, or °F)

Worked examples

Example 1 – Mach 0.85 to km/h at cruise altitude

Assume standard cruise altitude (~11 km), so \( a \approx 295\ \text{m/s} \).

\( v_{\text{km/h}} = 0.85 \times 295\ \text{m/s} \times 3.6 \)

\( v_{\text{km/h}} \approx 0.85 \times 1{,}062 \approx 903\ \text{km/h} \)

Example 2 – Mach 1.2 to km/h at sea level

At sea level, \( a \approx 340.3\ \text{m/s} \).

\( v_{\text{km/h}} = 1.2 \times 340.3 \times 3.6 \)

\( v_{\text{km/h}} \approx 1.2 \times 1{,}225 \approx 1{,}470\ \text{km/h} \)

Example 3 – 950 km/h to Mach at cruise altitude

Again take \( a \approx 295\ \text{m/s} \) (1,062 km/h).

\( \text{Mach} = \dfrac{v_{\text{km/h}}}{a_{\text{km/h}}} = \dfrac{950}{1{,}062} \approx 0.895 \)

FAQ

Is Mach 1 always 1,225 km/h?

No. 1,225 km/h is a commonly quoted value for Mach 1 at standard sea‑level conditions. In colder air at altitude, the speed of sound is lower, so Mach 1 in km/h is also lower.

Why do pilots care about Mach instead of just km/h?

Many aerodynamic effects, such as shock waves and compressibility, depend on Mach number rather than absolute speed. Using Mach keeps those effects comparable across different altitudes and temperatures.

Can I use this converter for gases other than air?

The built‑in models assume dry air. For other gases you would need their specific heat ratio and gas constant. You can still approximate by entering an equivalent temperature in the custom mode, but for precise work a gas‑specific model is recommended.
