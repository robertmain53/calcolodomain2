How the series and parallel resistor calculator works

This tool lets you build a simple 1D circuit made of multiple groups of resistors. Each group can be:

  • Series – resistors end-to-end, same current through all.
  • Parallel – resistors side-by-side, same voltage across each branch.

The calculator first finds the equivalent resistance of each group, then treats all groups as being in series with each other. If you enter a supply voltage, it also computes currents and voltage drops using Ohm’s law.

Formulas used

1. Resistors in series

Equivalent resistance:

\[ R_{\text{eq,series}} = R_1 + R_2 + \dots + R_n \]

Current (with supply voltage \(V\)):

\[ I = \frac{V}{R_{\text{eq,total}}} \]

Voltage drop on resistor \(i\):

\[ V_i = I \cdot R_i \]

2. Resistors in parallel

Equivalent resistance:

\[ \frac{1}{R_{\text{eq,parallel}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{R_n} \]

or for two resistors:

\[ R_{\text{eq}} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \]

Branch current (with supply voltage \(V\)):

\[ I_i = \frac{V}{R_i} \]

Total current:

\[ I_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} I_i \]

3. Total power

Power from supply:

\[ P_{\text{total}} = V \cdot I_{\text{total}} = \frac{V^2}{R_{\text{eq,total}}} \]

Power on resistor \(i\):

\[ P_i = I_i^2 R_i = \frac{V_i^2}{R_i} \]

Step-by-step example

Suppose you have this network:

  • Group 1 (series): R1 = 100 Ω, R2 = 220 Ω
  • Group 2 (parallel): R3 = 330 Ω, R4 = 470 Ω
  • Supply voltage: V = 12 V
  1. Series group: \(R_{\text{G1}} = 100 + 220 = 320\ \Omega\)
  2. Parallel group: \[ \frac{1}{R_{\text{G2}}} = \frac{1}{330} + \frac{1}{470} \Rightarrow R_{\text{G2}} \approx 193.6\ \Omega \]
  3. Total equivalent: \[ R_{\text{eq}} = R_{\text{G1}} + R_{\text{G2}} \approx 320 + 193.6 = 513.6\ \Omega \]
  4. Total current: \[ I_{\text{total}} = \frac{12}{513.6} \approx 0.0234\ \text{A} = 23.4\ \text{mA} \]
  5. Voltage on Group 1: \[ V_{\text{G1}} = I_{\text{total}} \cdot 320 \approx 7.49\ \text{V} \] Voltage on Group 2: \[ V_{\text{G2}} = 12 - 7.49 \approx 4.51\ \text{V} \]
  6. Current in each parallel branch: \[ I_3 = \frac{4.51}{330} \approx 13.7\ \text{mA},\quad I_4 = \frac{4.51}{470} \approx 9.6\ \text{mA} \]

Tips for using the calculator

  • Use kΩ or MΩ units for large resistor values to avoid tiny decimals.
  • Set supply voltage to 0 if you only care about equivalent resistance.
  • Use multiple groups to approximate more complex series-parallel networks before moving to full circuit simulators.

FAQ

In a series connection, resistors are connected end-to-end. The same current flows through each resistor, and the total resistance is the sum of all resistances.

In a parallel connection, both ends of each resistor are connected together. The voltage across each resistor is the same, and the total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistor.

Adding a parallel branch gives current an additional path, so the circuit becomes easier for current to flow through. Mathematically, you add conductances (1/R), so the equivalent resistance must be less than the smallest branch resistance.

This calculator assumes pure resistors and DC or low-frequency AC where reactive effects are negligible. For AC circuits with capacitors and inductors, you need to work with complex impedances instead of simple resistances.

The math is exact, but real resistors have tolerances (e.g., ±1%, ±5%) and temperature coefficients. For precise work, choose appropriate tolerance values and derate power to keep components within safe limits.
