Horizontal curve basics

A horizontal curve is the circular arc that connects two straight tangents in the plan view of a road, railway, or pipeline. For simple circular curves (no spirals), all geometry is defined by the radius R and the central (deflection) angle Δ.

Key relationships (R in length units, Δ in radians):

  • Curve length: \( L = R \cdot \Delta \)
  • Tangent length: \( T = R \cdot \tan\left(\dfrac{\Delta}{2}\right) \)
  • External distance: \( E = R \left(\sec\left(\dfrac{\Delta}{2}\right) - 1\right) \)
  • Middle ordinate: \( M = R \left(1 - \cos\left(\dfrac{\Delta}{2}\right)\right) \)

Note: The calculator lets you work in degrees; internally, angles are converted to radians for trigonometric functions.

Stationing: PI, PC, and PT

In highway design, the Point of Intersection (PI) is where the two tangents meet. The Point of Curvature (PC) is where the curve leaves the back tangent, and the Point of Tangency (PT) is where it joins the forward tangent.

Once the tangent length \(T\) is known, PC and PT stations are:

If stationing is in length units (e.g. ft or m):

  • \( \text{Station}_{PC} = \text{Station}_{PI} - T \)
  • \( \text{Station}_{PT} = \text{Station}_{PC} + L \)

Deflection angles for staking

To set out the curve in the field using equal chords of length \(c\), the central angle for each chord is:

\( \Delta_c = 2 \arcsin\left(\dfrac{c}{2R}\right) \)

The deflection angle from the tangent to that chord is: \( \delta = \dfrac{\Delta_c}{2} \)

For multiple chords, the cumulative deflection to each point is the sum of the individual deflections. The staking table generated by this calculator reports the deflection angle from PC to each point along the curve.

How to use this horizontal curve calculator

  1. Select units and angle format. Choose feet or meters, and whether you want to enter angles in decimal degrees or DMS.
  2. Enter at least two main parameters. Common design workflows:
    • Given design speed and maximum superelevation, you choose a radius R and a central angle Δ.
    • Given R and available tangent length, you solve for Δ and L.
    • Given R and desired curve length L, you solve for Δ and T.
  3. Optionally enter PI station and chord length. This allows the tool to compute PC/PT stations and a full staking table with deflection angles.
  4. Click “Calculate”. The calculator validates your inputs, solves the curve geometry, and displays all derived values.
  5. Review the staking table. Use the station and deflection angle for each point to set out the curve in the field.

Typical design checks

Agency manuals (e.g., AASHTO, state DOTs) specify minimum radii for a given design speed, side friction factor, and superelevation. While this calculator does not enforce those criteria, you should always verify that:

  • The chosen radius meets or exceeds the minimum for the design speed and superelevation.
  • Stopping sight distance is adequate on the inside of the curve (check middle ordinate vs. sight line).
  • Transition (spiral) curves are provided where required by your design standard.

Limitations

  • Simple circular curves only (no spirals, compound, or reverse curves).
  • Assumes small enough angles that standard trigonometric relationships apply without numerical issues.
  • Does not perform safety or comfort checks (e.g., side friction, superelevation limits).
