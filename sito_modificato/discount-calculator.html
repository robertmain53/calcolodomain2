How to use the discount calculator

This discount calculator is designed to handle everyday shopping, online deals, and business pricing scenarios. It works in four modes:

  • Basic discount – enter original price and discount % to get sale price, savings, and tax-inclusive total.
  • Find original price – you know the sale price and discount % and want to recover the original price.
  • Find % off – you know original and sale price and want to know the discount percentage.
  • Multi-discount – apply up to three stacked discounts and see the effective total discount.

Discount formulas

1. Sale price from original price and discount %

Let:

  • \( P_0 \) = original price
  • \( r \) = discount rate (in decimal, e.g. 25% = 0.25)

Then:

\[ \text{Discount amount} = P_0 \times r \]

\[ \text{Sale price} = P_0 - P_0 \times r = P_0 \times (1 - r) \]

2. Original price from sale price and discount %

Let \( P_s \) be the sale price. Then:

\[ P_s = P_0 \times (1 - r) \Rightarrow P_0 = \frac{P_s}{1 - r} \]

3. Discount percentage from original and sale price

\[ r = \frac{P_0 - P_s}{P_0} \times 100\% \]

4. Stacked (multi-level) discounts

For discounts \( r_1, r_2, r_3 \) applied one after another:

\[ P_s = P_0 \times (1 - r_1) \times (1 - r_2) \times (1 - r_3) \]

The effective discount rate is:

\[ r_{\text{eff}} = 1 - (1 - r_1)(1 - r_2)(1 - r_3) \]

Worked examples

Example 1 – Simple percent off

A jacket costs $80 and is on sale for 25% off. What is the sale price?

\[ \text{Discount} = 80 \times 0.25 = 20 \]

\[ \text{Sale price} = 80 - 20 = \$60 \]

Example 2 – Stacked discounts

A $200 item has 20% off plus an extra 10% off coupon.

  • After 20% off: \( 200 \times (1 - 0.20) = 160 \)
  • After extra 10% off: \( 160 \times (1 - 0.10) = 144 \)

Total discount amount: \( 200 - 144 = 56 \)

Effective discount rate: \( 56 / 200 = 28\% \).

Common questions

Does a “20% off + extra 10% off” sale mean 30% off?

No. Discounts are almost always applied sequentially, not added. In this case the effective discount is 28%, not 30%. Use the multi-discount tab to see the exact result.

What if the discount is more than 100%?

A discount above 100% would imply a negative price, which is not realistic. The calculator warns you if the inputs would lead to impossible results (for example, sale price higher than original price when computing % off).

FAQ

How do I calculate a discount manually?

Multiply the original price by the discount rate (as a decimal) to get the discount amount, then subtract it from the original price. Example: 15% off $40 → 0.15 × 40 = $6 discount, $40 − $6 = $34 sale price.

Can this calculator handle tax-inclusive prices?

Yes. Enter your tax rate and choose whether tax is applied before or after discount. The calculator then shows both the pre-tax sale price and the final amount including tax.

What’s the difference between discount and markdown?

In retail, a discount is a temporary reduction (like a coupon or promotion), while a markdown is a permanent price reduction. Mathematically they are the same: both reduce the original price by a percentage or fixed amount.

Can I use this for business pricing?

Yes. Many small businesses use this type of calculator to test sale scenarios, coupon strategies, and to check that discounts still leave enough margin after tax.

Quick tips

  • Stacked discounts multiply, they don’t add.
  • Most regions apply tax after discount, not before.
  • Use “Find % off” to check if a sale is really a good deal.
  • Round to 2 decimals to match receipts and invoices.
