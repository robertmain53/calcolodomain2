Step-by-step (derivation & checks)
Law of Cosines: \[ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C,\quad b^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac\cos B,\quad a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc\cos A. \] Law of Sines: \[ \frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} = 2R. \] Area (Heron): \[ s=\frac{a+b+c}{2},\quad \Delta = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}. \] Inradius and circumradius: \[ r=\frac{\Delta}{s},\quad R=\frac{abc}{4\Delta}. \]

Authoritative data source & methodology

AuthoritativeDataSource: Euclid’s Elements (Book I, Props. 12–13, 22–32) and standard trigonometry identities (Law of Cosines, Law of Sines). See: Wolfram MathWorld — Triangle, Law of Cosines, Law of Sines.
All calculations strictly follow the formulas and definitions provided by this source.

The formulas explained

Given a triangle with sides \(a,b,c\) opposite angles \(A,B,C\) (in degrees) and perimeter \(p=a+b+c\):
Law of Cosines and Sines (above); Area \(\Delta\) via Heron; inradius \(r=\Delta/s\); circumradius \(R=abc/(4\Delta)\).

Glossary of variables

  • a, b, c: sides opposite angles A, B, C (length \(> 0\)).
  • A, B, C: internal angles in degrees with \(A+B+C=180^\circ\).
  • p: perimeter \(a+b+c\).
  • s: semiperimeter \((a+b+c)/2\).
  • \(\Delta\): area; r: inradius; R: circumradius.

How it works: a step-by-step example

Example (SSS): a=3, b=4, c=5

  1. Semiperimeter \(s=(3+4+5)/2=6\); area \(\Delta=\sqrt{6(6-3)(6-4)(6-5)}=6\).
  2. Angles via Law of Cosines: \(C=\cos^{-1}\!\big((a^2+b^2-c^2)/(2ab)\big)=90^\circ\), \(A\approx36.87^\circ\), \(B\approx53.13^\circ\).
  3. Inradius \(r=\Delta/s=1\); circumradius \(R=abc/(4\Delta)=2.5\).

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Which mode should I choose?

Pick SSS if you know all three sides, SAS for two sides and the included angle, ASA/AAS for two angles and any side.

What if my angles don’t sum to 180°?

The calculator will warn you. Adjust one angle until the sum is 180° (within a tiny tolerance).

Do ambiguous cases occur?

Yes, in SSA (not supported) the “ambiguous case” can yield 0, 1, or 2 solutions. Use SAS or ASA/AAS instead.

How precise are outputs?

Computed in double precision; displayed with sensible rounding. Minor round-off differences can occur.

Can I compute heights?

Yes. Heights are derived as \(h_a=2\Delta/a\), \(h_b=2\Delta/b\), \(h_c=2\Delta/c\) and exposed via area/radius relations.
