How this mortgage payment calculator works

This mortgage payment calculator uses the standard fixed-rate loan formula to estimate your monthly payment and full amortization schedule. It goes beyond basic tools by including:

  • Property taxes and homeowners insurance (PITI)
  • Private mortgage insurance (PMI) when your down payment is under 20%
  • HOA dues
  • Extra monthly, annual and one-time payments
  • Payoff date, interest savings and time saved with extra payments

The mortgage payment formula

The base principal-and-interest payment for a fixed-rate mortgage is:

Monthly payment (principal & interest):

Let:

  • P = loan amount (home price − down payment)
  • r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100)
  • n = total number of payments (years × 12)

Then the monthly payment is:

\\( M = P \cdot \dfrac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1} \\)

The calculator computes this base payment, then adds your estimated property taxes, insurance, PMI and HOA dues to show your total monthly housing cost.

For a plain‑language overview of mortgage terms, escrow, and monthly payment components, see the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) mortgage education materials: CFPB: Mortgages.

What is included in your monthly mortgage payment?

Most homeowners pay more than just principal and interest each month. Lenders often collect these items in an escrow account:

  • Principal: The portion of your payment that reduces your loan balance.
  • Interest: The cost of borrowing money from the lender.
  • Property taxes: Annual property taxes divided by 12.
  • Homeowners insurance: Annual premium divided by 12.
  • PMI: Required on many conventional loans when your down payment is under 20%.
  • HOA dues: Monthly fees for condos or communities with a homeowners association.

How PMI is estimated

PMI is typically charged as a percentage of your loan amount per year (for example 0.5%–1.5%). This calculator:

  • Checks whether your down payment is below 20% of the home price.
  • If so, applies your chosen PMI rate to the current loan balance.
  • Shows the monthly PMI cost and includes it in your total payment.

In reality, PMI may drop off once your loan-to-value (LTV) reaches 80% and you meet lender requirements. For background on PMI and LTV thresholds, see the CFPB explanation: CFPB: What is PMI?. For simplicity, this tool assumes PMI continues for the full term, which slightly overestimates long‑term costs and keeps the math transparent.

Extra payments and interest savings

Extra payments applied to principal reduce your balance faster, which:

  • Shortens your payoff time.
  • Cuts total interest paid over the life of the loan.

The calculator lets you add:

  • Extra monthly payments (for example rounding up your payment).
  • Extra annual payments (for example a yearly bonus).
  • One-time lump sum in the first month.

It then compares the original schedule (no extras) to the new schedule and shows:

  • New payoff date.
  • Time saved (months and years).
  • Interest saved.

How to use the mortgage payment calculator

  1. Enter the home price. Use the purchase price or an estimated value.
  2. Set your down payment. You can type either a dollar amount or a percentage; the other field updates automatically.
  3. Choose the loan term. Drag the slider or click a preset (15, 20, 30 years).
  4. Adjust the interest rate. Use your quoted rate or a current market estimate.
  5. Estimate property taxes and insurance. If you are unsure, start with 1%–1.5% for taxes and $800–$2,000 per year for insurance depending on your area.
  6. Decide whether to include PMI. Leave it on if your down payment is under 20%.
  7. Add HOA dues and extra payments if they apply to you.
  8. Review the summary box to see your total monthly payment and payoff details.
  9. Scroll the amortization table to see how each payment is split between principal and interest.

Frequently asked questions

Is my mortgage payment fixed for the entire term?

With a fixed-rate mortgage, your principal and interest payment stays the same for the life of the loan. However, your total monthly payment can still change if property taxes, insurance premiums or HOA dues go up or down. Adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs) can also change when the interest rate resets.

How much house can I afford?

Lenders often look for a front-end DTI (housing costs ÷ gross monthly income) under about 28% and a back-end DTI (all debts ÷ income) under about 36%–43%, depending on the loan program. Use this calculator together with a debt-to-income ratio tool to see how different home prices and down payments affect affordability.

What is the difference between principal and interest?

Principal is the amount you borrowed. Interest is the fee the lender charges for using their money. Early in your mortgage, most of your payment goes toward interest; over time, more goes toward principal. The amortization schedule in this calculator makes that shift easy to see.

Should I pay extra on my mortgage?

Paying extra can be a good idea if:

  • You have an adequate emergency fund.
  • You have paid off higher-interest debt (like credit cards).
  • You plan to stay in the home long enough to benefit from the interest savings.

Use the extra payment fields to see exactly how much time and interest you could save.

Does this calculator include closing costs?

No. This tool focuses on your ongoing monthly payment and amortization schedule. Closing costs are typically 2%–5% of the purchase price and are paid upfront at closing. Some buyers choose to roll certain costs into the loan, which would increase the loan amount and monthly payment.

The CFPB explains common closing cost categories and how they appear on the Loan Estimate: CFPB closing costs overview.
