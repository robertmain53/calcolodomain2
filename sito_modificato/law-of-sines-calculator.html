The Formula Explained

The Law of Sines states that the ratio of the length of a side of a triangle to the sine of its opposite angle is the same for all three sides. It is the fundamental relationship used to solve oblique (non-right) triangles.

$$ \frac{a}{\sin(A)} = \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{c}{\sin(C)} $$

It can also be written inverted, which is often more convenient when solving for an unknown angle:

$$ \frac{\sin(A)}{a} = \frac{\sin(B)}{b} = \frac{\sin(C)}{c} $$

Glossary of Variables

To use the calculator, you must understand the triangle's labeling convention:

  • Side a: The length of the side opposite Angle A.
  • Side b: The length of the side opposite Angle B.
  • Side c: The length of the side opposite Angle C.
  • Angle A: The angle at vertex A, opposite Side a.
  • Angle B: The angle at vertex B, opposite Side b.
  • Angle C: The angle at vertex C, opposite Side c.

The sum of all angles (A + B + C) must equal 180° (or $\pi$ radians).

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Let's solve a triangle where we know Angle-Angle-Side (AAS).

Given:

  • Angle A = 45°
  • Angle B = 60°
  • Side a = 10

Step 1: Find the third angle (Angle C)

The sum of a triangle's angles is always 180°. Therefore, we can find Angle C:

$$ C = 180^\circ - A - B $$ $$ C = 180^\circ - 45^\circ - 60^\circ = 75^\circ $$

Step 2: Find the remaining sides (Side b and Side c)

Using the Law of Sines, we set up ratios using the known side/angle pair (a / sin A):

To find Side b:

$$ \frac{b}{\sin(B)} = \frac{a}{\sin(A)} $$ $$ b = \frac{a \cdot \sin(B)}{\sin(A)} = \frac{10 \cdot \sin(60^\circ)}{\sin(45^\circ)} $$ $$ b \approx \frac{10 \cdot 0.866}{0.707} \approx 12.25 $$

To find Side c:

$$ \frac{c}{\sin(C)} = \frac{a}{\sin(A)} $$ $$ c = \frac{a \cdot \sin(C)}{\sin(A)} = \frac{10 \cdot \sin(75^\circ)}{\sin(45^\circ)} $$ $$ c \approx \frac{10 \cdot 0.966}{0.707} \approx 13.66 $$

Result:

The fully solved triangle is:

  • Angle A = 45°, Angle B = 60°, Angle C = 75°
  • Side a = 10, Side b ≈ 12.25, Side c ≈ 13.66

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the Law of Sines used for?

The Law of Sines is used to find unknown angles or sides of an oblique triangle (any triangle that is not a right triangle). It requires knowing at least one side and its opposite angle, plus one other measurement.

When should I use the Law of Sines vs. the Law of Cosines?

Use the Law of Sines when you know:

  • AAS (Angle-Angle-Side)
  • ASA (Angle-Side-Angle)
  • SSA (Side-Side-Angle - the ambiguous case)

Use the Law of Cosines when you know:

  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side)
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side)
What is the ambiguous case (SSA)?

The ambiguous case occurs when you are given two sides and a non-included angle (Side-Side-Angle). Depending on the lengths of the sides and the size of the angle, this configuration can result in zero, one, or two possible valid triangles. This calculator will automatically detect this and show you all possible solutions.

Can I use the Law of Sines for a right triangle?

Yes, you can, but it's unnecessary. For a right triangle (one angle = 90°), the sine of 90° is 1. The Law of Sines simplifies to the basic SOHCAHTOA definitions (e.g., $\sin(A) = \frac{a}{c}$). It's much simpler to use SOHCAHTOA or the Pythagorean theorem for right triangles.

What is Heron's Formula?

Heron's Formula is used to find the area of a triangle when you know the lengths of all three sides (SSS). This calculator finds the area using the formula $Area = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin(C)$ once all sides and angles are known.

Why do my results say 'Impossible Triangle'?

This error appears if the values you entered cannot form a real triangle. Common reasons include:

  • The sum of two known angles is 180° or more.
  • A side value is zero or negative.
  • In the SSA case, the given side is too short to reach the base, resulting in no solution.
