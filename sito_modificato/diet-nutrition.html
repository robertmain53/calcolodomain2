How this diet & nutrition calculator works

This tool combines widely used scientific formulas with the core principles from major public health organizations (WHO, CDC, NHS, Harvard, NIDDK and others). It is not a medical device, but a practical starting point for building a healthier eating pattern.

1. Estimating your energy needs (calories)

We estimate your basal metabolic rate (BMR) using the Mifflin–St Jeor equation, which is commonly used in clinical nutrition, then multiply by an activity factor to estimate total daily energy expenditure (TDEE).

Mifflin–St Jeor (BMR)

For men:

\(\text{BMR} = 10 \times \text{weight (kg)} + 6.25 \times \text{height (cm)} - 5 \times \text{age (years)} + 5\)

For women:

\(\text{BMR} = 10 \times \text{weight (kg)} + 6.25 \times \text{height (cm)} - 5 \times \text{age (years)} - 161\)

Total daily energy expenditure (TDEE)

\(\text{TDEE} = \text{BMR} \times \text{activity factor}\)

Typical activity factors:

  • 1.2 – Sedentary (desk job, little exercise)
  • 1.375 – Lightly active (1–3 exercise sessions/week)
  • 1.55 – Moderately active (3–5 sessions/week)
  • 1.725 – Very active (hard exercise 6–7 days/week)
  • 1.9 – Athlete / very hard exercise or physical job

Your goal (weight loss, maintenance, or gain) then adjusts calories up or down by roughly 250–500 kcal/day, which corresponds to about 0.25–0.5 kg (0.5–1 lb) per week for many adults.

2. Macronutrient ranges (carbs, protein, fat)

Major guidelines such as the Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDR) suggest that healthy adults can thrive within these broad ranges:

  • Carbohydrates: 45–65% of total calories
  • Protein: 10–35% of total calories
  • Fat: 20–35% of total calories

The calculator lets you choose between three macro patterns, all within these ranges:

  • Balanced: ~50% carbs, 20% protein, 30% fat
  • Higher protein: ~40% carbs, 30% protein, 30% fat
  • Lower carb: ~30% carbs, 30% protein, 40% fat

We convert macro calories into grams using:

Carbs (g) = (Carb % × Calories) ÷ 4

Protein (g) = (Protein % × Calories) ÷ 4

Fat (g) = (Fat % × Calories) ÷ 9

For protein, we also show an approximate g/kg body weight value. Many experts recommend around 1.2–2.0 g/kg for active people, older adults, or those trying to lose fat while maintaining muscle.

3. Food group targets: turning numbers into meals

Calories and macros are only part of the story. Public health guidance emphasizes dietary patterns: what your plate looks like over the day or week.

The suggested servings in your results are loosely based on patterns like the Harvard Healthy Eating Plate, WHO healthy diet recommendations, and national guidelines. They scale with your calorie needs:

  • Vegetables: usually 3–5+ servings/day
  • Fruits: usually 2–4 servings/day
  • Whole grains / starchy foods: 3–8 servings/day depending on energy needs
  • Protein foods: 2–3+ servings/day (beans, lentils, tofu, fish, eggs, lean meat, nuts)
  • Dairy or fortified alternatives: around 2–3 servings/day for many adults
  • Added fats: small amounts of healthy oils, nuts, and seeds

One “serving” is approximate and varies by guideline. For example, 1 serving of vegetables might be 1 cup raw leafy greens or ½ cup cooked vegetables.

Principles of a healthy diet

Despite differences between countries and specific diet brands, major health organizations agree on a few core principles:

  1. Emphasize whole, minimally processed foods. Vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds, and minimally processed animal products if you eat them.
  2. Limit added sugars and sugary drinks. Especially sodas, energy drinks, and sweets.
  3. Choose healthy fats. Prefer unsaturated fats from fish, nuts, seeds, and plant oils; limit trans fats and large amounts of saturated fat.
  4. Watch salt (sodium) intake. Many people exceed recommended limits, mostly from processed foods and restaurant meals.
  5. Balance energy in and out. Over time, weight tends to reflect the balance between calories eaten and calories burned.
  6. Consider your whole lifestyle. Sleep, stress, physical activity, and smoking or alcohol use all interact with diet.

Examples of healthy dietary patterns

Well-studied patterns that align with these principles include:

  • Mediterranean-style diets rich in vegetables, fruits, olive oil, nuts, legumes, whole grains, and fish.
  • Plant-forward or vegetarian diets that prioritize plant proteins and whole plant foods.
  • DASH-style diets (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) emphasizing fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy with limited sodium.

You do not need to follow any pattern perfectly. The goal is to move your usual eating closer to these principles in a way that fits your culture, budget, and preferences.

Limitations and when to seek professional advice

This calculator is for general education and is not a substitute for personalized medical or dietetic advice. It does not account for:

  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Chronic diseases (e.g., diabetes, kidney disease, heart disease)
  • Food allergies or intolerances
  • Eating disorders or a history of disordered eating
  • Specific medical nutrition therapy prescribed by your clinician

If you have a medical condition, take regular medications, or plan major diet changes, consult a registered dietitian or healthcare professional.

Diet & nutrition FAQ

How many calories should I eat per day?

There is no single number that fits everyone. Most adult women maintain weight somewhere between about 1,600–2,400 kcal/day, and most adult men between about 2,000–3,000 kcal/day, depending on body size and activity. Use the calculator to get a personalized estimate, then track your weight and how you feel for a few weeks and adjust by 100–200 kcal/day if needed.

Do I need to count calories or macros?

Not necessarily. Some people find tracking helpful for a short period to learn portion sizes and patterns; others find it stressful. You can use your results as a reference and focus more on building balanced plates (½ vegetables and fruits, ¼ protein, ¼ whole grains, plus healthy fats) rather than tracking every gram long term.

Is sugar always bad?

Naturally occurring sugars in whole fruits and plain dairy come packaged with fiber, vitamins, and minerals and are generally fine for most people. Added sugars (in sweets, sugary drinks, many packaged foods) should be limited. Many guidelines suggest keeping added sugars below about 10% of total calories, and ideally lower.

How quickly should I lose or gain weight?

Gradual changes are usually safer and more sustainable. For most adults, losing about 0.25–0.5 kg (0.5–1 lb) per week or gaining at a similar pace is considered reasonable. Faster changes may increase the risk of muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, or rebound weight gain, especially without professional guidance.

What if I have diabetes, kidney disease, or other conditions?

In that case, you may need specific targets for carbohydrates, protein, sodium, potassium, or other nutrients. Use this tool only as a rough background reference and work with your healthcare team or a registered dietitian for an individualized plan.
