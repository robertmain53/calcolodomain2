Inclusive vs exclusive day counting

There are two common ways to count days between dates:

Exclusive count (default in many spreadsheets):

\(\text{days} = \text{End date} - \text{Start date}\)

Example: from 1 Jan to 2 Jan = 1 day.

Inclusive count (often used for events and age ranges):

\(\text{days} = \text{End date} - \text{Start date} + 1\)

Example: from 1 Jan to 2 Jan = 2 days (1st and 2nd).

Use the “Include end date in count” checkbox to switch between these two modes. The badge above the main result reminds you which mode is active.

Counting weekdays only (business days)

If you need to know how many working days fall between two dates (for project timelines, SLAs, or payroll), set the Count mode to:

  • Weekdays only (Mon–Fri) – counts Monday through Friday, ignoring Saturdays and Sundays.
  • Custom weekdays… – lets you choose exactly which days of the week to include (for example, Mon–Sat for retail, or Fri–Tue for shift work).

The calculator loops through each day in the range and counts only those whose weekday matches your selection. This ensures leap years and month lengths are handled correctly.

How the calculation works (technical details)

Total days

Internally, each date is converted to a UTC timestamp (milliseconds since 1 Jan 1970). The difference in days is:

\[ \text{days} = \frac{\text{EndUTC} - \text{StartUTC}}{1000 \times 60 \times 60 \times 24} \]

We then adjust by +1 if you choose to include the end date.

Years, months, and days breakdown

To get a human-friendly breakdown like “2 years, 3 months, 5 days”, we use calendar arithmetic:

  1. Start from the earlier date.
  2. Increase the year until adding another year would go past the end date.
  3. Increase the month until adding another month would go past the end date.
  4. The remaining difference is the number of days.

Approximate months and years

For the approximate values, we simply divide the total day count:

\[ \text{years} \approx \frac{\text{days}}{365.25}, \quad \text{months} \approx \frac{\text{days}}{30.4375} \]

where 365.25 and 30.4375 are average values that account for leap years.

Typical use cases

  • Project planning: how many calendar days or working days are between a start and end date.
  • Age or tenure: exact time between a birth/hire date and today.
  • Deadlines: days remaining until an exam, event, or due date.
  • Billing and interest: number of days in a billing cycle or interest period.
  • Compliance and SLAs: verify whether a response or resolution met a time-based requirement.

FAQ

What if the start date is after the end date?

The calculator automatically swaps the dates so the earlier one is treated as the start. You’ll always get a non‑negative number of days.

Does this handle leap years and different month lengths?

Yes. All calculations use the built‑in calendar rules of your browser, so February 29 and varying month lengths are handled correctly.

Why do I see different results than in Excel?

Excel’s =DATEDIF and =end-start formulas are usually exclusive of the end date. If you want the same behavior here, uncheck “Include end date in count”. If you want inclusive counting, keep it checked.

Can I ignore holidays?

This version lets you skip weekends or custom weekdays. For holiday‑aware calculations, you can export the result and adjust manually, or combine this with a holiday list in a spreadsheet.

Which time zone is used?

The calculator uses your browser’s local time zone. If you’re comparing dates from different time zones, convert them to the same zone first for consistent results.
