Key takeaways

How this traditional IRA calculator works

This traditional IRA calculator projects how your retirement savings could grow under different assumptions about contributions, investment returns, fees, and tax rates. It also compares a traditional IRA to investing in a taxable account or a Roth-style account using the same after-tax dollars.

1. Growth of your traditional IRA

We model your IRA balance using standard compound interest formulas. First we adjust your expected return for fees:

Net annual return = Expected return − Annual fees

Example: 7% return − 0.5% fees = 6.5% net

Then we compound your current balance and annual contributions over the number of years until retirement:

Years of growth = Retirement age − Current age

Future value of current balance = \( B_0 \times (1 + r)^{n} \)

Future value of contributions = \( C \times \frac{(1 + r)^{n} - 1}{r} \)

where:

  • \( B_0 \) = current IRA balance
  • \( C \) = annual contribution
  • \( r \) = net annual return (decimal)
  • \( n \) = years of contributions

2. Tax savings from deductible contributions

Traditional IRA contributions may be fully, partially, or not deductible depending on your income and whether you (or your spouse) are covered by a workplace plan. Because eligibility rules are complex and change over time, this calculator lets you specify what percentage of your contribution is deductible.

Annual deductible contribution = Contribution × Deductible %

Annual tax savings = Annual deductible contribution × Current tax rate

Total tax savings ≈ Annual tax savings × Years of contributions

3. After-tax value of your traditional IRA

Withdrawals from a traditional IRA are generally taxed as ordinary income. To estimate the after-tax value of your IRA at retirement, we apply your estimated retirement tax rate to the projected balance:

After-tax IRA value = IRA balance at retirement × (1 − Retirement tax rate)

This is a simplification: in reality, you will likely withdraw over many years, and your tax rate could change. Use this as a planning estimate rather than a precise forecast.

4. Comparing traditional IRA vs taxable vs Roth-style investing

To make an apples-to-apples comparison, we assume you are willing to give up the same after-tax amount of cash each year, regardless of account type.

  • Traditional IRA: You contribute pre-tax dollars (to the extent they are deductible).
  • Taxable account: You invest after-tax dollars and pay tax on gains each year.
  • Roth-style account: You invest after-tax dollars, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free.

For the taxable account, we approximate the drag from annual taxes by reducing the effective growth rate. For the Roth-style account, we use the same net return as the IRA but treat the final balance as tax-free.

When a traditional IRA may be a good fit

  • You are eligible for a full or large tax deduction on your contributions.
  • You expect your tax rate in retirement to be lower than it is today.
  • You want to reduce your current-year taxable income.

When a Roth IRA or taxable investing might be better

  • You expect your tax rate in retirement to be the same or higher than today.
  • You value tax-free withdrawals and not having required minimum distributions (RMDs) during your lifetime.
  • You are not eligible to deduct traditional IRA contributions.

Frequently asked questions

What is a traditional IRA?

A traditional IRA (Individual Retirement Account) is a personal retirement account that offers tax-deferred growth. Contributions may be tax-deductible, and investment earnings are not taxed until you withdraw the money, usually in retirement. Withdrawals are generally taxed as ordinary income.

What are the contribution limits?

The IRS sets annual contribution limits that can change over time and may be higher for people age 50 or older (catch-up contributions). Check current IRS guidance or your tax authority for the latest limits and rules.

When do required minimum distributions (RMDs) start?

Traditional IRAs are subject to required minimum distributions starting at a specific age (which has changed in recent legislation). RMD rules are complex and depend on your age, account balance, and beneficiary structure. This calculator does not model RMDs; it focuses on your balance at retirement and the after-tax value.

Can I contribute to both a traditional and a Roth IRA?

Yes, you can contribute to both in the same year, but your combined contributions cannot exceed the annual IRA limit. Income limits may restrict your ability to contribute to a Roth IRA or deduct traditional IRA contributions.
