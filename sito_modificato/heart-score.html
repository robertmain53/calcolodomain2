What is the HEART score?

The HEART score is a validated clinical tool used to risk‑stratify adult patients presenting to the emergency department with chest pain suggestive of acute coronary syndrome (ACS). It predicts the short‑term risk of major adverse cardiac events (MACE), typically within 30 days, including myocardial infarction, need for revascularization, and death.

HEART is an acronym for:

  • History
  • ECG
  • Age
  • Risk factors
  • Troponin

HEART score components and scoring

Total HEART score

\[ \text{HEART} = H + E + A + R + T \]

  • H (History): 0–2 points
  • E (ECG): 0–2 points
  • A (Age): 0–2 points
  • R (Risk factors): 0–2 points
  • T (Troponin): 0–2 points

Minimum score: 0, maximum score: 10.

History (H)

  • 0 points: Slightly suspicious – atypical features, alternative diagnosis more likely.
  • 1 point: Moderately suspicious – mixed typical and atypical features.
  • 2 points: Highly suspicious – classic ischemic chest pain (e.g., pressure, exertional, radiating, associated diaphoresis or dyspnea).

ECG (E)

  • 0 points: Normal ECG.
  • 1 point: Nonspecific repolarization abnormalities, bundle branch block, or unchanged chronic changes.
  • 2 points: Significant ST‑segment depression compatible with ischemia.

Age (A)

  • 0 points: < 45 years
  • 1 point: 45–65 years
  • 2 points: > 65 years

Risk factors (R)

Common cardiovascular risk factors include:

  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Current or recent smoking
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Obesity
  • Positive family history of premature coronary artery disease
  • Known atherosclerotic disease (prior MI, PCI/CABG, stroke, or peripheral arterial disease)
  • 0 points: No risk factors or only 1 risk factor.
  • 1 point: 2–3 risk factors or known atherosclerotic disease.
  • 2 points: ≥ 3 risk factors or multiple significant comorbidities.

Troponin (T)

Troponin is scored relative to the upper reference limit (URL) of your local assay:

  • 0 points: Troponin ≤ URL (within normal range).
  • 1 point: Troponin > URL but ≤ 3× URL.
  • 2 points: Troponin > 3× URL.

The calculator allows you to enter both the measured troponin and the URL so that the relative elevation is calculated automatically.

Clinical use and limitations

The HEART score has been validated in multiple cohorts and is widely used to support decision‑making in chest pain units and emergency departments. It is particularly helpful for identifying low‑risk patients who may be safely discharged after appropriate evaluation.

However, it has important limitations:

  • It is intended for adult patients with chest pain where ACS is a diagnostic consideration.
  • It should not be used in patients with clear STEMI, hemodynamic instability, or obvious alternative diagnoses.
  • It does not replace serial ECGs, serial troponins, or comprehensive clinical assessment.
  • Local protocols, available troponin assays (including high‑sensitivity assays), and population characteristics may affect performance.

Always apply the HEART score within the context of your institution’s chest pain pathway and consult cardiology or senior clinicians when in doubt.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use high‑sensitivity troponin with the HEART score?

Yes. The HEART score can be used with either conventional or high‑sensitivity troponin assays, as long as you use the correct upper reference limit (URL) for your lab. Some institutions have adapted HEART into pathways that incorporate serial high‑sensitivity troponin measurements and accelerated diagnostic protocols.

How often should troponin be repeated?

Many protocols repeat troponin at 1–3 hours after the initial sample, especially in low‑to‑intermediate risk patients. The exact timing and number of measurements depend on your local guidelines and the specific troponin assay used.

Is a low HEART score enough to discharge a patient?

A HEART score of 0–3 is associated with a low risk of 30‑day MACE, but discharge decisions must also consider:

  • Serial ECG and troponin results
  • Symptom evolution and response to treatment
  • Comorbidities and social factors
  • Availability of timely outpatient follow‑up

Use the HEART score as one piece of the overall clinical picture, not as a stand‑alone rule.
