What is the cash ratio?

The cash ratio is a conservative liquidity ratio that shows how much of a company’s current liabilities can be paid off immediately using only cash and cash equivalents. It ignores receivables and inventory, focusing purely on “money in the bank”.

Cash Ratio Formula

\[ \text{Cash Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cash} + \text{Cash Equivalents}}{\text{Current Liabilities}} \]

Where:

  • Cash – physical cash and bank balances.
  • Cash equivalents – highly liquid, short-term investments (e.g., T-bills, money market funds).
  • Current liabilities – obligations due within 12 months.

Example calculation

Suppose a company has:

  • Cash and bank balances: $250,000
  • Marketable securities (cash equivalents): $50,000
  • Total current liabilities: $400,000

The cash ratio is:

\[ \text{Cash Ratio} = \frac{250{,}000 + 50{,}000}{400{,}000} = \frac{300{,}000}{400{,}000} = 0.75 \]

A cash ratio of 0.75 means the company could cover 75% of its short-term obligations instantly using only cash and cash equivalents.

How to interpret the cash ratio

There is no universal “perfect” cash ratio. Interpretation depends on the company’s industry, business model, and risk tolerance, but the ranges below are a useful starting point:

Cash Ratio Interpretation Comments
< 0.2 Very tight liquidity Company relies heavily on collections and refinancing; vulnerable in a cash crunch.
0.2 – 0.5 Below conservative Common in some capital-intensive industries; monitor working capital closely.
0.5 – 1.0 Comfortable for many firms Indicates solid liquidity without excessive idle cash.
> 1.0 Very high liquidity Short-term obligations fully covered by cash; may signal under-investment if persistent.

Cash ratio vs. current ratio vs. quick ratio

The cash ratio is the strictest of the three main liquidity ratios:

  • Current ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities (includes inventory and prepaids).
  • Quick ratio ≈ (Cash + Marketable Securities + Receivables) ÷ Current Liabilities.
  • Cash ratio = (Cash + Cash Equivalents) ÷ Current Liabilities.

Because it excludes receivables and inventory, the cash ratio focuses on the company’s ability to pay immediately, without selling inventory or waiting for customers to pay.

Limitations of the cash ratio

  • It ignores the quality and collectability of receivables.
  • It ignores the liquidity of inventory, which may be very high in some industries (e.g., supermarkets).
  • It is a point-in-time measure and can be distorted by seasonality or one-off events.
  • A very high cash ratio is not always positive; excess cash may reduce returns if not deployed effectively.

How to improve your cash ratio

  • Build and maintain a realistic cash reserve policy.
  • Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers where possible.
  • Accelerate collections (early payment discounts, better invoicing processes).
  • Refinance short-term debt into longer maturities when appropriate.
  • Reduce non-essential cash outflows and discretionary spending.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good cash ratio?

Many analysts consider a range of about 0.2 to 1.0 reasonable, depending on the industry. Very stable, predictable businesses can operate safely with lower ratios, while cyclical or high-risk businesses may target higher liquidity. Always compare your cash ratio to industry peers and your own historical trend.

Is a higher cash ratio always better?

Not necessarily. A higher cash ratio means more safety in the short term, but excess cash can drag down returns if it is not invested in growth, used to reduce debt, or returned to shareholders. The “right” level balances liquidity with profitability.

Should I include marketable securities in cash equivalents?

Most definitions of cash equivalents include highly liquid, short-term investments that can be converted to cash quickly with minimal risk of value change (e.g., T-bills, money market funds). Longer-term or volatile investments should not be included. Our calculator lets you enter them separately so you can see the impact with or without them.

Can I use the cash ratio for personal finance?

Yes. You can treat your cash and savings as the numerator and your short-term obligations (credit card balances, bills due soon, short-term loans) as the denominator. This gives a quick view of how easily you could cover near-term obligations with cash on hand.
