Percent yield formula

In chemistry, percent yield compares how much product you actually obtained to how much you could have obtained in an ideal, perfectly efficient reaction.

Percent yield formula

\[ \text{Percent yield} = \frac{\text{Actual yield}}{\text{Theoretical yield}} \times 100\% \]

  • Actual yield – the amount of product you actually isolate from the experiment.
  • Theoretical yield – the maximum possible amount of product predicted by stoichiometry, assuming:
    • reaction goes to completion, and
    • no side reactions or losses.

Rearranged formulas

You can also solve for actual or theoretical yield:

Solve for actual yield

\[ \text{Actual yield} = \frac{\text{Percent yield}}{100\%} \times \text{Theoretical yield} \]

Solve for theoretical yield

\[ \text{Theoretical yield} = \frac{\text{Actual yield} \times 100\%}{\text{Percent yield}} \]

Worked example: percent yield from grams

Suppose the theoretical yield of a reaction is 10.0 g of product, but you only isolate 8.5 g.

  1. Identify the values:
    • Actual yield = 8.5 g
    • Theoretical yield = 10.0 g
  2. Apply the formula:
    \[ \text{Percent yield} = \frac{8.5\ \text{g}}{10.0\ \text{g}} \times 100\% = 0.85 \times 100\% = 85\% \]
  3. Interpretation: the reaction ran at 85% efficiency.

How to find theoretical yield (quick overview)

The percent yield calculator assumes you already know the theoretical yield. To find it from a balanced equation:

  1. Write and balance the chemical equation.
  2. Convert the given amount of limiting reactant to moles.
  3. Use the mole ratio to find moles of product.
  4. Convert moles of product to grams using molar mass (if needed).

The result is your theoretical yield.

Typical percent yield ranges

  • 90–100% – excellent yield; very efficient reaction.
  • 70–90% – common in many organic and inorganic syntheses.
  • < 70% – often indicates significant losses, side reactions, or incomplete conversion.
  • > 100% – usually due to impurities, solvent still present, or weighing errors.

Common reasons for low percent yield

  • Incomplete reaction (equilibrium not fully shifted, insufficient time or temperature).
  • Side reactions forming by-products.
  • Losses during filtration, transfer, or purification.
  • Product decomposition (heat, light, or air sensitivity).

Common reasons for percent yield > 100%

  • Product not fully dried (contains solvent or water).
  • Contamination with unreacted reagents or side products.
  • Balance calibration or weighing errors.

FAQ

What units should I use for yield?

Any consistent unit works: grams, kilograms, milligrams, or moles. The key is that actual and theoretical yield must use the same unit, because percent yield is a ratio.

Is it better to use moles or grams?

For calculations on paper, chemists often use moles. In the lab, you usually weigh products in grams. As long as both yields are in the same unit, the percent yield will be the same.

Do I always use the limiting reactant?

Yes. Theoretical yield is based on the limiting reactant – the reactant that runs out first. If you use an excess reactant to compute theoretical yield, your percent yield will be incorrect.

Percent Yield – Quick Questions

What is percent yield in chemistry?

Percent yield measures how efficiently a reaction produces product. It is the ratio of actual yield (what you obtained) to theoretical yield (maximum possible), multiplied by 100%.

Can percent yield be negative?

No. If your calculation gives a negative value, check your inputs. Actual and theoretical yield should both be positive numbers, and percent yield should be zero or greater.

Why is my percent yield over 100%?

Percent yields above 100% almost always indicate experimental issues: wet or impure product, unremoved solvent, or weighing errors. Re-dry and re-weigh the product, and verify your theoretical yield calculation.
