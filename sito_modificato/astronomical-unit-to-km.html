Conversion formulas: astronomical units to kilometers and back

Modern astronomy uses a fixed, exact definition of the astronomical unit (AU). Since 2012, the International Astronomical Union (IAU) defines:

1 AU = 149,597,870.7 km (exactly)

AU → km

To convert a distance from astronomical units to kilometers, multiply by the conversion factor:

\(\text{km} = \text{AU} \times 149{,}597{,}870.7\)

Example: How many kilometers are 2.5 AU?

\(\text{km} = 2.5 \times 149{,}597{,}870.7 \approx 373{,}994{,}676.75\ \text{km}\)

km → AU

To convert from kilometers to astronomical units, divide by the same factor:

\(\text{AU} = \dfrac{\text{km}}{149{,}597{,}870.7}\)

Example: How many AU are 300,000,000 km?

\(\text{AU} = \dfrac{300{,}000{,}000}{149{,}597{,}870.7} \approx 2.005\ \text{AU}\)

What is an astronomical unit (AU)?

An astronomical unit is a unit of length used mainly for distances within the Solar System. It is approximately the average distance between Earth and the Sun.

  • Symbol: AU or au (sometimes “ua” in older literature)
  • Exact length: 149,597,870,700 meters = 149,597,870.7 km
  • Typical use: orbital radii, planet–Sun distances, spacecraft trajectories

Historically, the AU was defined via the semi-major axis of Earth’s orbit, which depended on observations and could change as measurements improved. The 2012 IAU resolution fixed the AU as a constant length in meters, simplifying calculations and ensuring consistency.

FAQ

How many kilometers are in 1 astronomical unit?

Exactly 149,597,870.7 km. This is a defined constant, not a measured approximation.

Why do astronomers use AU instead of kilometers?

Using AU keeps Solar System distances in manageable numbers. Saying “Jupiter orbits at 5.2 AU” is easier to read and compare than “778,570,000 km”. It also reflects the natural scale of planetary orbits relative to Earth’s orbit.

Is 1 AU always the Earth–Sun distance?

Earth’s orbit is slightly elliptical, so its actual distance from the Sun varies from about 0.983 AU (perihelion) to 1.017 AU (aphelion). The AU is based on the average orbital radius (semi-major axis), but since 2012 it is defined as a fixed length in meters, independent of small orbital variations.

How does AU compare to light-years and parsecs?

  • 1 light-year ≈ 63,241 AU
  • 1 parsec ≈ 206,265 AU ≈ 3.26 light-years

AU is best for Solar System scales; light-years and parsecs are more convenient for interstellar and intergalactic distances.
