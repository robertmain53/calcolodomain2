How this concrete mix calculator works

This tool lets you design a simple concrete mix by volume, using a classic cement : sand : coarse aggregate ratio and a chosen water–cement ratio (w/c). It then converts the mix into practical quantities: volumes and weights of each component, plus the number of cement bags.

1. Mix ratio by volume

A mix ratio like 1 : 2 : 3 means:

  • 1 part cement
  • 2 parts sand (fine aggregate)
  • 3 parts coarse aggregate (gravel or crushed stone)

The calculator sums the parts:

Total parts = cement + sand + aggregate

Fraction of cement = cement parts / total parts

Fraction of sand = sand parts / total parts

Fraction of aggregate = aggregate parts / total parts

2. From concrete volume to component volumes

For a required concrete volume \(V_c\) and a waste factor \(w\) (e.g. 5%), the calculator first inflates the target:

\( V_\text{target} = V_c \times (1 + w/100) \)

Then it allocates that volume to each component according to the ratio:

\( V_\text{cement} = V_\text{target} \times \dfrac{\text{cement parts}}{\text{total parts}} \)

\( V_\text{sand} = V_\text{target} \times \dfrac{\text{sand parts}}{\text{total parts}} \)

\( V_\text{agg} = V_\text{target} \times \dfrac{\text{agg parts}}{\text{total parts}} \)

3. Converting volumes to weights

Using the bulk densities you provide (defaults are typical values):

  • Cement ≈ 1440 kg/m³ (90 lb/ft³)
  • Sand ≈ 1600 kg/m³ (100 lb/ft³)
  • Coarse aggregate ≈ 1500 kg/m³ (94 lb/ft³)

The calculator multiplies:

\( m = \rho \times V \)

where \(m\) is mass, \(\rho\) is bulk density, and \(V\) is volume.

Cement mass is then divided by your bag size (e.g. 25 kg or 40 kg) to give the number of bags, rounded up to the next whole bag.

4. Water–cement ratio and water quantity

The water–cement ratio (w/c) is defined by weight:

\( \text{w/c} = \dfrac{m_\text{water}}{m_\text{cement}} \)

so \( m_\text{water} = \text{w/c} \times m_\text{cement} \)

Assuming water density ≈ 1 kg/L (62.4 lb/ft³), the calculator converts water mass to liters or gallons.

Typical concrete mix ratios

Always follow local codes and manufacturer datasheets, but these ballpark ratios are common for site-mixed concrete:

  • 1 : 2 : 3 – general-purpose slabs, paths, footings
  • 1 : 1.5 : 3 – higher strength structural elements (with proper design)
  • 1 : 2 : 4 – lean mixes, blinding layers, non-structural

Pre-bagged products from brands like QUIKRETE, Sakrete, Basalite, etc. are factory-designed mixes. When using bagged concrete, follow the water dosage and yield printed on the bag rather than overriding with your own ratio.

Practical tips for mixing concrete

1. Measure consistently

  • Use the same bucket or shovel count for all components.
  • Do not guess by eye; small errors accumulate quickly on larger pours.

2. Control water

  • Add about 80–90% of the calculated water first.
  • Mix thoroughly, then add the remaining water slowly until you reach the desired workability.
  • A wetter mix is easier to place but reduces strength and durability.

3. When to use a structural mix design

For critical elements (foundations, columns, beams, suspended slabs, retaining walls), a qualified engineer or ready-mix supplier should provide a tested mix design that meets the specified compressive strength and exposure class. This calculator is best used for:

  • Small slabs, patios, walkways
  • Non-structural pads and footpaths
  • Estimating material quantities before ordering

Concrete mix FAQ

What is the best concrete mix ratio for a driveway?

For driveways, many contractors use a mix around 1 : 2 : 3 or 1 : 1.5 : 3 with a low water–cement ratio (≈0.45–0.50) and proper air entrainment in cold climates. The exact specification should come from local codes or an engineer, considering soil conditions, freeze–thaw cycles and load requirements.

Can I use this calculator for ready-mix truck orders?

Yes, you can use it to estimate the total volume and approximate cement content, but ready-mix suppliers normally quote by cubic meter or cubic yard of a standard strength class (e.g. 25 MPa / 3500 psi). Provide them with the required strength and exposure class rather than a simple 1:2:3 ratio.

Why is the total component volume larger than the concrete volume?

When you add cement, sand and aggregate, the smaller particles fill the voids between larger ones. The compacted concrete volume is therefore less than the sum of the loose component volumes. This calculator works in terms of the finished concrete volume you need, then back-calculates the required loose volumes using typical bulk densities and ratios.
