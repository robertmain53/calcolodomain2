About this D&D name generator

This D&D name generator is designed for Dungeon Masters and players who want fast, lore-friendly names without scrolling through endless lists. Instead of static tables, it uses race-specific syllables, fantasy phonetics, and templates for characters, taverns, guilds, and places.

You can filter by race, gender, class flavor, tone (good, neutral, evil, whimsical), and style (short, classic, elaborate), then generate up to 50 names at once. It’s perfect for building NPC rosters, random encounter tables, or naming an entire town in minutes.

What makes this generator different?

  • Race-aware names: Elves sound flowing and melodic, dwarves are harsher and grounded, tieflings and dragonborn lean exotic.
  • Multiple name types: Characters, taverns/inns, guilds/factions, and places/locations.
  • Alignment & tone: Tilt names toward heroic, neutral, dark, or whimsical vibes.
  • Batch generation: Up to 50 names at once, ready to paste into your notes or VTT.
  • Copy-friendly output: One click copies the entire list to your clipboard.

How to use the D&D name generator effectively

  1. Select a Name type (Character, Tavern, Guild, or Place).
  2. For characters, choose a Race and optionally Gender and Class flavor.
  3. Pick a Tone / alignment to nudge names toward heroic, neutral, dark, or whimsical.

Tips for choosing great D&D character names

Match the name to the character concept

  • Backstory: A noble-born paladin might have a formal, multi-part name; a street urchin rogue might use a short nickname.
  • Culture: Use the race filter to keep names consistent within a region or kingdom.
  • Alignment: Dark, sharp-sounding names often suit villains; softer, open vowels feel more heroic or gentle.

Keep it playable at the table

  • Favor names that are easy to pronounce and remember.
  • Consider giving each character a short nickname even if their full name is elaborate.
  • Avoid names that sound too similar for party members (e.g., “Thorin” and “Thoran”).

FAQ

Can I use these names in published adventures?

Yes. Names are generated procedurally and are not copied from official D&D products. You can use them in home games, streams, or published material. Just remember that Dungeons & Dragons and related terms are trademarks of their respective owners.

Does this work for other fantasy RPGs?

Absolutely. While tuned for D&D-style fantasy, the names work well for Pathfinder, OSR games, and most generic fantasy systems.

How random are the names?

Names are built from curated syllable lists and templates with randomness layered on top. This keeps them pronounceable and thematic while still giving you a huge variety of combinations.
