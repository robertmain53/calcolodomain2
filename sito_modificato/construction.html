How these construction calculators work

This hub is designed for quick, practical planning. It combines several core construction calculations that homeowners, contractors, and estimators use every day.

1. Concrete volume

For a rectangular slab or footing, the basic volume formula is:

Volume = Length × Width × Thickness

If you work in feet, the result is in cubic feet. To convert to cubic yards:

cubic yards = cubic feet ÷ 27

The calculator also applies a waste factor to account for spillage, over-excavation, and uneven subgrade.

2. Lumber board feet

Board feet is a standard unit for rough lumber:

Board feet per board = (Thickness in inches × Width in inches × Length in feet) ÷ 12

Total board feet = Board feet per board × Quantity

3. Roofing area and squares

For a simple gable roof, the plan area is building length × width. Roof surface area increases with pitch:

Plan area = Length × Width

Pitch factor = √(1 + (rise / 12)²)

Roof area ≈ Plan area × Pitch factor

Squares = Roof area ÷ 100

4. Excavation volume and swell

Excavation volume uses the same rectangular volume formula, but soil expands (swell) when loosened:

In-place volume = Length × Width × Depth

Loose volume = In-place volume × (1 + Swell% / 100)

Limitations and professional use

  • Assumes simple rectangular geometry and uniform depths.
  • Does not check structural capacity, rebar design, or code compliance.
  • Unit prices are user-supplied and may vary by region and market conditions.

For critical structures, always consult local building codes, OSHA safety requirements, and a licensed engineer or architect.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use this for bidding jobs?

You can use it for preliminary takeoffs and sanity checks, but detailed bids should be based on full drawings, specifications, and trade-specific estimating software or spreadsheets.

How do I improve accuracy?

  • Measure from final construction drawings, not rough sketches.
  • Use realistic waste and swell factors based on past projects.
  • Update unit prices regularly from your suppliers.

  • Check lead times for materials before locking in a schedule.
  • Document your assumptions (waste, prices, dimensions) with each estimate.
