Regular nonagon formulas

This calculator assumes a regular nonagon (9‑sided polygon with all sides and angles equal). Let:

  • n = number of sides = 9
  • s = side length
  • P = perimeter
  • A = area
  • R = circumradius (radius of circumscribed circle)
  • a = inradius / apothem (radius of inscribed circle)
  • θ = central angle (in radians or degrees)

Key angle formulas

For any regular n‑gon:

  • Central angle: \( \theta_c = \dfrac{360^\circ}{n} \Rightarrow \theta_c = \dfrac{360^\circ}{9} = 40^\circ \)
  • Interior angle (each): \( \theta_i = \dfrac{(n-2)\cdot 180^\circ}{n} \Rightarrow \theta_i = \dfrac{7\cdot 180^\circ}{9} = 140^\circ \)
  • Exterior angle (each): \( \theta_e = 180^\circ - \theta_i = 40^\circ \)

Perimeter and side length

Perimeter is just 9 times the side length:

\[ P = 9s \quad \Rightarrow \quad s = \frac{P}{9} \]

Area of a regular nonagon

Using the general regular polygon area formula:

\[ A = \frac{n s^2}{4 \tan(\pi/n)} \]

For a nonagon (n = 9):

\[ A = \frac{9 s^2}{4 \tan(\pi/9)} \]

You can also express area using the apothem:

\[ A = \frac{1}{2} P a = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 9s \cdot a \]

Radii and side length relationships

From the central triangle of the nonagon:

  • \( R = \dfrac{s}{2 \sin(\pi/9)} \)
  • \( a = \dfrac{s}{2 \tan(\pi/9)} \)
  • \( s = 2R \sin(\pi/9) \)
  • \( s = 2a \tan(\pi/9) \)

How the nonagon calculator works

The tool is bidirectional: it can start from any one of the main parameters and derive the rest using the formulas above.

  1. Normalize units – your input is treated as a base length or area in the chosen unit.
  2. Determine side length – depending on which field you filled:
    • From perimeter: \( s = P/9 \)
    • From area: solve \( A = \dfrac{9 s^2}{4 \tan(\pi/9)} \Rightarrow s = \sqrt{\dfrac{4A \tan(\pi/9)}{9}} \)
    • From circumradius: \( s = 2R \sin(\pi/9) \)
    • From apothem: \( s = 2a \tan(\pi/9) \)
    • From central angle: for a regular nonagon θ must be 40°, so the calculator simply checks consistency.
  3. Compute all other properties – perimeter, area, radii, and angles are then calculated from the side length.
  4. Format results – values are rounded to a reasonable number of decimal places (up to 6 for small values).

Worked example

Suppose you have a regular nonagon with side length \( s = 5\ \text{cm} \).

  • Perimeter: \[ P = 9s = 9 \cdot 5 = 45\ \text{cm} \]
  • Circumradius: \[ R = \frac{s}{2\sin(\pi/9)} \approx \frac{5}{2\sin(20^\circ)} \approx 7.30\ \text{cm} \]
  • Apothem: \[ a = \frac{s}{2\tan(\pi/9)} \approx \frac{5}{2\tan(20^\circ)} \approx 6.87\ \text{cm} \]
  • Area: \[ A = \frac{1}{2}Pa \approx \frac{1}{2}\cdot 45 \cdot 6.87 \approx 154.6\ \text{cm}^2 \]

FAQ

Is a nonagon always regular?

No. A nonagon is any 9‑sided polygon. It is regular only if all sides and all angles are equal. This calculator is specifically for the regular case.

Can I use this for an irregular nonagon?

Not directly. For an irregular nonagon you would need side lengths and angles individually, and the formulas above no longer apply. You would typically split the shape into triangles and sum their areas.

What is the difference between circumradius and inradius?

The circumradius is the radius of the circle that passes through all 9 vertices. The inradius (or apothem) is the radius of the circle tangent to all 9 sides inside the nonagon.
