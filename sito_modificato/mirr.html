What is MIRR (Modified Internal Rate of Return)?

The Modified Internal Rate of Return (MIRR) is an improvement over the traditional IRR. It assumes that:

  • Negative cash flows are financed at a specified finance rate (often the cost of capital or borrowing rate).
  • Positive cash flows are reinvested at a specified reinvestment rate (often the firm’s hurdle rate or expected return).

This avoids the multiple‑IRR problem and gives a single, economically meaningful rate of return for projects with non‑conventional or uneven cash flows.

MIRR formula

Let:

  • \( CF_t \) = cash flow at period \( t \)
  • \( r_f \) = finance rate (as a decimal)
  • \( r_r \) = reinvestment rate (as a decimal)
  • \( n \) = number of periods between the first and last cash flow

Step 1 – Present value of negative cash flows (PV of outflows)

\[ PV_{\text{neg}} = \sum_{t = t_0}^{t_n} \frac{CF_t}{(1 + r_f)^t} \quad \text{for } CF_t < 0 \]

Step 2 – Future value of positive cash flows (FV of inflows)

\[ FV_{\text{pos}} = \sum_{t = t_0}^{t_n} CF_t \cdot (1 + r_r)^{(t_n - t)} \quad \text{for } CF_t > 0 \]

Step 3 – MIRR

\[ \text{MIRR} = \left( \frac{FV_{\text{pos}}}{-PV_{\text{neg}}} \right)^{\frac{1}{n}} - 1 \]

Interpreting the MIRR result

  • If MIRR > required return (hurdle rate), the project is generally acceptable.
  • If MIRR < required return, the project may destroy value and should be rejected.
  • Between mutually exclusive projects, the one with the higher MIRR is usually preferred, assuming similar scale and risk.

Example: MIRR step‑by‑step

Suppose you have the following annual cash flows (in thousands):

  • Year 0: −100 (initial investment)
  • Year 1: +30
  • Year 2: +40
  • Year 3: +50

Assume a finance rate of 8% and a reinvestment rate of 10%.

1. PV of negative cash flows

There is only one negative cash flow at t = 0:

\[ PV_{\text{neg}} = \frac{-100}{(1 + 0.08)^0} = -100 \]

2. FV of positive cash flows at t = 3

\[ \begin{aligned} FV_{\text{pos}} &= 30(1 + 0.10)^{3-1} + 40(1 + 0.10)^{3-2} + 50(1 + 0.10)^{3-3} \\ &= 30(1.10)^2 + 40(1.10)^1 + 50(1.10)^0 \\ &= 30 \cdot 1.21 + 40 \cdot 1.10 + 50 \cdot 1 \\ &= 36.3 + 44 + 50 = 130.3 \end{aligned} \]

3. MIRR

Here \( n = 3 \) (from t = 0 to t = 3):

\[ \text{MIRR} = \left( \frac{130.3}{100} \right)^{1/3} - 1 \approx (1.303)^{0.3333} - 1 \approx 0.092 \text{ or } 9.2\% \]

So the project’s modified internal rate of return is about 9.2% per year.

MIRR vs IRR: when MIRR is better

  • Multiple IRRs: If cash flows change sign more than once (e.g., − + + − +), IRR can have multiple solutions or none. MIRR always gives a single rate.
  • Reinvestment assumption: IRR assumes reinvestment at the IRR itself, which can be unrealistic (especially if IRR is very high). MIRR lets you set a realistic reinvestment rate.
  • Comparability: MIRR is often more comparable across projects because it uses a consistent finance and reinvestment rate.

Tips for using this MIRR calculator

  • Enter all cash flows in the same currency and units (e.g., dollars, thousands, or millions).
  • Use a finance rate close to your weighted average cost of capital (WACC) or borrowing rate.
  • Use a reinvestment rate equal to your target return or expected return on reinvested cash.
  • Try different reinvestment rates to see how sensitive MIRR is to your assumptions.

MIRR Calculator – FAQ

What is MIRR (Modified Internal Rate of Return)?

MIRR is the Modified Internal Rate of Return. It refines the traditional IRR by assuming that negative cash flows are financed at a finance rate and positive cash flows are reinvested at a reinvestment rate. This produces a single, more realistic rate of return, especially for projects with non‑conventional cash flow patterns.

How does this MIRR calculator work?

The calculator separates your cash flows into negative (outflows) and positive (inflows). It discounts all negative cash flows back to time 0 using the finance rate, compounds all positive cash flows to the final period using the reinvestment rate, and then computes the MIRR using the standard formula: \[ \text{MIRR} = \left( \frac{FV_{\text{pos}}}{-PV_{\text{neg}}} \right)^{1/n} - 1 \] where \( n \) is the number of periods between the first and last cash flow.

What is the difference between finance rate and reinvestment rate?

The finance rate is the cost of capital or borrowing rate used to discount negative cash flows (investments, additional costs). The reinvestment rate is the rate at which you assume positive cash flows can be reinvested until the end of the project. In practice, both are often set to the firm’s WACC or hurdle rate, but they can differ if financing and reinvestment conditions are not the same.

Can MIRR be negative?

Yes. If the future value of positive cash flows is less than the present value of negative cash flows, the MIRR will be negative. This indicates that the project does not recover its cost of capital under the assumed finance and reinvestment rates.
