Torque formula

In vector form, torque is defined as the cross product of the position vector r (from the axis to the point of application of the force) and the force vector F:

\(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}\)

For many practical problems (like a wrench on a bolt), we only need the magnitude:

\(|\tau| = r \cdot F \cdot \sin(\theta)\)

  • \(r\): distance from axis to where the force is applied (lever arm)
  • \(F\): magnitude of the force
  • \(\theta\): angle between \(\vec{r}\) and \(\vec{F}\)

If the force is applied perpendicular to the lever arm (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)), then \(\sin(\theta) = 1\) and the formula simplifies to:

\(\tau = r \cdot F\)

Solving for force or distance

Rearranging the basic formula (for \(\theta = 90^\circ\)):

  • Given torque and distance, find force:

    \(F = \dfrac{\tau}{r}\)

  • Given torque and force, find distance:

    \(r = \dfrac{\tau}{F}\)

Units of torque

The SI unit of torque is the newton‑metre (N·m):

  • 1 N·m = 1 newton of force applied at 1 metre from the axis, perpendicular to the lever arm.

Common engineering units:

  • 1 N·m ≈ 0.73756 ft·lb
  • 1 ft·lb ≈ 1.35582 N·m
  • 1 N·m = 100 N·cm = 1000 N·mm
  • 1 kgf·m ≈ 9.80665 N·m

Worked example

Example: You apply a 15 N force at the end of a 0.25 m wrench, perpendicular to the wrench. What torque do you produce?

Given:

  • \(F = 15\ \text{N}\)
  • \(r = 0.25\ \text{m}\)
  • \(\theta = 90^\circ \Rightarrow \sin(\theta) = 1\)

Compute:

\(\tau = r \cdot F = 0.25 \times 15 = 3.75\ \text{N·m}\)

In ft·lb:

\(3.75\ \text{N·m} \times 0.73756 \approx 2.77\ \text{ft·lb}\)

Torque vs. work and power

  • Torque causes rotation; it is a vector quantity.
  • Work is energy transferred; for rotation with constant torque:

    \(W = \tau \cdot \theta\)

    where \(\theta\) is in radians.

  • Power in rotational motion:

    \(P = \tau \cdot \omega\)

    where \(\omega\) is angular velocity in rad/s.

Frequently asked questions

What is torque in simple terms?

Torque is the “twisting force” that makes things rotate. Pushing a door near the handle is easier than near the hinges because the same force at a larger distance produces more torque.

Why does the angle matter?

Only the component of the force perpendicular to the lever arm produces torque. If the force is at an angle \(\theta\), the effective force is \(F_\perp = F \sin(\theta)\), so the torque is \(r \cdot F \cdot \sin(\theta)\).

Can torque be negative?

Yes. The sign indicates the direction of rotation relative to your chosen convention (e.g., counterclockwise positive, clockwise negative). The calculator reports magnitude; sign must be assigned based on your coordinate system.
