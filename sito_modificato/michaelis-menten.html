Michaelis-Menten equation

\( v = \dfrac{V_{\max}[S]}{K_m + [S]} \)

  • v – initial reaction rate
  • Vmax – maximum rate at saturating substrate
  • Km – Michaelis constant (substrate concentration at v = Vmax/2)
  • [S] – substrate concentration

How this calculator works

In single-point mode, the tool directly evaluates the Michaelis-Menten equation for your chosen Km, Vmax and [S]. It also reports the fraction of Vmax (\(v/V_{\max}\)), which is useful to see how close you are to saturation.

In fitting mode, the calculator uses nonlinear least squares (Levenberg–Marquardt style iteration) to find Km and Vmax that best fit your experimental v vs [S] data. It then:

  • Plots the fitted Michaelis-Menten curve with your data points
  • Generates Lineweaver–Burk and Eadie–Hofstee linearized plots
  • Reports an approximate R² as a measure of goodness of fit

Interpreting Km and Vmax

Under the usual Michaelis-Menten assumptions (single-substrate, steady-state, negligible product inhibition):

  • Vmax reflects the catalytic capacity of the enzyme at saturating substrate.
  • Km is the [S] at which v = Vmax/2. A lower Km often means higher apparent affinity.
  • The ratio Vmax/Km is a measure of catalytic efficiency when [S] ≪ Km.

Common use cases

  • Estimating Km and Vmax from initial rate experiments
  • Comparing wild-type vs mutant enzymes
  • Checking whether an enzyme is near saturation at physiological [S]
  • Teaching and visualizing classic enzyme kinetics plots

Assumptions and limitations

  • Single-substrate reaction following classic Michaelis-Menten behavior
  • Initial rates (no significant product accumulation or substrate depletion)
  • No allosteric effects, cooperativity, or strong product inhibition
  • No explicit inhibitor terms (competitive, noncompetitive, etc.) in this basic model

For strongly cooperative enzymes, multi-substrate reactions, or detailed inhibitor analysis, more advanced models (Hill equation, ordered/rapid-equilibrium mechanisms, full mechanistic fits) are required.
