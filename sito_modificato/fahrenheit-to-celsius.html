The Formula Explained

\( °C = \frac{5}{9} \times (°F - 32) \)

Where °F is the temperature in Fahrenheit and °C is the temperature in Celsius.

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

For example, to convert 98.6°F to Celsius:

  1. Subtract 32 from 98.6: \( 98.6 - 32 = 66.6 \)
  2. Multiply 66.6 by 5/9: \( 66.6 \times \frac{5}{9} = 37 \text{ °C} \)

Result: 98.6°F = 37°C (normal human body temperature)

Glossary of Variables

  • °F (Fahrenheit): A temperature scale where water freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F at standard atmospheric pressure. Commonly used in the United States.
  • °C (Celsius): A temperature scale where water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C at standard atmospheric pressure. Used worldwide and in scientific contexts.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the formula to convert Fahrenheit to Celsius?

The formula is \( °C = \frac{5}{9} \times (°F - 32) \). This accounts for both the different zero points and the different degree sizes between the two scales.

Why do we subtract 32 in the formula?

The number 32 is subtracted to adjust for the offset in the Fahrenheit scale where water freezes at 32°F (compared to 0°C in Celsius). This aligns the two scales before applying the ratio.

Is this conversion used worldwide?

Celsius is widely used around the world, especially in scientific contexts, while Fahrenheit is primarily used in the United States, its territories, and a few other countries.

Are there other temperature scales?

Yes, other scales include Kelvin, which is used in scientific contexts and starts at absolute zero, and Rankine, which is used in some engineering fields (primarily in the United States).

How accurate is this calculator?

This tool provides conversions rounded to two decimal places, which is suitable for most practical applications including cooking, weather, and scientific work.
