How the cycling speed calculator works

The main calculator uses the classic average speed formula:

Average speed \( v \) is:

\[ v = \frac{d}{t} \]

  • \( d \) = distance (km or miles)
  • \( t \) = moving time (hours)

The tool converts your distance and time into consistent units, then solves for whichever variable (speed, time or distance) you selected:

  • Speed: \( v = d / t \)
  • Time: \( t = d / v \)
  • Distance: \( d = v \cdot t \)

Power‑based speed estimation (simplified physics)

The power‑based estimator uses a simplified cycling physics model. At a steady speed on a constant gradient, your power output \( P \) roughly balances:

  • Air resistance (drag)
  • Rolling resistance from tires and surface
  • Gravity on climbs or descents

We approximate:

\[ P \approx \tfrac{1}{2} \rho C_d A v^3 + C_{rr} m g v + m g \sin(\theta) v \]

  • \( \rho \) = air density
  • \( C_d A \) = drag area (depends on bike and position)
  • \( C_{rr} \) = rolling resistance coefficient
  • \( m \) = total mass (rider + bike)
  • \( g \) = 9.81 m/s²
  • \( \theta \) = road angle (from gradient)
  • \( v \) = speed

The calculator picks reasonable default values for \( C_d A \) and \( C_{rr} \) based on your bike type and solves numerically for \( v \). Wind is handled by adjusting the effective air speed.

Factors that affect your cycling speed

  • Fitness and power: Higher sustainable power (FTP) means higher speed, especially on climbs.
  • Bike and position: Aero bikes and tucked positions reduce drag significantly.
  • Terrain: Hills, corners and rough surfaces all slow you down.
  • Wind: Headwinds can cut speed dramatically; tailwinds and drafting increase it.
  • Weight: Matters most on climbs; less important on flat, windy rides.

Practical pacing examples

  • Example 1 – Commute planning: 12 km to work, you average 20 km/h → time ≈ 36 minutes.
  • Example 2 – Sportive goal: 100 km event, target 25 km/h → time ≈ 4 hours.
  • Example 3 – Climb estimate: 5 km climb at 7% gradient, 75 kg rider + 10 kg bike, 250 W → use the power estimator to get an approximate climbing speed and time.

FAQ

How do you calculate cycling average speed?

Convert your distance to kilometers or miles and your moving time to hours, then divide distance by time. For example, 30 km in 1 h 30 min is 1.5 hours, so 30 ÷ 1.5 = 20 km/h.

What is a realistic average speed for a new cyclist?

On mostly flat roads, many new cyclists ride at 15–20 km/h (9–12 mph). After a few months of riding consistently, 20–24 km/h (12–15 mph) is very common. Don’t compare yourself directly to group ride or race speeds, which benefit from drafting and experience.

Why is my speed slower on hills even at the same power?

On climbs, more of your power goes into lifting your body against gravity, so speed drops sharply. On descents, gravity helps you, but air resistance grows quickly with speed, so you hit a “terminal” speed where drag balances your power and gravity.

How accurate is the power‑based speed estimate?

It’s usually in the right ballpark for steady efforts, but real‑world speed also depends on micro‑terrain, corners, drafting, road surface, tire choice, air density and how steady you hold power. Treat it as a planning and comparison tool, not an exact prediction.
