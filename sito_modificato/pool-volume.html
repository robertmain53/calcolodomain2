How this pool volume calculator works

This tool estimates how much water your swimming pool holds using standard geometric formulas. You can switch between feet/gallons and meters/liters, choose the pool shape, and optionally include steps or benches for a more accurate result.

1. Rectangular pool volume formula

Step 1 – Average depth

\[ d_\text{avg} = \frac{d_\text{shallow} + d_\text{deep}}{2} \]

Step 2 – Volume

In cubic units:

\[ V = L \times W \times d_\text{avg} \]

In US gallons (dimensions in feet):

\[ V_\text{gal} = L \times W \times d_\text{avg} \times 7.48 \]

In liters (dimensions in meters):

\[ V_\text{L} = L \times W \times d_\text{avg} \times 1000 \]

2. Round pool volume formula

First compute the radius \( r = \frac{D}{2} \).

\[ V = \pi r^2 d_\text{avg} \]

Then convert to gallons or liters as above.

3. Oval pool volume formula

For an oval pool with length \( L \) and width \( W \):

\[ V = \pi \times \frac{L}{2} \times \frac{W}{2} \times d_\text{avg} = \frac{\pi}{4} L W d_\text{avg} \]

4. Kidney / irregular pool approximation

Irregular and kidney-shaped pools are approximated by treating them like a rectangle with an average length and width:

\[ V \approx L_\text{avg} \times W_\text{avg} \times d_\text{avg} \]

For better accuracy, measure width at several points along the pool and average the values.

5. Steps and benches

Built-in steps and benches hold water too. We approximate them as a small rectangular prism:

\[ V_\text{steps} = L_\text{steps} \times W_\text{steps} \times d_\text{steps} \]

Total pool volume:

\[ V_\text{total} = V_\text{pool} + V_\text{steps} \]

Worked example

Suppose you have a rectangular in-ground pool:

  • Length = 30 ft
  • Width = 15 ft
  • Shallow depth = 3 ft
  • Deep depth = 8 ft

Average depth:

\[ d_\text{avg} = \frac{3 + 8}{2} = 5.5 \text{ ft} \]

Volume in cubic feet:

\[ V = 30 \times 15 \times 5.5 = 2475 \text{ ft}^3 \]

Convert to gallons:

\[ V_\text{gal} = 2475 \times 7.48 \approx 18{,}501 \text{ gallons} \]

Tips for accurate pool volume measurements

  • Measure length and width at the waterline, not the coping.
  • Use a rigid measuring tape for better accuracy on in-ground pools.
  • For sloped bottoms, always use the average of shallow and deep depths.
  • For very irregular shapes, break the pool into sections, calculate each, and add them.

Using pool volume for chemical dosing

Most pool chemicals specify dosage per 10,000 gallons (or per cubic meter). Once you know your pool volume, you can scale the recommended dose:

Example: Product label says “2 oz per 10,000 gallons” and your pool is 18,500 gallons.

\[ \text{Dose} = 2 \times \frac{18{,}500}{10{,}000} \approx 3.7 \text{ oz} \]

Always follow manufacturer instructions and add chemicals gradually, allowing time to circulate before retesting the water.
