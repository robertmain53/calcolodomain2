What is a circular sector?

A circular sector is the region of a circle bounded by two radii and the arc between them – like a “slice of pizza”. It is completely determined by the radius \(r\) and the central angle \(\theta\).

Formulas: area, arc length, and perimeter

If the angle \(\theta\) is measured in radians, the key formulas are:

\[ A = \tfrac12 r^2 \theta \] \[ s = r\theta \] \[ P = 2r + s = 2r + r\theta \]

where:

  • \(A\) is the sector area,
  • \(s\) is the arc length,
  • \(P\) is the sector perimeter (two radii plus the arc).

If the angle is given in degrees \(\theta_{\text{deg}}\), first convert to radians:

\[ \theta = \theta_{\text{deg}} \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} \quad\Rightarrow\quad A = \frac{\pi r^2 \theta_{\text{deg}}}{360}, \quad s = \frac{\pi r \theta_{\text{deg}}}{180}. \]

Rearranging the formulas

Sometimes you know the area and need to recover radius or angle. From \(A = \tfrac12 r^2 \theta\) (with \(\theta\) in radians) we can solve:

  • For angle (given \(A\) and \(r\)): \[ \theta = \frac{2A}{r^2}. \]
  • For radius (given \(A\) and \(\theta\)): \[ r = \sqrt{\frac{2A}{\theta}}. \]

The calculator automates these rearrangements and converts angles to and from degrees for you.

Worked example

Suppose \(r = 5\ \text{m}\) and \(\theta_{\text{deg}} = 60^\circ\).

  • Convert to radians: \(\theta = 60^\circ \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{3}\).
  • Area: \[ A = \tfrac12 r^2 \theta = \tfrac12 \cdot 25 \cdot \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{25\pi}{6} \approx 13.09\ \text{m}^2. \]
  • Arc length: \[ s = r\theta = 5 \cdot \frac{\pi}{3} \approx 5.24\ \text{m}. \]
  • Perimeter: \[ P = 2r + s = 10 + 5.24 \approx 15.24\ \text{m}. \]

FAQ: circular sector area calculator

What angle range is valid for a sector?

For a standard sector in a full circle, the central angle satisfies \(0 < \theta \le 2\pi\) radians (or \(0^\circ < \theta \le 360^\circ\)). In some applications one may consider angles larger than a full turn, but those are usually treated as multiple turns rather than a single geometric sector.

Which units should I use for radius?

You can use any consistent length unit: metres, centimetres, inches, feet, and so on. The sector area will then be in the corresponding squared unit (m², cm², in², …), while arc length and perimeter use the original unit of the radius.

Why do engineers often prefer radians?

Many formulas in geometry, trigonometry, and physics simplify when angles are in radians. For sectors, the area becomes \(A = \tfrac12 r^2 \theta\) and the arc length \(s = r\theta\) without extra conversion factors. This reduces mistakes and makes derivatives and integrals more natural.

Can this calculator replace formal design checks?

No. The tool uses standard formulas and double-precision arithmetic, which is more than adequate for most classroom and many practical calculations. For safety-critical structures or regulated design work, always follow the applicable codes, safety factors, and internal verification procedures.

Practical tips

  • Keep angle units consistent – do not mix degrees and radians in the same formula.
  • For very small angles, area and arc length scale linearly with θ; doubling θ doubles both.
  • When checking drawings, compare sector area with the full circle area \(A_{\text{circle}} = \pi r^2\).
  • In CAD or FEA software, cross-check one manual sector calculation against the tool’s output.
