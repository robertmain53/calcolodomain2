How this strength training calculator works

This tool combines several evidence-based formulas and practical coaching rules to give you realistic estimates for your one-rep max (1RM), training max, working weights, and weekly training volume.

1. 1RM estimation from any rep range

When you enter a hard set (weight × reps), the calculator estimates your 1RM using multiple common formulas and then averages them:

Epley: \( 1RM = w \times (1 + r/30) \)

Brzycki: \( 1RM = w \times \frac{36}{37 - r} \)

Lander: \( 1RM = w \times \frac{100}{101.3 - 2.671r} \)

Lombardi: \( 1RM = w \times r^{0.10} \)

O’Connor: \( 1RM = w \times (1 + 0.025r) \)

where w is the weight you lifted and r is the number of reps completed.

Each formula behaves slightly differently at high reps or low reps. By averaging them, we reduce the bias of any single method and give you a more stable estimate.

2. Training max: why it’s lower than your 1RM

Many popular strength programs (5/3/1, Texas Method variants, etc.) use a training max that is 85–90% of your true 1RM. This calculator lets you pick 85%, 87.5%, or 90% and shows the corresponding training max.

Using a training max helps you:

  • Keep technique solid even on tired days
  • Accumulate more high-quality volume
  • Reduce injury risk from constant max attempts

3. Working weights by percentage

Once your 1RM is estimated, we generate a table of working weights at common intensities:

  • 50–65% – technique work, warm-ups, speed work
  • 70–80% – strength & hypertrophy (5–10 reps)
  • 80–90% – heavy strength work (3–5 reps)
  • 90–100% – testing, singles, peaking

These are starting points. Always adjust based on how you feel, bar speed, and your recovery.

4. Volume, tonnage, and weekly workload

The Volume & Tonnage tab lets you log sets for a lift and see:

  • Total sets – how many hard sets you did
  • Total reps – total work done
  • Tonnage – weight × sets × reps

For most intermediate lifters, gradually increasing weekly volume and tonnage (while still recovering) is associated with strength and muscle gains. Large sudden jumps in volume can increase injury risk.

5. Progress comparison

The Progress & Comparison tab converts two performances on the same lift into estimated 1RMs and shows the percentage change. This lets you compare, for example:

  • 100 × 5 vs. 110 × 3 on squat
  • Bench press progress across different rep ranges

Programming guidelines (not medical advice)

General strength guidelines for healthy adults with some lifting experience:

  • Reps: 3–6 reps per set for pure strength, 5–10 for strength + size
  • Sets: 3–6 working sets per lift per session
  • Frequency: 2–3 sessions per week per main lift
  • Intensity: 70–90% of 1RM for most working sets

Always consider your injury history, recovery, and any medical conditions. If you’re unsure whether heavy lifting is safe for you, talk to a qualified healthcare professional before starting or changing a program.
