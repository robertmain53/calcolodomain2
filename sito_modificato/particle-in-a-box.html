Schrödinger equation for a particle in a box

The particle in a box (infinite potential well) is one of the most important exactly solvable models in quantum mechanics. It describes a particle of mass \(m\) confined to a region of space where the potential energy is \(V = 0\) inside the box and \(V = \infty\) at the walls.

1D infinite potential well

Consider a 1D box of length \(L\) with walls at \(x = 0\) and \(x = L\). The time‑independent Schrödinger equation is

\[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} = E\psi(x), \quad 0 < x < L \] \[ \psi(0) = 0,\quad \psi(L) = 0 \]

The boundary conditions force the wavefunction to vanish at the walls. The allowed stationary states are

\[ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1,2,3,\dots \] \[ E_n = \frac{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2}{2mL^2} = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \]

The calculator uses the exact expression with Planck’s constant \(h\) and converts the energy to both joules and electronvolts. The normalization factor \(\sqrt{2/L}\) ensures that the total probability is 1:

\[ \int_0^L |\psi_n(x)|^2\,dx = 1 \]

2D and 3D rectangular boxes

For a 2D box with sides \(L_x\) and \(L_y\), the potential is zero inside \(0

\[ \psi_{n_x,n_y}(x,y) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L_x}}\sin\left(\frac{n_x\pi x}{L_x}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2}{L_y}}\sin\left(\frac{n_y\pi y}{L_y}\right) \] \[ E_{n_x,n_y} = \frac{h^2}{8m}\left( \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} \right) \]

For a 3D rectangular box with sides \(L_x, L_y, L_z\), the normalized wavefunction and energy are

\[ \psi_{n_x,n_y,n_z}(x,y,z) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L_x}}\sin\left(\frac{n_x\pi x}{L_x}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2}{L_y}}\sin\left(\frac{n_y\pi y}{L_y}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2}{L_z}}\sin\left(\frac{n_z\pi z}{L_z}\right) \] \[ E_{n_x,n_y,n_z} = \frac{h^2}{8m}\left( \frac{n_x^2}{L_x^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{L_y^2} + \frac{n_z^2}{L_z^2} \right) \]

Probability density and nodes

The probability density is \(|\psi|^2\). In 1D:

\[ |\psi_n(x)|^2 = \frac{2}{L}\sin^2\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \]

Higher quantum numbers \(n\) have more nodes (points where \(\psi = 0\) inside the box) and higher energy. In 2D and 3D, the number of nodes increases with \(n_x, n_y, n_z\), creating checkerboard‑like patterns in the probability density.

FAQ

Why can’t n be zero?

If \(n = 0\), the sine function \(\sin(n\pi x/L)\) is identically zero, so the wavefunction would vanish everywhere. That is not a physical state, so the lowest allowed quantum number is \(n = 1\).

What happens if I double the box length?

In 1D, the energy scales as \(E_n \propto 1/L^2\). Doubling \(L\) reduces all energy levels by a factor of 4. This is why larger quantum dots have smaller energy gaps.

How does mass affect the energy levels?

The energy is inversely proportional to the mass: \(E_n \propto 1/m\). Heavier particles have more closely spaced energy levels than lighter ones in the same box.

Is this model realistic?

Real systems do not have truly infinite walls, but many quantum wells and nanostructures are well approximated by this model. It captures the essential physics of confinement and quantization while remaining analytically solvable.
