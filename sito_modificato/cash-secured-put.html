How the Cash-Secured Put Calculator Works

A cash-secured put is a conservative options strategy where you sell a put option and keep enough cash in your account to buy the underlying shares if you are assigned. This calculator helps you quantify:

  • How much premium income you receive.
  • Your breakeven price and downside protection.
  • Simple and annualized yield on the cash you reserve.
  • Profit or loss at expiration under different stock price scenarios.
  • Approximate probability of assignment based on implied volatility.

Key Formulas

Shares per contract

\(\text{Shares} = \text{Contracts} \times 100\)

Total premium received

\(\text{Total Premium} = \text{Premium per share} \times \text{Shares}\)

Cash reserved (capital you must set aside)

\(\text{Cash Reserved} = \text{Strike Price} \times \text{Shares}\)

Simple yield for the period

\(\text{Yield} = \dfrac{\text{Total Premium}}{\text{Cash Reserved}}\)

Annualized yield

\(\text{Annualized Yield} = \text{Yield} \times \dfrac{365}{\text{Days to Expiration}}\)

Breakeven price

\(\text{Breakeven} = \text{Strike Price} - \text{Premium per share}\)

Maximum profit

\(\text{Max Profit} = \text{Total Premium}\)

Theoretical maximum loss (if stock goes to zero)

\(\text{Max Loss} = (\text{Strike Price} - \text{Premium per share}) \times \text{Shares}\)

P/L at expiration

  • If stock price at expiration \(S_T \geq K\) (strike): put expires worthless, P/L = Total Premium.
  • If \(S_T < K\): you are assigned and buy shares at \(K\).
    \(\text{P/L} = \text{Total Premium} + (S_T - K) \times \text{Shares}\)

Probability of Expiring OTM (Approximate)

If you enter implied volatility, the calculator uses a simple lognormal model (similar to Black–Scholes) to estimate the probability that the stock finishes above the strike at expiration. This is only an approximation and assumes:

  • Constant volatility and risk-free rate.
  • Lognormal price distribution.
  • No dividends or early exercise.

Interpreting the Results

1. Income vs. Risk

The premium and annualized yield show how much income you earn for tying up your cash. Compare the annualized yield to your risk-free rate or savings account to see if the extra risk is justified.

2. Breakeven and Downside Protection

The breakeven price is effectively your net cost basis if you are assigned. Selling a put below the current stock price gives you a margin of safety: the stock can fall to the breakeven and you still break even overall.

3. Assignment Scenarios

  • Stock stays above strike: you keep the premium and your cash is freed at expiration.
  • Stock falls slightly below strike: you are assigned, own the shares, and your effective cost is strike minus premium.
  • Stock falls sharply: losses on the shares can far exceed the premium received.

Cash-Secured Put FAQ

What is a cash-secured put?

A cash-secured put is a short put position backed by enough cash to buy the underlying shares if assigned. It is often used by investors who want to acquire stock at a discount while earning option premium.

Is a cash-secured put the same as a naked put?

No. A naked put is sold without setting aside the full cash needed to buy the shares, which creates leverage and higher risk. A cash-secured put is fully collateralized by cash, making it more conservative.

When does assignment happen?

For American-style options, assignment can happen at any time, but it is most common at or near expiration when the option is in the money. This calculator focuses on outcomes at expiration, which is how most risk profiles are analyzed.

How should I choose a strike and expiration?

Many investors pick:

  • A strike price where they would be comfortable owning the stock.
  • An expiration that balances premium income with the time their cash is tied up.
  • Implied volatility levels that offer attractive premiums relative to the risk.
