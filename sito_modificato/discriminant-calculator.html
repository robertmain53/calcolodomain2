The formula explained

Quadratic (degree 2): \( f(x) = ax^2 + bx + c,\; a\neq 0 \)

Discriminant: \( \Delta = b^2 - 4ac \).

Roots: \( x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{\Delta}}{2a} \).

Cubic (degree 3): \( f(x)=ax^3+bx^2+cx+d,\; a\neq 0 \)

Discriminant: \[ \Delta = 18abcd - 4b^3d + b^2c^2 - 4ac^3 - 27a^2d^2. \]

Interpretation: \( \Delta>0 \Rightarrow \) three distinct real roots; \( \Delta=0 \Rightarrow \) multiple root(s); \( \Delta<0 \Rightarrow \) one real and two nonreal complex conjugates.

Glossary of variables

  • a — leading coefficient (must be nonzero).
  • b, c, d — remaining coefficients of the polynomial.
  • \(\Delta\) — discriminant; sign determines the nature and multiplicity of roots.
  • Root nature — classification (two real, repeated, complex conjugates for quadratics; three real / repeated / one real + complex pair for cubics).

How it works: a step-by-step example

Example (Quadratic)

Let \( a=1, b=5, c=6 \). Then \( \Delta = b^2-4ac = 25-24=1 \). Since \( \Delta>0 \) and is a perfect square, there are two distinct rational roots: \( x=(-5\pm\sqrt{1})/2 = \{-2,-3\} \).

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Δ determine the exact roots?

Not by itself. It determines the nature of roots. For quadratics you can then use the quadratic formula; for cubics, Δ classifies multiplicity and reality patterns.

Why is a ≠ 0 required?

If a = 0, the polynomial degree drops (to linear for “quadratic”, to quadratic for “cubic”), so the corresponding discriminant formula no longer applies.

How precise are the numeric roots?

Quadratic roots are computed in double precision and shown with up to 10 significant digits. You can copy the values for further analysis.

What if Δ is negative for a quadratic?

There are two complex conjugate roots. The calculator will classify this case; numeric complex roots are not displayed to keep the UI focused.

Do you support higher degrees?

This tool focuses on degrees 2 and 3 for authoritative accuracy and clarity. Support for higher degrees may be added in a separate advanced tool.
