How this long-term care cost calculator works

Long-term care (LTC) can be one of the largest expenses in retirement. This calculator helps you estimate how much care might cost in the future and how much you may need to pay out of pocket.

1. Baseline monthly cost today

You can either start from typical national median costs by care type or enter a custom monthly quote. For presets, we use approximate national medians (rounded) in today’s dollars:

  • In-home care (aide 44 hrs/week): about $5,500/month
  • Assisted living facility: about $5,000/month
  • Nursing home (private room): about $9,500/month
  • Memory care: about $7,000/month (often 20–40% above assisted living)

The “Adjust for your area” slider scales these medians up or down (for example, 120% for a high-cost city, 80% for a lower-cost region).

Adjusted current monthly cost
\( \text{CurrentMonthly} = \text{BaselineMonthly} \times \dfrac{\text{Area%}}{100} \)

2. Projecting costs with inflation

Long-term care costs rarely stay flat. We project the cost forward from today until the year you expect care to begin using a compound inflation rate:

Future monthly cost at start of care
\( \text{FutureMonthly} = \text{CurrentMonthly} \times (1 + i)^{n} \)
where:
  • \( i \) = annual LTC inflation rate (e.g., 0.035 for 3.5%)
  • \( n \) = years until care may start

If you set “Years until care may start” to 0, the calculator assumes care begins now and skips the inflation step.

3. Total cost of care and your share

Once we know the projected monthly cost at the start of care, we estimate the total cost over the number of years you selected:

Total cost of care (all sources)
\( \text{TotalCost} = \text{FutureMonthly} \times 12 \times \text{YearsOfCare} \)

You can then specify what percentage you expect to be covered by long-term care insurance, Medicaid, or other sources. The remainder is your out-of-pocket share:

Your out-of-pocket cost
\( \text{OOP} = \text{TotalCost} \times \left(1 - \dfrac{\text{Coverage%}}{100}\right) \)

4. Simple savings target

To give you a rough savings goal, the calculator assumes you want to have the full out-of-pocket amount available at the start of care and that you earn no investment return (a conservative assumption):

Lump sum needed at start of care
\( \text{LumpSum} = \text{OOP} \)

Approximate monthly saving needed
\( \text{MonthlySaving} = \dfrac{\text{LumpSum}}{n \times 12} \)
where \( n \) is the years until care may start.

In reality, your investments may grow over time, which could reduce the monthly amount you need to save. A financial planner or advisor can refine this using your specific return and tax assumptions.

Choosing realistic assumptions

Inflation rate for long-term care

Consider the following when choosing an inflation rate:

  • 2–3%: closer to general inflation; may be optimistic for LTC.
  • 3–4%: common planning range for long-term care costs.
  • 5%+: conservative, often used for younger clients or high-cost markets.

How many years of care to plan for?

Average long-term care use is often cited as around 2–3 years, but there is wide variation. Some people never need care; others may need 5–10 years, especially with dementia or other chronic conditions.

  • 3–5 years: moderate planning horizon for many households.
  • 5–8+ years: more conservative, often used when there is a family history of cognitive decline.

Ways to pay for long-term care

This calculator only estimates costs; it does not recommend how to pay for them. Common funding sources include:

  • Personal savings and investments (taxable accounts, IRAs, 401(k)s, etc.).
  • Long-term care insurance or hybrid life/LTC policies.
  • Home equity (downsizing, reverse mortgage, or sale).
  • Family support (informal caregiving or financial help).
  • Medicaid for those who meet strict income and asset limits.

Medicare generally covers only short-term skilled nursing or rehabilitation—not ongoing custodial long-term care. That’s why advance planning is so important.

Limitations and next steps

This tool is for education and planning only. Actual long-term care costs will depend on your health, location, facility choice, and policy details if you have insurance. It does not replace personalized advice from a licensed financial planner, insurance professional, or elder law attorney.

Use the results as a starting point for conversations with your family and advisors about how you would prefer to receive care and how you might fund it.

Frequently asked questions

Is long-term care insurance worth the cost?

It depends on your age, health, assets, and risk tolerance. Insurance can protect savings from very high care costs, but premiums can be significant and may increase over time. Many people use a mix of insurance and self-funding. Comparing the projected cost of care from this calculator with potential policy benefits can help you decide.

What if I never need long-term care?

That is possible. Planning for LTC is about managing a risk that may or may not occur. Some people prefer to self-insure and accept the risk; others prefer to transfer some of the risk to an insurer. Even if you never need formal care, having extra savings can still support other retirement goals.

How often should I update my long-term care plan?

Consider revisiting your assumptions every few years, or after major life events (retirement, diagnosis of a chronic condition, death of a spouse, large move). Costs, inflation, and your health status can all change over time.
