How this aircraft range calculator works

This tool implements both the classical Breguet range equation and a simple fuel-burn model. It is designed for education, conceptual design, and quick “what‑if” studies, not for operational flight planning.

Breguet range equation for jets

For turbojets and turbofans in steady, level cruise:

\( R = \dfrac{V}{c_T} \dfrac{L}{D} \ln\left(\dfrac{W_i}{W_f}\right) \)
  • \(R\): range (m or ft)
  • \(V\): true airspeed (m/s)
  • \(c_T\): thrust-specific fuel consumption (1/s or 1/hr)
  • \(L/D\): lift-to-drag ratio in cruise
  • \(W_i, W_f\): initial and final weight (N or lbf)

In this calculator we use a time-based SFC (1/hr). Internally, the equation is applied in consistent SI units and the result is converted to nautical miles and kilometers.

Breguet range equation for propeller aircraft

For propeller-driven aircraft:

\( R = \dfrac{\eta_p V}{c_P} \dfrac{L}{D} \ln\left(\dfrac{W_i}{W_f}\right) \)
  • \(\eta_p\): propeller efficiency (typically 0.75–0.9)
  • \(c_P\): power-specific fuel consumption (1/hr)

The propeller case effectively replaces thrust with shaft power and accounts for propeller efficiency. The calculator lets you specify \(\eta_p\) explicitly.

Weight and fuel modelling

You can either:

  • Enter initial and final weight directly (e.g. from a performance manual), or
  • Enter operating empty weight, payload, fuel, and reserve. The tool then computes:
    • \(W_i = \text{OEW} + \text{payload} + \text{fuel}\)
    • \(W_f = W_i - \text{fuel used in cruise}\)

Simple fuel-burn model

When you know cruise fuel flow and fuel on board, the simple model uses:

Endurance (hours): \( E = \dfrac{F_\text{usable}}{\dot{F}} \)

Range (distance): \( R = V \cdot E \)

where \(F_\text{usable}\) is fuel available for cruise (total minus reserve), \(\dot{F}\) is fuel flow, and \(V\) is cruise speed.

Interpreting the results

  • Range: reported in nautical miles, kilometers, and statute miles.
  • Endurance: reported in hours and minutes.
  • Fuel fraction: the fraction of initial weight that is burned as fuel during cruise.
  • Weight ratio \(W_i/W_f\): appears inside the logarithm in the Breguet equation; higher values generally mean more range (up to structural and payload limits).

Assumptions and limitations

  • Steady, level, unaccelerated cruise at constant speed and altitude.
  • Constant lift-to-drag ratio and specific fuel consumption.
  • No wind, no climb/descent, no step climbs.
  • No regulatory reserves or alternate fuel beyond what you explicitly enter.

Real-world range will usually be lower than the ideal Breguet prediction because of climb, descent, holding, routing, winds aloft, temperature deviations, and operational constraints.

Practical tips

  • Use manufacturer data for realistic L/D and SFC values.
  • Include climb and reserve fuel in the weight breakdown if you want a more conservative range estimate.
  • For maximum range, aircraft are typically flown at a specific “max range” speed, not necessarily the normal cruise speed.
  • For maximum endurance, a lower speed (near minimum power required) is usually used.

Disclaimer: This calculator is for educational and preliminary estimation only. It does not replace certified performance data, flight manuals, or regulatory requirements. Do not use it as the sole basis for flight planning or operational decisions.

Aircraft range FAQ

What is aircraft range?

Aircraft range is the maximum distance an airplane can fly on a given amount of fuel under specified conditions. It depends on fuel on board, specific fuel consumption, lift-to-drag ratio, cruise speed, and the change in weight as fuel is burned.

What is the Breguet range equation?

The Breguet range equation relates range to aircraft efficiency and fuel fraction. For jets: \( R = (V / c_T) (L/D) \ln(W_i / W_f) \). For propeller aircraft: \( R = (\eta_p V / c_P) (L/D) \ln(W_i / W_f) \).

What is the difference between range and endurance?

Range is the distance flown; endurance is the time aloft. Maximum range is achieved at conditions that maximize distance per unit fuel, while maximum endurance is achieved at conditions that maximize time per unit fuel, usually at a lower speed.

How accurate is this aircraft range calculator?

It uses standard textbook equations and assumes idealized cruise. Real-world range will differ due to climb, descent, winds, temperature, and operational constraints. Treat the results as approximate and educational.

Can I use this for real flight planning?

No. Always use the aircraft flight manual, certified performance charts, and applicable regulations for flight planning. This tool is not a certified performance calculator.
