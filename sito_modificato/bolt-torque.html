Step-by-Step Calculation

The KFD Torque Formula ($T = K F d$)

The calculation of required bolt torque is crucial for maintaining the structural integrity of bolted joints, as torque is directly proportional to the bolt's axial tension (preload). The standard empirical formula is:

$$T = K \cdot F \cdot d$$

Where:

  • $\mathbf{T}$: The required torque (e.g., $N \cdot m$ or $ft \cdot lbf$).
  • $\mathbf{K}$: The dimensionless **Nut Factor** or **Friction Coefficient**. This accounts for friction in the threads and under the bolt head, which consumes about 90% of the input torque.
  • $\mathbf{F}$: The desired axial clamping force or **preload** (e.g., Newtons or Pounds-force).
  • $\mathbf{d}$: The nominal diameter of the bolt (e.g., meters or inches).

Understanding the K-Factor

The K-Factor is the most variable part of the calculation. Typical ranges include:

  • **Unlubricated (Dry Steel):** $K \approx 0.20$
  • **Zinc Plated:** $K \approx 0.17$
  • **Lubricated (Oil/Grease):** $K \approx 0.15$
  • **Anti-Seize/Waxes:** $K \approx 0.12$

Using the wrong K-Factor (e.g., using a dry value on a lubricated bolt) can lead to severe over-tightening and bolt failure.

Bolt Grades and Tension Targets

The target tension ($F$) is usually determined by the material strength of the bolt, typically aiming for 60% to 75% of the bolt's yield strength to ensure durability and prevent loosening from vibration.

Common Metric grades (e.g., 8.8, 10.9) and US Imperial grades (e.g., Grade 5, Grade 8) have standardized yield strengths which determine the maximum safe preload ($F$) you should aim for.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the K-Factor in the bolt torque formula?

The K-Factor is the dimensionless friction coefficient that accounts for thread friction and under-head friction. Since approximately 90% of the required torque is used to overcome friction, the K-Factor is critical. A typical value is 0.20 (dry/unlubricated), while lubricated bolts use lower values like 0.15 or 0.12.

Why is 75% of yield strength a common target for bolt tension?

The target clamping force (F) is often set to 75% of the bolt's proof load or yield strength. This ensures maximum clamping force without permanently stretching (yielding) the bolt material, providing durability and vibration resistance.

What is the difference between bolt grade 8.8 and 10.9?

Metric bolts are graded by their tensile strength. Grade 8.8 has a nominal tensile strength of 800 MPa and a yield strength of 640 MPa. Grade 10.9 is higher strength, with a nominal tensile strength of 1000 MPa and a yield strength of 900 MPa. Higher grades require higher torque for the same tension.

Why does lubrication reduce the required torque?

Torque is primarily needed to overcome friction. Lubrication significantly lowers the friction coefficient (K), meaning less torque is needed to achieve the same desired bolt tension (F). Using dry torque values on a lubricated bolt will severely over-tighten it.
