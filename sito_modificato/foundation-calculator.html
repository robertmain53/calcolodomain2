Data Source and Methodology

This calculator uses standard geometric formulas for volume calculation. All conversions between imperial and metric units, as well as pre-mix bag estimations, are based on industry-standard values referenced in construction and quantity surveying guides, such as the RSMeans Building Construction Cost Data.

All calculations are based strictly on the formulas and data provided by these sources. Always double-check your measurements and consult a professional engineer for load-bearing structural foundations.

The Formulas Explained

The core of this calculator is determining the total volume of your foundation shape.

1. Rectangular Slab / Pad / Footing

For any rectangular shape, the volume is found by multiplying its three dimensions.

$ Volume = Length \times Width \times Thickness $

2. Circular Piers / Columns

For cylindrical shapes, the volume is the area of the circular base multiplied by the height. This total is then multiplied by the number of piers (N).

$ Volume_{pier} = \pi \times (\frac{Diameter}{2})^2 \times Height $
$ Total \, Volume = N \times Volume_{pier} $

3. Unit Conversions

The calculator converts all inputs into a consistent base unit (cubic feet or cubic meters) before calculating the final volume, which is then presented in standard units for ordering.

  • $ 1 \text{ cubic yard} = 27 \text{ cubic feet} $
  • $ 1 \text{ cubic meter} = 1,000,000 \text{ cubic centimeters} $
  • $ 1 \text{ foot} = 12 \text{ inches} $
  • $ 1 \text{ meter} = 100 \text{ centimeters} $

Glossary of Variables

Length
The longest dimension of a rectangular slab or the total run of a strip footing.
Width
The shorter dimension of a rectangular slab or the width of a strip footing.
Thickness / Depth
The vertical height of the concrete pour.
Diameter
The measurement across the center of a circular pier.
Height
The vertical length of a pier or column.
Cubic Yard (cu yd)
The standard unit for ordering ready-mix concrete in the United States and other imperial-system regions. (3ft x 3ft x 3ft).
Cubic Meter (m³)
The standard unit for ordering concrete in metric-system regions. (1m x 1m x 1m).

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Let's calculate the concrete needed for a 10 ft x 12 ft patio slab that is 4 inches thick.

  1. Select Units: Choose 'Imperial'.
  2. Select Type: Choose 'Slab / Pad'.
  3. Convert Units: The calculator first converts all measurements to feet. The thickness is 4 inches, so: $ 4 \, \text{in} \div 12 \, \text{in/ft} = 0.333 \, \text{ft} $
  4. Calculate Volume (Cubic Feet): Using the formula $ V = L \times W \times T $: $ V = 12 \, \text{ft} \times 10 \, \text{ft} \times 0.333 \, \text{ft} = 39.96 \, \text{cu ft} $
  5. Convert to Cubic Yards: Since 1 cu yd = 27 cu ft: $ 39.96 \, \text{cu ft} \div 27 = 1.48 \, \text{cu yd} $
  6. Calculate Bags: If using 60 lb bags (which yield ~0.45 cu ft each): $ 39.96 \, \text{cu ft} \div 0.45 \, \text{cu ft/bag} \approx 89 \, \text{bags} $

The calculator would display 1.48 cu yd and 89 bags.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Should I add extra for waste?

Yes, absolutely. It is a standard industry practice to add a "waste factor" of 5% to 10% to your final volume. This covers spills, spillage during transport, uneven subgrade, and slight measurement inaccuracies. It is far better to have a small amount left over than to be short on a pour.

How accurate is the pre-mix bag estimate?

The bag estimate is based on the average yield-per-bag provided by manufacturers (e.g., a 60lb bag yields ~0.45 cu ft, an 80lb bag yields ~0.60 cu ft). This can vary slightly by brand. Always check the manufacturer's specific yield listed on the bag you are purchasing.

Can I use this for Sonotubes or other tube forms?

Yes. Select the 'Circular Piers / Columns' option. Enter the number of tubes you are filling, the inner diameter of the tube as the 'Diameter', and the length of the tube as the 'Height'.

What's the difference between a slab and a footing?

A slab is typically a large, flat area of concrete, like a patio, garage floor, or the base of a building. A strip footing is a continuous, narrow "strip" of concrete, usually poured in a trench, that runs under load-bearing walls to distribute their weight.

When should I order ready-mix concrete vs. use bags?

As a general rule, for any project requiring 1 cubic yard or more, it is significantly more cost-effective and time-efficient to order ready-mix concrete delivered by a truck. Mixing dozens or hundreds of bags by hand is extremely labor-intensive and prone to inconsistencies.
