How the self-directed IRA real estate calculator works

This calculator is designed specifically for self-directed IRA real estate deals, where your IRA buys a rental property directly, optionally using a non‑recourse loan. It helps you:

  • Estimate Year‑1 cash flow to the IRA after expenses and debt service.
  • Compare leveraged vs. all‑cash returns inside the IRA.
  • Approximate the share of income that may be exposed to UBIT when leverage is used.
  • Project IRA equity at exit based on appreciation and loan amortization.

Key formulas

1. Effective gross income (EGI)

Monthly rent × 12 × (1 − vacancy rate)

2. Net operating income (NOI)

NOI = EGI − annual operating expenses

3. Loan payment (amortizing, monthly)

Let L = loan amount, i = monthly interest rate, n = total number of payments:

\( \text{Payment} = L \times \dfrac{i(1+i)^n}{(1+i)^n - 1} \)

4. Year‑1 net cash flow to IRA

Net cash flow = NOI − annual debt service

5. Cash‑on‑cash return

Cash‑on‑cash = (Year‑1 net cash flow ÷ IRA cash invested) × 100%

6. Simplified UBIT exposure share

Debt‑financed share ≈ loan amount ÷ total project cost

Actual UBIT is calculated on Unrelated Debt‑Financed Income (UDFI) with depreciation and other adjustments on IRS Form 990‑T. This tool only gives a high‑level estimate of the portion of income that may be subject to UBIT when leverage is used.

Step‑by‑step: using the calculator

1. Enter property and income assumptions

Start with the purchase price, closing costs paid by the IRA, and the expected monthly rent. Use a realistic vacancy rate (e.g., 5–10%) and include all recurring expenses that must be paid from the IRA:

  • Property taxes and insurance
  • HOA or condo fees
  • Repairs and capital reserves
  • Property management fees
  • Utilities paid by the owner

2. Model IRA funding and non‑recourse loan

Enter how much cash your IRA will commit to this deal and the down payment percentage. The calculator will:

  • Compute the down payment = purchase price × down payment %.
  • Compute the loan amount = purchase price − down payment.
  • Add closing costs to determine total IRA cash invested.

Then set the interest rate and loan term for the non‑recourse loan. The tool calculates the fully amortizing monthly payment and annual debt service.

3. Set growth and holding period

Use conservative assumptions for rent growth, expense growth, and property appreciation. The holding period determines how long the IRA owns the property before a hypothetical sale.

At exit, the calculator estimates:

  • Future property value based on appreciation.
  • Approximate remaining loan balance (assuming constant amortization).
  • IRA equity = future value − remaining loan.

4. Interpret the results

  • Total project cost shows the economic size of the deal (price + closing).
  • IRA cash invested is the actual capital your IRA must commit.
  • Year‑1 net cash flow shows whether the property is likely to be cash‑flow positive to the IRA after debt service.
  • Cash‑on‑cash return lets you compare this deal to other IRA investments.
  • Leveraged vs. all‑cash IRR illustrates how debt can amplify returns (and risk).
  • Estimated UBIT share highlights how much of the income may be considered debt‑financed.

Self-directed IRA real estate rules to know

1. Prohibited transactions and disqualified persons

IRS rules strictly prohibit self‑dealing. Your IRA must invest for the exclusive benefit of the IRA, not you personally. Common disqualified persons include:

  • You and your spouse
  • Your parents, grandparents, children and grandchildren (and their spouses)
  • Entities you control (e.g., corporations or LLCs where you are a majority owner)

They generally cannot:

  • Live in or use the property.
  • Provide services like repairs or management in exchange for compensation.
  • Sell property to or buy property from the IRA.

2. All income and expenses must flow through the IRA

Rent checks must be paid to the IRA (or IRA LLC), and all expenses must be paid from IRA funds. You should not:

  • Pay expenses personally and reimburse yourself from the IRA.
  • Deposit rent into a personal account and then move it to the IRA.

Doing so can trigger a prohibited transaction and potentially disqualify the IRA.

3. Non‑recourse loans only

If you use leverage, the loan must be non‑recourse: the lender’s only collateral is the property itself. You cannot personally guarantee the loan or pledge other assets.

Non‑recourse loans often come with:

  • Higher interest rates
  • Larger required down payments
  • Stricter underwriting and reserve requirements

4. UBIT and UDFI

When an IRA uses debt to buy real estate, the portion of income and gain attributable to the debt‑financed part of the property may be subject to Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) on Unrelated Debt‑Financed Income (UDFI).

The calculator’s UBIT estimate is intentionally simplified. Actual UBIT calculations:

  • Use average acquisition indebtedness vs. average adjusted basis.
  • Allow depreciation and other deductions.
  • Are reported on IRS Form 990‑T.

Always work with a tax professional who understands self‑directed IRAs and UBIT before executing a leveraged deal.

Pros and cons of self-directed IRA real estate

Potential benefits

  • Diversification beyond stocks and bonds.
  • Tax‑advantaged growth of rental income and appreciation inside the IRA.
  • Leverage via non‑recourse loans can amplify returns (and losses).
  • Direct control over the type of property and market you invest in.

Key risks and drawbacks

  • Complex IRS rules and risk of prohibited transactions.
  • Illiquidity: real estate is harder to sell than securities.
  • Concentration risk if a large share of your retirement is in one property.
  • Potential UBIT on leveraged income and gains.
  • Custodian fees and higher financing costs for non‑recourse loans.

When this calculator is most useful

  • Comparing a self‑directed IRA real estate deal to keeping funds in traditional investments.
  • Testing different down payment and leverage scenarios.
  • Seeing how vacancy, expenses or appreciation assumptions affect IRA returns.
  • Preparing questions for your custodian, CPA or financial planner.

Always confirm your strategy with a qualified tax and investment professional before moving retirement funds into a self‑directed IRA real estate investment.
