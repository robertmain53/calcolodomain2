What is negative gearing?

In Australia, negative gearing happens when the costs of holding an investment (usually a rental property) are higher than the income it produces. The investment runs at a loss before tax, but that loss can generally be offset against your other income (such as salary), reducing your overall tax bill.

Many investors accept a small cash loss each year in the hope that capital growth on the property will more than compensate over time. The tax deduction from negative gearing reduces the size of the loss, but it does not turn a poor investment into a good one.

How this negative gearing calculator works

The calculator models a single year of rental income and expenses for a residential investment property. It separates:

  • Cash flow – money actually paid in or out (rent, interest, bills, repairs).
  • Non‑cash deductions – mainly depreciation and capital works.
  • Taxable income or loss – what the ATO taxes you on after deductions.

1. Loan amount and interest

Loan amount

\(\text{Loan} = \text{Purchase price} \times (1 - \text{Deposit \%}/100)\)

Interest (interest‑only)

\(\text{Interest} = \text{Loan} \times \text{Interest rate \%}/100\)

If you choose principal & interest, the calculator assumes a 30‑year term and uses the standard amortisation formula to estimate the annual repayment. It then splits that into:

  • Interest – tax‑deductible.
  • Principal – not tax‑deductible, but it reduces your loan balance.

2. Rental income

Annual rent is calculated as:

\(\text{Annual rent} = \text{Weekly rent} \times 52\)

If you want to allow for vacancy (e.g. 2 weeks empty per year), reduce the weekly rent or multiply by fewer weeks (e.g. 50 instead of 52).

3. Cash expenses

Cash expenses include interest plus all the running costs you enter:

  • Council and water rates
  • Insurance
  • Repairs and maintenance
  • Strata / body corporate fees
  • Property management fees
  • Other deductible expenses (e.g. advertising, travel where allowed)

Total cash expenses

\(\text{Cash expenses} = \text{Interest} + \text{Other cash costs}\)

4. Pre‑tax cash flow

Pre‑tax cash flow

\(\text{Pre‑tax cash flow} = \text{Annual rent} - \text{Cash expenses}\)

If this number is negative, your property is cash flow negative before tax.

5. Taxable income or loss

For tax purposes, you can usually deduct both cash expenses and eligible non‑cash deductions such as depreciation and capital works.

Taxable income

\(\text{Taxable income} = \text{Annual rent} - (\text{Cash expenses} + \text{Depreciation})\)

If taxable income is negative, you have a tax loss that can generally be offset against your other assessable income in that year (subject to ATO rules).

6. Tax impact and after‑tax cash flow

Tax impact

If taxable income < 0 (loss):
\(\text{Tax saving} = |\text{Taxable income}| \times \text{Tax rate}\)

If taxable income > 0 (profit):
\(\text{Extra tax} = \text{Taxable income} \times \text{Tax rate}\)

After‑tax cash flow

If loss:
\(\text{After‑tax cash flow} = \text{Pre‑tax cash flow} + \text{Tax saving}\)

If profit:
\(\text{After‑tax cash flow} = \text{Pre‑tax cash flow} - \text{Extra tax}\)

7. Effective yields

To give you a quick sense of scale, the calculator also shows the pre‑tax and after‑tax yields relative to the purchase price:

\(\text{Pre‑tax yield} = \dfrac{\text{Pre‑tax cash flow}}{\text{Purchase price}}\)

\(\text{After‑tax yield} = \dfrac{\text{After‑tax cash flow}}{\text{Purchase price}}\)

Interpreting your results

  • Negative pre‑tax cash flow means you are tipping in money each year to hold the property.
  • Tax saving reduces that loss but does not make the investment profitable on its own.
  • After‑tax cash flow shows the real impact on your pocket each year.
  • You should also consider capital growth, interest rate risk, vacancy risk and buffers.

Limitations and assumptions

  • Assumes you are an Australian resident individual for tax purposes.
  • Uses a single marginal tax rate including Medicare levy, not the full ATO tax scale.
  • Ignores land tax, CGT, offset accounts, redraw, and complex structures (trusts, companies).
  • Models one year only; it does not project multiple years or changing interest rates.

For personalised advice, speak with a licensed financial adviser or registered tax agent.

Negative gearing FAQ

Is negative gearing legal in Australia?

Yes. Under current Australian tax law, rental property losses can generally be offset against other income, subject to ATO rules. Policy settings can change over time, so always check the latest guidance.

Should I always aim for negative gearing?

No. Many investors now prefer neutral or positive cash flow properties, especially in a higher interest rate environment. Negative gearing can magnify gains in a rising market but also magnifies losses if prices fall or rents drop.

What expenses are usually deductible?

Common deductible expenses include interest on the investment loan, council and water rates, insurance, property management fees, repairs and maintenance, strata fees, and eligible depreciation and capital works. Some items have specific rules, so check the ATO website or ask your tax adviser.

Does this calculator replace tax advice?

No. This tool is for education and planning only. It simplifies the Australian tax system and cannot account for every personal circumstance. Always confirm important decisions with a qualified professional.
