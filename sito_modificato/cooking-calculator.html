How this cooking calculator works

This tool combines several kitchen helpers into one place so you don’t have to jump between multiple calculators while cooking or baking.

1. Time & temperature adjustment

When you change oven temperature, cooking time does not change perfectly in proportion, but a simple and practical model is:

Model used:

\[ t_{new} \approx t_{orig} \times \left(\frac{T_{orig,K}}{T_{new,K}}\right)^{1.5} \]

  • \(t_{orig}\) = original time (minutes)
  • \(T_{orig,K}\) = original temperature in Kelvin (°C + 273.15)
  • \(T_{new,K}\) = new temperature in Kelvin

This captures that hotter ovens cook faster but with diminishing returns. It’s still an estimate: always rely on visual cues and internal temperature for safety.

2. Recipe scaling

The scaling feature is straightforward:

\[ \text{Scale factor} = \frac{\text{new servings}}{\text{original servings}} \]

Each leading number in your ingredient lines is multiplied by this factor.

For example, scaling a 4-serving recipe to 10 servings gives a factor of 2.5, so “200 g flour” becomes “500 g flour”.

3. Cups to grams and back

Volume-to-weight conversions depend on ingredient density. This calculator uses typical reference values:

Ingredient Approx. g per cup
All-purpose flour120 g
Granulated sugar200 g
Brown sugar (packed)220 g
Butter227 g
Uncooked rice195 g
Water / stock / milk240 g

These are close enough for everyday cooking. For precision baking, a kitchen scale is still best.

4. Roast & turkey helper

For whole birds, a common rule of thumb is:

  • Turkey, no leftovers: ~0.45 kg (1 lb) per adult, half for a child.
  • Turkey, with leftovers: ~0.68 kg (1.5 lb) per adult.
  • Chicken: ~0.35–0.45 kg (0.75–1 lb) per adult.

Roasting time is then estimated as:

Baseline (un-stuffed turkey at 180°C / 350°F):

\[ t_{base} \approx 13 \text{ minutes per lb} \quad (\approx 29 \text{ min per kg}) \]

We then adjust slightly for oven temperature using the same temperature ratio model as above.

Again, these are guidelines. Always cook poultry to a safe internal temperature of at least 74°C / 165°F measured in the thickest part of the meat.

Safety and practical tips

  • For meat and poultry, use a reliable instant-read thermometer.
  • For baked goods, start checking 5–10 minutes before the estimated time when using a new oven.
  • When scaling recipes, very large batches may need slight adjustments to baking time and pan size.

Cooking calculator FAQ

How do I adjust cooking time for a different oven temperature?
Enter the original temperature and time, then your new oven temperature. The calculator uses a temperature ratio model to estimate the new time. Because every oven and recipe behaves a bit differently, start checking earlier than the estimate and rely on visual cues or a thermometer for final doneness.
Can I safely cook at a much lower or higher temperature?
Small changes (20–40°C / 25–75°F) are usually fine. Large changes can affect texture, browning, and food safety. Slow-cooking at low temperatures requires extra care; for meat and poultry, always ensure the internal temperature reaches a safe minimum regardless of oven setting or time estimate.
How accurate are the cups-to-grams conversions?
The calculator uses standard reference densities (e.g., 120 g per cup of flour, 200 g per cup of sugar). Real-world values can vary depending on how tightly ingredients are packed and humidity. For everyday cooking this is fine; for delicate baking, weigh ingredients on a scale when possible.
How much turkey do I need per person?
A simple rule is 0.45 kg (1 lb) of whole turkey per adult, or 0.68 kg (1.5 lb) if you want leftovers. Children typically eat about half an adult portion. The roast helper uses these guidelines and then estimates roasting time from the total weight and oven temperature.
