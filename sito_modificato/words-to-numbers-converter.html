Data Source & Methodology

We implement the short-scale naming convention (million = 106, billion = 109, …) with an option to switch to the long scale (billion = 1012). Parsing rules follow U.S. Government Publishing Office Style Manual guidelines and linguistics references on English number word tokenization.

  • GPO Style Manual (2016)
  • Long vs. short scale overview

The Parsing Formula

Tokenization → normalize hyphens, ordinals, and filler words.
Segment folding → accumulate small numbers and apply hundred multipliers.
Scale promotion → when a scale word \( M \in \{10^3,10^6,\dots\} \) appears, compute total += segment × M and reset the segment.
Decimals → everything after “point” is interpreted as individual digits.
Final value → sign × (total + segment + decimal).

Worked Example

Input: negative one hundred twenty-three thousand and forty-five point six seven

  1. Sign = negative → -1.
  2. Segment folding: one hundred twenty-three → 123; “thousand” promotes to 123 000.
  3. Remainder: forty-five → 45; cumulative = 123 045.
  4. Decimal: point six seven → 0.67.
  5. Result: -123 045.67

Frequently Asked Questions

Which English variants are supported?

Modern short-scale English by default. Disable the short-scale toggle to interpret billion = 1012, trillion = 1018, and to accept milliard / billiard.

How do ordinals work?

When “Normalize ordinals” is enabled, common ordinals such as “twenty-first” are rewritten to “twenty one” prior to parsing.

What limits should I keep in mind?

We cap absolute values at 1018 and allow decimal precision up to 18 places to avoid overflow.

Can I paste messy text?

We ignore commas and hyphens automatically. Unknown words raise clarification errors so you can fix typos quickly.
