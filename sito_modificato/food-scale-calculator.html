How this food scale calculator works

Measuring by weight is the most accurate way to cook and bake. This food scale calculator converts between volume units (cups, tablespoons, teaspoons, milliliters) and weight units (grams, ounces) using ingredient-specific densities.

Core formulas

1. Volume → Weight

\(\text{Weight (g)} = \text{Volume (mL)} \times \text{Density (g/mL)}\)

2. Weight → Volume

\(\text{Volume (mL)} = \dfrac{\text{Weight (g)}}{\text{Density (g/mL)}}\)

The calculator converts cups/tbsp/tsp to milliliters using standard US measures, then applies the ingredient density to get grams or ounces.

Why grams per cup are different for each ingredient

A cup is a measure of space, not weight. A cup of feathers weighs less than a cup of sand because the density is different. The same is true in the kitchen:

  • 1 cup water ≈ 240 g
  • 1 cup all-purpose flour ≈ 120 g
  • 1 cup granulated sugar ≈ 200 g
  • 1 cup butter ≈ 227 g

That’s why generic “1 cup = 240 g” conversions are misleading. This calculator uses ingredient-specific densities to give you realistic values.

Accuracy and limitations

The densities used here are based on common food science and nutrition references and assume typical packing (e.g., spooned and leveled flour). Real-world values can vary due to:

  • How tightly ingredients are packed into the cup
  • Grind size (e.g., fine vs coarse salt or sugar)
  • Moisture content and brand differences

For critical recipes (especially baking), always treat the calculator as a guide and rely on your food scale as the final authority.

Tips for using a food scale effectively

  • Always tare: Put your empty bowl on the scale and press Tare before adding ingredients.
  • Weigh directly into the bowl: Add ingredients one by one, taring between each, to reduce dishes and improve accuracy.
  • Prefer grams over ounces: Grams give finer resolution and are standard in professional recipes.
  • Stick to one system: Avoid mixing cups and grams in the same recipe when possible.
