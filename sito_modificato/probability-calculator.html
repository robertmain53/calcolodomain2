Data Source and Methodology

Probability is a fundamental branch of mathematics concerning numerical descriptions of how likely an event is to occur. All calculations performed by this tool are strictly based on the foundational axioms and theorems of probability theory, as outlined in authoritative academic texts.

  • Authoritative Source: Ross, S. (2019). A First Course in Probability (10th ed.). Pearson.
  • Methodology: The calculator applies standard formulas for simple, compound, and conditional probability. All inputs are treated as standard probabilities $P$ where $0 \le P \le 1$.

The Formulas Explained

1. Probability of a Single Event: P(A)

The probability of a single event $A$ is the ratio of the number of favorable outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes.

$ P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of Favorable Outcomes}}{\text{Total Number of Outcomes}} $

The probability that event $A$ does not occur (its complement, $P(A')$) is:

$ P(A') = 1 - P(A) $

2. Probability of Two Independent Events

Two events, $A$ and $B$, are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. (e.g., flipping two separate coins).

Probability of A AND B (Intersection, $P(A \cap B)$):

$ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) $

Probability of A OR B (Union, $P(A \cup B)$):

$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) $

3. Probability of Two Dependent Events

Two events are dependent if the occurrence of one does affect the probability of the other (e.g., drawing two cards from a deck without replacement). This involves conditional probability, $P(B|A)$, which reads "the probability of B, given A has already occurred."

Probability of A AND B (Intersection, $P(A \cap B)$):

$ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A) $

Probability of A OR B (Union, $P(A \cup B)$):

$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) $

(Note: To find $P(B)$ in a dependent scenario, you would need more information, such as $P(B|A')$ and $P(A')$, using the law of total probability.)

Glossary of Variables

  • $P(A)$: The probability that event A will occur. A decimal value between 0 and 1.
  • $P(B)$: The probability that event B will occur.
  • $P(A')$: The probability of the complement of A (i.e., that event A will not occur).
  • $P(B')$: The probability that event B will not occur.
  • $P(A \cap B)$: The probability that both A and B will occur. Also written as $P(A \text{ and } B)$.
  • $P(A \cup B)$: The probability that either A or B (or both) will occur. Also written as $P(A \text{ or } B)$.
  • $P(B|A)$: The conditional probability that event B will occur, given that event A has already occurred.

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Scenario: You are drawing cards from a standard 52-card deck.

Case 1: Independent Events (with replacement) You want to find the probability of drawing a King (Event A), replacing it, and then drawing a Queen (Event B).

  1. Find P(A): There are 4 Kings in a 52-card deck. $ P(A) = 4 / 52 = 0.0769 $
  2. Find P(B): Since you replaced the first card, there are still 4 Queens in the 52-card deck. $ P(B) = 4 / 52 = 0.0769 $
  3. Select the 'Independent Events' tab.
  4. Input: $ P(A) = 0.0769 $ and $ P(B) = 0.0769 $.
  5. Result $P(A \text{ and } B)$ (Intersection): The calculator finds $ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B) = 0.0769 \times 0.0769 = 0.0059 $ (or 0.59%).

Case 2: Dependent Events (without replacement) You want to find the probability of drawing a King (Event A) and, without replacing it, drawing a Queen (Event B).

  1. Find P(A): $ P(A) = 4 / 52 = 0.0769 $
  2. Find P(B|A): This is the probability of drawing a Queen, given you already drew a King. There are now only 51 cards left, but all 4 Queens are still in the deck. $ P(B|A) = 4 / 51 = 0.0784 $
  3. Select the 'Dependent Events' tab.
  4. Input: $ P(A) = 0.0769 $ and $ P(B|A) = 0.0784 $.
  5. Result $P(A \text{ and } B)$ (Intersection): The calculator finds $ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A) = 0.0769 \times 0.0784 = 0.0060 $ (or 0.60%).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between independent and dependent events?

Independent events are events that do not affect each other. For example, flipping a coin twice. The outcome of the first flip (heads or tails) has no impact on the outcome of the second flip. Dependent events are events where the outcome of one affects the outcome of the other. For example, drawing two cards from a deck without replacement. The probability of drawing a King on the second draw depends on whether a King was drawn on the first draw.

What does a probability of 0 mean?

A probability of 0 (or 0%) means the event is impossible. For example, the probability of rolling a 7 on a standard six-sided die is 0.

What does a probability of 1 mean?

A probability of 1 (or 100%) means the event is certain to happen. For example, the probability of rolling a number less than 7 on a standard six-sided die is 1.

Can probability be negative or greater than 1?

No. The probability of any event must be a value between 0 and 1, inclusive (or 0% and 100%). This calculator will show an error if you enter values outside this range.

How is probability different from 'odds'?

Probability measures the likelihood of an event happening (favorable outcomes / total outcomes). Odds compare the likelihood of an event happening to it not happening (favorable outcomes : unfavorable outcomes). For example, the probability of rolling a 2 on a die is 1/6. The odds of rolling a 2 are 1:5.

What is P(A or B)?

$P(A \text{ or } B)$, or $P(A \cup B)$, is the probability that at least one of the events will occur. The formula $P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$ is used to avoid double-counting the scenario where both events happen.
