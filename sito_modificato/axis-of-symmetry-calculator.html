The formula explained

For \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\\) with \(a\\neq 0\), the axis of symmetry is

\(x = -\\dfrac{b}{2a}\).

The vertex is \(\\big(h,k\\big)\\) with \(h=-\\dfrac{b}{2a}\\) and \(k=f(h)=ah^2+bh+c\\).

The discriminant is \(\\Delta=b^2-4ac\\), determining root nature.

If real roots \(r_1, r_2\\) exist, then the axis can also be written as \(x=\\dfrac{r_1+r_2}{2}\\).

Glossary of variables

  • a — coefficient of \(x^2\) (must be non-zero).
  • b — coefficient of \(x\).
  • c — constant term.
  • h — \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex, \(h=-\\dfrac{b}{2a}\\).
  • k — \(y\)-coordinate of the vertex, \(k=f(h)\\).
  • \(\Delta\) — discriminant \(b^2-4ac\\).
  • Axis — vertical line \(x=h\\) that mirrors the parabola.

How it works: a step-by-step example

Example

Given \(f(x)=2x^2-8x+3\\):

  1. Compute \(h=-\\dfrac{b}{2a}=-\\dfrac{-8}{2\\cdot 2}=\\dfrac{8}{4}=2\\).
  2. Compute \(k=f(2)=2(2)^2-8(2)+3=8-16+3=-5\\).
  3. The axis is \(x=h=2\\). Vertex is \((2,-5)\\).
  4. Discriminant \(\\Delta=b^2-4ac=(-8)^2-4(2)(3)=64-24=40>0\\Rightarrow\\) two real roots; the axis equals \((r_1+r_2)/2=2\\).

Frequently asked questions

Is the axis always vertical?

For quadratics in the form \(y=ax^2+bx+c\\), yes — the symmetry axis is a vertical line \(x=h\\).

What if I only know the roots?

Use the Roots mode. The axis is simply the midpoint: \(x=(r_1+r_2)/2\\).

Do complex roots change the axis?

No. The axis formula \(x=-b/(2a)\\) holds regardless of whether the roots are real or complex.

How does changing \(a\) affect the graph?

The sign of \(a\) controls whether the parabola opens up (\(a>0\)) or down (\(a<0\)); its magnitude affects how “wide” it is. The axis remains \(x=-b/(2a)\\).

Can a linear function have an axis of symmetry?

No. If \(a=0\\), the function is linear and there is no parabolic axis of symmetry.
