How this semaphore translator works

This tool implements the standard two‑flag semaphore system used in maritime and scouting contexts. Each letter or digit is represented by a unique combination of left and right arm positions. The translator:

  • Normalizes your input (uppercases letters, strips unsupported characters).
  • Maps each character to a pair of arm positions (0–7) and corresponding angles.
  • Animates a stick figure holding two flags at those angles.
  • Lets you step through the message manually or play it automatically at adjustable speed.
  • Can also decode a sequence of positions like 0-3 1-5 2-6 back into text.

Angle system used

We number arm positions from 0 to 7, starting at the top and moving clockwise:

  • 0 – 90° (straight up)
  • 1 – 45° (up‑right)
  • 2 – 0° (right)
  • 3 – −45° (down‑right)
  • 4 – −90° (straight down)
  • 5 – −135° (down‑left)
  • 6 – 180° (left)
  • 7 – 135° (up‑left)

The left and right arms are rotated independently using these discrete angles. The combination of both arms uniquely identifies a character (with a few reserved combinations for control symbols in the full standard).

Tips for learning semaphore quickly

  • Start with your own name and watch the animation several times.
  • Enable NATO phonetic hints to associate each letter with a word (ALFA, BRAVO, CHARLIE…).
  • Practice decoding: look at the animated figure and try to guess the letter before reading it.
  • Print or save the chart above as a cheat sheet for offline practice.

FAQ

Which semaphore standard does this tool use?

The translator follows the common international two‑flag semaphore alphabet used in maritime signaling and scouting, with one unique arm‑position pair per letter A–Z and digits 0–9. Some control symbols from the full code (such as “numerals” and “rest”) are simplified here for ease of use.

Can I use this for real maritime communication?

It’s great for learning and practice, but real‑world safety‑critical communication at sea should always follow your organization’s official procedures and training. Use this tool as an educational aid, not as a substitute for certified signaling equipment or training.

How do I enter spaces and punctuation?

Spaces are preserved between words. Supported punctuation includes period (.), comma (,), question mark (?), exclamation mark (!) and slash (/). Unsupported characters are ignored during translation.

How do I decode a flag sequence back to text?

Switch to the Flags → Text tab and enter positions in the format L-R where L is the left arm position and R is the right arm position, both from 0 to 7. Separate characters with spaces, for example:

0-3 1-5 2-6 X 0-2 1-4

Use X as a special token for a space between words. The decoded text will appear on the right.
