How to convert Torr to mmHg

Both Torr and millimeters of mercury (mmHg) are non‑SI units of pressure commonly used in vacuum technology, physics, and medicine (e.g. blood pressure).

Exact definitions

  • \(1 \text{ Torr} = \dfrac{1}{760} \text{ atm} = \dfrac{101325}{760} \text{ Pa} \approx 133.322 \text{ Pa}\)
  • \(1 \text{ mmHg} \approx 133.3224 \text{ Pa}\) (at 0 °C, standard gravity)

Because the numerical values in pascals are almost identical, the conversion between Torr and mmHg is effectively 1:1 for nearly all practical purposes.

Conversion formulas

Approximate (sufficient for most uses)

\[ P_{\text{mmHg}} \approx P_{\text{Torr}} \]

\[ P_{\text{Torr}} \approx P_{\text{mmHg}} \]

More precise (via pascals)

\[ P_{\text{mmHg}} = P_{\text{Torr}} \times \frac{101325/760}{133.3224} \]

\[ P_{\text{Torr}} = P_{\text{mmHg}} \times \frac{133.3224}{101325/760} \]

Numerically, these factors differ from 1 by less than 0.000015%.

Example: 760 Torr to mmHg

Using the approximate relationship:

\[ 760 \text{ Torr} \approx 760 \text{ mmHg} \]

This corresponds to standard atmospheric pressure.

When does the small difference matter?

The tiny difference between Torr and mmHg only becomes relevant in:

  • high‑precision metrology and calibration work
  • scientific publications that specify a particular unit definition
  • legal or standards‑driven measurements where the exact definition is mandated

For routine vacuum measurements, blood pressure readings, and most lab work, treating 1 Torr = 1 mmHg is entirely acceptable.

FAQ

Is Torr an SI unit?

No. The SI unit of pressure is the pascal (Pa). Torr and mmHg are legacy units that remain popular in specific fields such as vacuum technology, plasma physics, and medicine.

Why are Torr and mmHg so similar?

Historically, pressure was measured using mercury barometers, where the height of a mercury column (in millimeters) indicated pressure. The Torr was later defined as exactly 1/760 of a standard atmosphere, which numerically matches the traditional value of 760 mmHg at standard conditions, making the two units nearly identical.

How do Torr and mmHg relate to atmospheres?

  • \(1 \text{ atm} = 760 \text{ Torr}\)
  • \(1 \text{ atm} \approx 760 \text{ mmHg}\)

This is why 760 Torr and 760 mmHg are both commonly used as shorthand for “one atmosphere” of pressure.
