What is ABV?

ABV stands for Alcohol by Volume. It is the standard way to express how much pure ethanol is present in an alcoholic drink, as a percentage of the total volume.

For example, a beer labeled 5% ABV contains about 5 mL of pure alcohol in every 100 mL of beer (measured at 20 °C).

ABV formulas used in this calculator

1. Simple OG–FG ABV formula

ABV [%] ≈ (OG − FG) × 131.25

This classic homebrew formula is quick and works well for most standard‑strength beers and ciders.

2. Advanced ABV formula (more accurate)

ABV [%] ≈ 76.08 × (OG − FG) / (1.775 − OG) × (FG / 0.794)

This empirical formula better accounts for the changing density of the solution as sugar is converted to alcohol and CO₂. It is usually closer to lab measurements, especially for stronger beers and wines.

3. Converting Brix / Plato to specific gravity

When you enter Brix or Plato, the calculator internally converts them to specific gravity using:

SG ≈ 1 + (Brix / (258.6 − ((Brix / 258.2) × 227.1)))

Plato and Brix are numerically very close for brewing purposes, so the same conversion is used.

4. Dilution and blending ABV

When you dilute or blend two liquids, the total amount of alcohol is conserved:

Final ABV = (V₁ × ABV₁ + V₂ × ABV₂) / (V₁ + V₂)

where volumes are in the same units (L, gal, etc.) and ABV is in percent.

How to use this ABV calculator

  1. Measure OG before fermentation with a hydrometer or refractometer.
  2. Ferment completely until gravity readings are stable for at least 2–3 days.
  3. Measure FG at the end of fermentation.
  4. Enter OG and FG in the OG/FG tab and choose simple or advanced formula.
  5. Read the result and optionally check attenuation and alcohol units per serving.

Typical ABV ranges

  • Light beer / session IPA: 3.0–4.5% ABV
  • Pale ale / lager: 4.5–5.5% ABV
  • IPA / strong lager: 5.5–7.5% ABV
  • Imperial stout / barleywine: 8–12% ABV
  • Table wine: 10–13.5% ABV
  • Fortified wine: 15–22% ABV
  • Spirits (vodka, gin, whisky): ~40% ABV

Frequently asked questions about ABV

Is this ABV calculator only for beer?

No. The formulas work for beer, cider, mead and wine as long as you have reliable gravity or Brix/Plato readings and a complete fermentation.

Why do I get different ABV values with different formulas?

All ABV formulas are approximations. The simple formula assumes a linear relationship between gravity drop and alcohol. The advanced formula better models the changing density of the solution. Differences of 0.1–0.3% ABV are normal.

How does priming sugar affect ABV?

Priming sugar for bottle conditioning adds a small amount of extra alcohol, typically +0.1 to +0.3% ABV depending on the dose. This calculator does not explicitly add priming sugar, but you can approximate it by treating the primed beer as Batch 1 and adding a small Batch 2 with the sugar’s equivalent alcohol.

Can I use this to check if my beer is legal to sell?

No. Commercial labeling and taxation usually require lab analysis or certified methods. This calculator is designed for homebrew and educational use.
