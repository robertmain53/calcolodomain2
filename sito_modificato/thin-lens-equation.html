Thin Lens Equation: Definition and Formula

The thin lens equation relates the focal length of a lens to the distances of the object and image from the lens. It is a cornerstone of geometric optics and is widely used in physics, engineering, photography, and vision science.

Thin lens equation

\[ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} \]

  • \(f\) = focal length of the lens
  • \(d_o\) = object distance (distance from object to lens)
  • \(d_i\) = image distance (distance from image to lens)

The linear magnification \(m\) produced by the lens is given by:

Magnification

\[ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} \]

  • \(|m| > 1\): image is larger than the object (magnified)
  • \(|m| < 1\): image is smaller than the object (reduced)
  • \(m < 0\): image is inverted
  • \(m > 0\): image is upright

Sign Conventions Used in This Calculator

There are several sign conventions in optics (e.g., real-is-positive, Gaussian, Cartesian). This calculator uses a common real-is-positive convention:

  • Object distance \(d_o\): positive for real objects (on the incoming-light side of the lens).
  • Image distance \(d_i\):
    • \(d_i > 0\): real image, on the opposite side of the lens from the object.
    • \(d_i < 0\): virtual image, on the same side as the object.
  • Focal length \(f\):
    • \(f > 0\): converging (convex) lens.
    • \(f < 0\): diverging (concave) lens.

In the interface, you normally enter positive distances for real objects and images. The lens type (converging or diverging) determines the sign of \(f\). The calculator then computes \(d_i\) (which may be positive or negative) and interprets the result for you.

Rearranging the Thin Lens Equation

You can solve the thin lens equation for any of the three distances.

Solve for image distance \(d_i\)

Starting from \(\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}\):

\[ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} \] \[ d_i = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{f} - \dfrac{1}{d_o}} \]

Solve for object distance \(d_o\)

\[ \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_i} \] \[ d_o = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{f} - \dfrac{1}{d_i}} \]

Solve for focal length \(f\)

\[ \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} \] \[ f = \frac{1}{\dfrac{1}{d_o} + \dfrac{1}{d_i}} \]

Worked Examples

1. Converging lens forming a real, inverted image

Suppose a converging lens has focal length \(f = 10\ \text{cm}\), and an object is placed at \(d_o = 30\ \text{cm}\).

Compute the image distance:

\[ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{3 - 1}{30} = \frac{2}{30} = \frac{1}{15} \] \[ d_i = 15\ \text{cm} \] \[ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{15}{30} = -0.5 \]

Interpretation: the image is real (\(d_i > 0\)), inverted (\(m < 0\)), and reduced in size (\(|m| = 0.5\)).

2. Converging lens forming a virtual, upright image

Now place the object closer to the same lens: \(f = 10\ \text{cm}\), \(d_o = 5\ \text{cm}\) (inside the focal length).

\[ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{10} - \frac{1}{5} = \frac{1 - 2}{10} = -\frac{1}{10} \] \[ d_i = -10\ \text{cm} \] \[ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-10}{5} = 2 \]

Interpretation: the image is virtual (\(d_i < 0\)), upright (\(m > 0\)), and magnified (\(|m| = 2\)).

3. Diverging lens

For a diverging lens with focal length \(f = -15\ \text{cm}\) and an object at \(d_o = 30\ \text{cm}\):

\[ \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o} = \frac{1}{-15} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{2}{30} - \frac{1}{30} = -\frac{3}{30} = -\frac{1}{10} \] \[ d_i = -10\ \text{cm} \] \[ m = -\frac{d_i}{d_o} = -\frac{-10}{30} = \frac{1}{3} \]

The image is virtual, upright, and reduced, as expected for a diverging lens with a real object.

FAQ

What is the thin lens equation?

The thin lens equation is \(\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}\). It assumes the lens thickness is negligible compared with the object and image distances, so refraction can be treated as occurring at a single plane.

How do I know if the image is real or virtual?

Using the sign convention in this calculator:

  • \(d_i > 0\): image is real, on the opposite side of the lens from the object.
  • \(d_i < 0\): image is virtual, on the same side as the object.

What do positive and negative focal lengths mean?

A positive focal length corresponds to a converging (convex) lens, which can form real images of real objects. A negative focal length corresponds to a diverging (concave) lens, which forms only virtual images for real objects.

How is magnification related to the thin lens equation?

Once you know \(d_o\) and \(d_i\), the magnification is \(m = -d_i/d_o\). The sign and magnitude of \(m\) tell you whether the image is inverted or upright and whether it is larger or smaller than the object.

When is the thin lens approximation valid?

The approximation is valid when:

  • the lens thickness is small compared with \(d_o\) and \(d_i\), and
  • rays make small angles with the optical axis (paraxial approximation).

For thick lenses, strongly curved lenses, or wide-angle systems, more advanced models (e.g., thick lens or matrix optics) are needed.
