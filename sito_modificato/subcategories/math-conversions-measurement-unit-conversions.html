About the Mohs scale

The Mohs scale ranks minerals by scratch resistance from 1 (talc) to 10 (diamond). It’s ordinal and non-linear: a higher number means a material that can scratch all lower numbers, but differences between adjacent numbers aren’t equal.

“Absolute” hardness (relative ratios)

A commonly cited relative progression assigns these values to the Mohs reference minerals:

[1, 2, 9, 21, 48, 72, 100, 200, 400, 1600] for Mohs 1→10 (talc→diamond).

These figures reflect relative scratch resistance and are useful for intuition and comparison (e.g., diamond ≈16× quartz), but they are not standardized indentation hardness values like Vickers or Brinell.

Scratch tests vs common objects

  • Fingernail ≈ 2.5
  • Copper coin ≈ 3
  • Knife/steel nail ≈ 5–5.5
  • Window glass ≈ 5.5–6
  • Steel file ≈ 6.5

FAQs

Can I convert Mohs to Vickers or Brinell?

Not directly. Mohs is based on scratch resistance; Vickers and Brinell measure indentation hardness and depend on method and load. Use dedicated charts for the specific material and test conditions.

Why is diamond so far ahead?

The diamond’s relative “absolute” value (≈1600) shows its scratch resistance is dramatically higher than corundum (≈400), illustrating the non-linearity of the Mohs sequence.
