What is the PaO2/FiO2 (P/F) ratio?

The PaO2/FiO2 ratio, often called the P/F ratio or Horowitz index, is a simple index of oxygenation:

\( \text{P/F ratio} = \dfrac{\text{PaO}_2\ (\text{mmHg})}{\text{FiO}_2} \)

It compares the arterial oxygen tension (PaO2) from an arterial blood gas (ABG) to the fraction of inspired oxygen (FiO2) the patient is receiving. Lower values indicate worse oxygen transfer from the lungs to the blood and more severe hypoxemia.

Normal values and ARDS severity thresholds

The Berlin definition of ARDS uses the P/F ratio (with PEEP or CPAP ≥ 5 cmH2O) to grade severity:

P/F ratio (mmHg) Interpretation
> 300 Normal oxygenation / no ARDS
201 – 300 Mild ARDS
101 – 200 Moderate ARDS
≤ 100 Severe ARDS

Thresholds assume bilateral opacities on imaging, respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload, and PEEP/CPAP ≥ 5 cmH2O.

Step-by-step: how to calculate the P/F ratio

  1. Obtain PaO2 from an ABG. Most labs report PaO2 in mmHg; some use kPa.
  2. Determine FiO2. Express it as a decimal between 0.21 and 1.0 (e.g. 40% O2 → 0.40).
  3. Convert units if needed. If PaO2 is in kPa, convert to mmHg: \( \text{PaO}_2(\text{mmHg}) = \text{PaO}_2(\text{kPa}) \times 7.5 \)
  4. Apply the formula. Divide PaO2 (mmHg) by FiO2.

Example 1 – patient on 40% oxygen

A ventilated patient has PaO2 = 80 mmHg on FiO2 = 0.40:

\( \text{P/F} = \dfrac{80}{0.40} = 200 \)

P/F = 200 → moderate ARDS (assuming PEEP ≥ 5 cmH2O and ARDS criteria otherwise met).

Example 2 – PaO2 in kPa

PaO2 = 10 kPa on FiO2 = 0.50:

\( \text{PaO}_2(\text{mmHg}) = 10 \times 7.5 = 75 \text{ mmHg} \)
\( \text{P/F} = \dfrac{75}{0.50} = 150 \)

P/F = 150 → moderate ARDS.

Clinical notes and limitations

  • The P/F ratio is instantaneous and can change quickly with FiO2, PEEP, or patient position.
  • It does not account for shunt fraction, lung compliance, or PaCO2.
  • For ARDS diagnosis, combine the P/F ratio with imaging, timing of onset, and exclusion of cardiogenic edema.
  • In spontaneously breathing patients on variable oxygen devices (e.g. nasal cannula), FiO2 is an estimate and the P/F ratio is less precise.

Frequently asked questions

Is a higher P/F ratio better?

Yes. A higher P/F ratio indicates better oxygenation. Values above 300 are generally considered normal, while values below 300 suggest impaired gas exchange and possible ARDS if other criteria are met.

Can I use the P/F ratio outside of ARDS?

The P/F ratio is widely used beyond ARDS—for example, to track oxygenation trends in pneumonia, sepsis, or post-operative respiratory failure, and in scoring systems such as MODS. Always interpret it in the full clinical context.

What is the difference between P/F ratio and A–a gradient?

The P/F ratio is simpler and does not require barometric pressure or PaCO2. The alveolar–arterial (A–a) gradient is more physiologic but more complex to calculate. Many ICU protocols favor the P/F ratio for its speed and ease of use.
