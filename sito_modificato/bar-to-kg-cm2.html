How to Convert Bar to kg/cm²

Both bar and kilogram-force per square centimeter (kg/cm²) are pressure units commonly used in engineering, hydraulics, pneumatics, and industrial instrumentation.

Exact conversion factors

  • 1 bar = 1,000,000 Pa (pascals)
  • 1 kg/cm² ≈ 98,066.5 Pa

Therefore:
1 bar = 1,000,000 / 98,066.5 ≈ 1.019716 kg/cm²

Formula: bar to kg/cm²

kg/cm² = bar × 1.019716

Formula: kg/cm² to bar

bar = kg/cm² ÷ 1.019716

Worked examples

Example 1 – Convert 5 bar to kg/cm²

Using the formula:

kg/cm² = 5 × 1.019716 ≈ 5.0986 kg/cm²

Example 2 – Convert 10 kg/cm² to bar

bar = 10 ÷ 1.019716 ≈ 9.8041 bar

What are bar and kg/cm²?

Bar

  • Unit of pressure widely used in industry and meteorology.
  • 1 bar = 100,000 Pa = 100 kPa.
  • Close to average atmospheric pressure at sea level (≈ 1.01325 bar).

kg/cm² (kilogram-force per square centimeter)

  • Legacy engineering unit based on kilogram-force, not pure SI.
  • 1 kg/cm² ≈ 98.0665 kPa.
  • Still common in hydraulic systems, tire pressure specs, and older gauges.

Practical tips

  • For quick mental estimates, you can approximate 1 bar ≈ 1 kg/cm², but for design or safety calculations always use the precise factor.
  • Check whether your value is gauge (relative to atmosphere) or absolute pressure; the numerical conversion factor is the same, but the reference point differs.
  • When converting specifications from datasheets, keep at least 3–4 decimal places to avoid rounding errors in calculations.

FAQ: Bar to kg/cm²

Is 1 bar exactly equal to 1 kg/cm²?

No. 1 bar is approximately 1.019716 kg/cm². The two units are close, which is why they are sometimes treated as equivalent in rough estimates, but they are not identical.

Why is kg/cm² considered a non-SI unit?

The kilogram is a unit of mass, not force. In kg/cm², the “kg” actually refers to kilogram-force, which depends on the acceleration due to gravity (≈ 9.80665 m/s²). Modern SI uses the pascal (Pa) based on newtons per square meter (N/m²), which is independent of local gravity.

Which unit should I use in new designs?

For new engineering designs, standards recommend using Pa or bar. kg/cm² is mainly kept for compatibility with older equipment, gauges, and documentation.

Does the conversion factor change with temperature or altitude?

No. The conversion between bar and kg/cm² is purely mathematical and does not depend on temperature or altitude. What may change is whether you are measuring gauge or absolute pressure, but the unit conversion factor stays the same.
