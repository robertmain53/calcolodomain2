How Missouri local earnings tax works

Missouri does not have a statewide local income tax, but state law allows certain cities to levy a local earnings tax. Currently, Kansas City and St. Louis each impose a 1% earnings tax on compensation.

  • Residents of the city generally pay 1% on all taxable compensation, regardless of where it is earned.
  • Non-residents pay 1% only on income earned for work performed within the city limits.
  • Employers usually withhold the earnings tax from paychecks, but you may need to file a return to reconcile or claim refunds.

Basic formula

Local Earnings Tax = Taxable Earnings in City × 1%

For non-residents, taxable earnings are often determined by apportioning your total compensation based on where you actually worked.

Apportionment for non-residents

Many non-resident workers split time between the city and other locations or work from home. A common approach is:

Taxable Earnings = Total Compensation × (Days Worked in City ÷ Total Work Days)

Both Kansas City and St. Louis provide specific forms and instructions for claiming refunds for days worked outside the city. This calculator lets you approximate that apportionment using either:

  • Days worked in city vs. total work days, or
  • A manual % of work performed in city for each income source.

Using the Missouri local earnings tax calculator

1. Choose your city and residency

  • Select Kansas City or St. Louis under “City with earnings tax”.
  • Choose whether you are a resident or non-resident of that city.

2. Enter work days (optional but helpful)

  • Total work days in year – for example, 260 for a full-time job.
  • Days worked in city – days you were physically present and working within city limits.

If you leave these blank, the calculator will rely on the “% of work performed in city” column for each income source.

3. Add income sources

Click “Add income source” and enter for each job or income type:

  • Description – e.g., “Main job”, “Side gig”, “Consulting”.
  • Annual amount – your gross compensation for the year.
  • % of work performed in city – if you know roughly how much of that work was done in the city.

If you provide both days-worked and a percentage, the calculator will blend them and show you the effective apportionment used.

4. Review your results

The results section shows:

  • Total income entered
  • Estimated taxable income in the city
  • Local earnings tax rate (currently 1%)
  • Estimated local earnings tax owed
  • Effective local tax rate on your total income

Worked example

Suppose you are a non-resident who works in Kansas City:

  • Total salary: $80,000
  • Total work days: 260
  • Days worked in Kansas City: 130 (half the time)

Apportionment:

Taxable Earnings = $80,000 × (130 ÷ 260) = $80,000 × 0.5 = $40,000

Local Earnings Tax = $40,000 × 1% = $400

Frequently asked questions

Do I pay Missouri local earnings tax if I work remotely?

If you are a resident of Kansas City or St. Louis, your earnings tax liability generally applies to all compensation, even if you work from home outside the city. If you are a non-resident, you typically owe the earnings tax only for days you physically work within city limits. Remote work days outside the city may be eligible for a refund if you file the appropriate forms.

Are self-employment and 1099 income subject to the earnings tax?

Yes, self-employment and 1099 income are generally subject to the local earnings tax if the work is performed in the city (for non-residents) or if you are a resident of the city. The exact treatment can depend on your business structure and where services are performed, so review city guidance or consult a tax professional.

How does the local earnings tax interact with Missouri state income tax?

The 1% local earnings tax is separate from Missouri state income tax. You may see it withheld on your paycheck as a distinct line item (e.g., “KCMO Earnings Tax” or “STL Earnings Tax”). When you file your state return, you generally cannot claim the local earnings tax as a credit against Missouri state income tax, but it may be deductible on certain federal returns if you itemize state and local taxes (subject to federal SALT limits).

Can this calculator be used by employers for payroll withholding?

Employers should rely on official city and state withholding tables and instructions. This calculator can provide a rough estimate but is not a substitute for compliant payroll systems or official guidance from the Missouri Department of Revenue, Kansas City, or St. Louis.
