The Formula Explained

For a circle of radius \( r \) and central angle \( \theta \) (in radians):
\[ A = \frac{\theta}{2}\,r^{2} \]
In degrees \( \alpha \) (where \( \alpha = \theta \cdot \frac{180}{\pi} \)):
\[ A = \pi r^{2}\,\frac{\alpha}{360} \]
Arc length \( L \) and perimeter \( P \):
\[ L = r\theta,\quad P = 2r + L \]
If \( L \) is known: \(\; A = \frac{rL}{2} \).
If \( A \) and \( r \) are known: \(\; \theta = \frac{2A}{r^{2}} \).

Glossary of Variables

  • r — radius of the circle (length).
  • \(\theta\) — central angle in radians; \(\alpha\) is the angle in degrees.
  • A — area of the sector (square length).
  • L — arc length (length along the curved boundary of the sector).
  • P — perimeter of the sector (sum of arc length and two radii).

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Given: \( r = 10\,\text{cm} \), \( \alpha = 60^\circ \).

  1. Convert to radians: \( \theta = \alpha \cdot \pi/180 = \pi/3 \).
  2. Area: \( A = (\theta/2) r^2 = (\pi/3)/2 \cdot 100 = 50\pi/3 \approx 52.36\,\text{cm}^2 \).
  3. Arc: \( L = r\theta = 10\cdot\pi/3 \approx 10.47\,\text{cm} \).
  4. Perimeter: \( P = 2r + L \approx 20 + 10.47 = 30.47\,\text{cm} \).
  5. Fraction of circle: \( \alpha/360 = 1/6 \approx 0.1667 \).

FAQ

Should I use degrees or radians?

Either works. The core formula \( A=(\theta/2)r^2 \) requires radians; if you enter degrees, the calculator converts internally.

What range is valid for the angle?

For a single sector, \( 0 < \alpha \le 360^\circ \) (or \( 0 < \theta \le 2\pi \)). Values at the bounds give a degenerate line (0) or full disk.

Can I calculate from arc length?

Yes. With known \( r \) and \( L \): \( A = (rL)/2 \) and \( \theta = L/r \).

Does unit choice matter?

Yes—be consistent. The tool lets you pick length units (mm, cm, m, in, ft) and shows area in squared units accordingly, with an alternate common unit.

How accurate are the results?

Double-precision arithmetic is used with sensible rounding for display. Internally the math follows standard geometry identities.

What is the sector perimeter?

\( P = 2r + L \), i.e., the two straight radii plus the curved arc segment.

Can I print the results?

Yes. Use your browser’s Print; the page formats a clean results area for paper or PDF.
