Compounding uses the standard future value of a lump sum plus a stream of level monthly contributions. Contribution caps reflect IRS limits for the selected year and age (including $1,000 catch-up for age 50+).

The Formulas Explained

Future value with monthly compounding and end-of-month contributions:

\( \mathrm{FV} = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{12}\right)^{12t} \;+\; PMT \cdot \frac{\left(1 + \frac{r}{12}\right)^{12t} - 1}{\frac{r}{12}} \)

If \( r = 0 \): \( \mathrm{FV} = P + PMT \cdot 12t \)

Traditional IRA estimated after‑tax value: \( \mathrm{FV_{after}} = \mathrm{FV} \cdot (1 - \tau_{ret}) \)

Roth IRA estimated after‑tax value (qualified): \( \mathrm{FV_{after}} = \mathrm{FV} \)

Current‑year tax savings (Traditional, if deductible): \( \mathrm{TaxSave} = \min(C, L_{year}) \cdot \tau_{now} \)

Glossary of Variables

  • P: Current IRA balance (principal).
  • PMT: Monthly contribution used (annual contribution divided by 12, capped if IRS limit is applied).
  • r: Expected annual nominal rate of return (decimal).
  • t: Years invested (retirement age minus current age).
  • FV: Projected balance at retirement.
  • τ_now: Current marginal tax rate (decimal).
  • τ_ret: Estimated retirement marginal tax rate (decimal), applied to Traditional IRA withdrawals.
  • L_year: IRS contribution limit for the selected year (plus $1,000 catch‑up if age ≥ 50).
  • C: User-entered annual contribution.

Worked Example

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Suppose you are 35, plan to retire at 65, have an existing IRA balance of $10,000, and plan to contribute $7,000 per year, with an expected annual return of 7%. You choose a Traditional IRA, your current tax rate is 22%, and your retirement tax rate is 20%. You enable the IRS cap for 2024.

  1. Years invested: t = 65 − 35 = 30 years.
  2. Monthly contribution used: PMT = $7,000 ÷ 12 = $583.33 (under the 2024 limit of $7,000, no catch-up because age < 50).
  3. Monthly rate: r/12 = 0.07/12 ≈ 0.0058333.
  4. Future value: \( \mathrm{FV} = 10000(1+\frac{0.07}{12})^{360} + 583.33\cdot\frac{(1+\frac{0.07}{12})^{360}-1}{\frac{0.07}{12}} \).
  5. After-tax Traditional IRA value: \( \mathrm{FV_{after}} = \mathrm{FV} \cdot (1 - 0.20) \).
  6. Current-year tax savings (Traditional): \( 7000 \times 0.22 = \$1{,}540 \) (if deductible).

Note: This example assumes a constant contribution and return; actual results will vary.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Does the calculator model Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)?

No. This tool focuses on accumulation until retirement. For RMDs, refer to IRS Pub 590-B and dedicated RMD calculators.

Are Roth IRA withdrawals always tax-free?

Qualified Roth withdrawals are tax-free if the 5-year rule is satisfied and you are 59½ or older (or meet another qualifying condition). See IRS Pub 590-B.

What if my expected return is 0%?

The calculator switches to a zero-rate formula: FV = P + PMT × months.

Can I exceed the contribution limit?

Potentially, but excess contributions may incur IRS penalties. Enable the “Apply IRS cap” option to stay within allowed limits.

Which tax rates should I enter?

Use your marginal federal (and optionally consider state/local outside this tool). The current rate estimates deductible savings for Traditional contributions; the retirement rate estimates post-tax Traditional IRA value.

Is my data stored?

No. Inputs are processed client-side only in your browser for this standalone tool.
