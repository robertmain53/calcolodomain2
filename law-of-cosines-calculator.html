The Formula Explained

The Law of Cosines relates the lengths of the sides of a triangle to the cosine of one of its angles. It is used when you know two sides and the included angle (SAS) or all three sides (SSS).

To Find a Side (SAS)

Use these formulas when you know two sides and their included angle:

$$ a^2 = b^2 + c^2 - 2bc \cos(A) $$ $$ b^2 = a^2 + c^2 - 2ac \cos(B) $$ $$ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos(C) $$

To Find an Angle (SSS)

Use these rearranged formulas when you know all three sides:

$$ \cos(A) = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} $$ $$ \cos(B) = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac} $$ $$ \cos(C) = \frac{a^2 + b^2 - c^2}{2ab} $$

Glossary of Variables

To use the calculator, you must understand the triangle's labeling convention:

  • Side a: The length of the side opposite Angle A.
  • Side b: The length of the side opposite Angle B.
  • Side c: The length of the side opposite Angle C.
  • Angle A: The angle at vertex A, opposite Side a.
  • Angle B: The angle at vertex B, opposite Side b.
  • Angle C: The angle at vertex C, opposite Side c.

The sum of all angles (A + B + C) must equal 180Â° (or $\pi$ radians).

Example 1: Solving for a Side (SAS)

Let's solve a triangle where we know Side-Angle-Side (SAS).

We use the Law of Cosines formula for finding side c:

$$ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos(C) $$ $$ c^2 = 10^2 + 12^2 - 2(10)(12) \cos(45^\circ) $$ $$ c^2 = 100 + 144 - 240(0.7071) $$ $$ c^2 = 244 - 169.704 \approx 74.296 $$ $$ c = \sqrt{74.296} \approx 8.62 $$

Example 2: Solving for Angles (SSS)

Let's solve a triangle where we know Side-Side-Side (SSS).

Given:

  • Side a = 8
  • Side b = 10
  • Side c = 12

Step 1: Find Angle A

$$ \cos(A) = \frac{b^2 + c^2 - a^2}{2bc} $$ $$ \cos(A) = \frac{10^2 + 12^2 - 8^2}{2(10)(12)} $$ $$ \cos(A) = \frac{100 + 144 - 64}{240} = \frac{180}{240} = 0.75 $$ $$ A = \arccos(0.75) \approx 41.41^\circ $$

$$ \cos(B) = \frac{a^2 + c^2 - b^2}{2ac} $$ $$ \cos(B) = \frac{8^2 + 12^2 - 10^2}{2(8)(12)} $$ $$ \cos(B) = \frac{64 + 144 - 100}{192} = \frac{108}{192} = 0.5625 $$ $$ B = \arccos(0.5625) \approx 55.77^\circ $$

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

When should I use the Law of Sines vs. the Law of Cosines?

Use the Law of Cosines when you know:

  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side)
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side)

Use the Law of Sines when you know:

  • AAS (Angle-Angle-Side)
  • ASA (Angle-Side-Angle)
  • SSA (Side-Side-Angle - the ambiguous case)

This calculator is only for SAS and SSS cases.

Why did I get an "Impossible Triangle" error?

This happens if the side lengths you entered for SSS mode violate the Triangle Inequality Theorem. This theorem states that the sum of the lengths of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the length of the third side. For example, sides 3, 4, and 8 cannot form a triangle because 3 + 4 is not greater than 8.

Can I use the Law of Cosines for a right triangle?

Yes. If you use it on a right triangle (e.g., Angle C = 90Â°), $\cos(90^\circ)$ is 0. The formula $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos(90^\circ)$ simplifies to $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$, which is the Pythagorean Theorem.

What is Heron's Formula?

Heron's Formula is another way to find the area of a triangle when you are given SSS (all three sides). This calculator computes the area using the formula $Area = \frac{1}{2}ab\sin(C)$ after it has solved for the angles, but the result is the same.

Why do I only see inputs for Side a, b, and Angle C in SAS mode?

The Law of Cosines works for any SAS combination (e.g., sides b, c, and included angle A). For simplicity, this calculator uses the standard $c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos(C)$ formula. You can simply relabel your triangle to fit these inputs (e.g., if you know sides 'x', 'y' and angle 'Z', input 'x' as 'a', 'y' as 'b', and 'Z' as 'C').
