Pension Calculator

This professional pension calculator estimates retirement income from both Defined Benefit (DB) and Defined Contribution (DC) plans. It is designed for employees, HR teams, and financial planners who need fast, transparent, and accessible projections that account for salary growth, employer match, investment returns, fees, inflation, and early retirement reductions.

The Formula Explained

Defined Benefit (DB):

Pension at retirement (before reductions):
\( P_{0} = m \times YOS \times FAS \)

Early retirement reduction (if retiring before normal retirement age, NRA):
\( P = P_{0} \times \big( 1 - r_e \cdot \max(0,\;NRA - R) \big) \)

Final-average-salary approximation with constant growth g and averaging over k final years, where \(S_R\) is salary in the year before retirement:
\( FAS \approx S_R \times \dfrac{(1+g)\,\big(1 - (1+g)^{-k}\big)}{k\,g} \) for \( g \neq 0 \); otherwise \(FAS = S_R\).

Convert to today’s dollars with inflation \(i\) and years to retirement \(n\):
\( P_{\text{real}} = \dfrac{P}{(1+i)^n} \) (if no full COLA).

Defined Contribution (DC):

Balance iteration per year t with salary growth g and net return \(r_n\):
\( S_t = S_{t-1}(1+g),\quad C_t = S_t\big(c_e + \min(c_e, c_{cap})\cdot c_m\big) \)
\( B_t = (B_{t-1} + C_t)\,(1+r_n) \), with \(r_n \approx r - f\).

Withdrawal rule income:
\( I \approx \text{WR} \times B_n \) (e.g., WR = 4%).

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

Example scenario

A 35-year-old plans to retire at 67 (n = 32 years). Current salary is $60,000 with 3% growth and inflation at 2.5%.

Defined Benefit: m = 1.75%; current YOS = 5; future service included; final-average period k = 3; NRA = 67; early reduction = 6%.

  1. Projected YOS at 67: 5 + 32 = 37 years.
  2. Salary at 67: S_R = 60,000 × (1.03)^32 ≈ 146,000.
  3. FAS: apply formula with k = 3, g = 3% → FAS ≈ S_R × (1.03 × (1 − 1.03^-3) / (3 × 0.03)) ≈ 141,000.
  4. Pension before reduction: P0 = 0.0175 × 37 × 141,000 ≈ 91,500 per year.
  5. No early reduction (R = NRA). Real (today’s dollars): P_real = 91,500 / (1.025)^32 ≈ 46,500.

Defined Contribution: Current balance = $50,000; employee contrib = 10%; employer match = 50% up to 6%; return = 6%; fees = 0.2%; WR = 4%.

  1. Net return r_n ≈ 6% − 0.2% = 5.8%.
  2. Total contrib rate each year: 10% + min(10%, 6%) × 50% = 10% + 3% = 13% of salary.
  3. Iterate annually for 32 years adding contributions that grow with salary and compounding at 5.8%.
  4. Suppose B_32 ≈ $1,150,000 nominal. Real balance ≈ $1,150,000 / (1.025)^32 ≈ $540,000.
  5. Income (WR 4%): ≈ $46,000/yr nominal; real ≈ $21,500/yr (maintaining purchasing power with inflation-adjusted withdrawals).

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is the difference between Defined Benefit and Defined Contribution?

A DB plan promises a formula-based pension; a DC plan builds an investment account from contributions and returns. This calculator supports both.

Do results include taxes?

No. Outputs are pre-tax. Consult your local tax rules for after-tax estimates.

How accurate are these estimates?

They follow industry-standard formulas and assumptions, but your actual plan rules, investment performance, and employment history may differ.

Can I model early retirement penalties?

Yes. Set the normal retirement age and the annual early reduction percentage; the tool applies the reduction automatically.

How does the employer match work in the DC model?

The match is computed as match rate times your contribution, capped at the match cap. If you contribute less than the cap, the employer match is proportionally lower.

What about COLA in DB plans?

Enable the COLA toggle if your pension is inflation-indexed. If not, the tool converts nominal income to today’s dollars using your inflation assumption.

Where can I find my plan’s specific rules?

Check official plan documents or your HR portal. Public plans often publish formulas and reductions; federal FERS/CSRS details are on OPM’s website.
