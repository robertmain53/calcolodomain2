Formulas

\[ x = r \cdot \cos(\theta) \]

\[ y = r \cdot \sin(\theta) \]

\[ z = z \]

Where r is the radial distance from the z-axis, θ (theta) is the angle in the xy-plane from the positive x-axis, and z is the height above the xy-plane.

How It Works

Cylindrical coordinates represent points in 3D space using a radial distance, an angle, and a height. The conversion to Cartesian coordinates involves using trigonometric functions to project the radial distance onto the x and y axes, while the z-coordinate remains unchanged.

Example Calculation

To convert cylindrical coordinates (r=5, θ=30°, z=10) to Cartesian coordinates:

  1. Calculate x: x = 5 × cos(30°) ≈ 4.33
  2. Calculate y: y = 5 × sin(30°) = 2.50
  3. z remains the same: z = 10
  4. Result: (5, 30°, 10) in cylindrical = (4.33, 2.50, 10) in Cartesian

Frequently Asked Questions

What are cylindrical coordinates?

Cylindrical coordinates represent a point in 3D space using three values: radial distance from the z-axis (r), angle in the xy-plane (θ), and height above the xy-plane (z). This system is particularly useful for problems with cylindrical symmetry.

When should I use cylindrical coordinates instead of Cartesian?

Use cylindrical coordinates when dealing with cylindrical objects, rotational symmetry around an axis, or problems involving radial distances and angles. Examples include pipe flow, rotating machinery, and cylindrical containers.

What units should I use for the angle θ?

Our calculator accepts θ in degrees. The angle is measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis in the xy-plane, ranging from 0° to 360°.

Can r be negative?

While mathematically possible, r is typically non-negative as it represents a distance. A negative r would indicate the point is in the opposite direction from the angle θ.

Data Source and Methodology
