The Formula Explained

\( \text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \)

This formula converts an angle measured in degrees to its equivalent value in radians.

Data Source and Methodology

All calculations are based on the standard mathematical formula for converting degrees to radians. For more information, visit Wikipedia - Radian.

How It Works: A Step-by-Step Example

For example, to convert 90 degrees to radians:

  1. Start with the formula: \( \text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \)
  2. Substitute 90 for degrees: \( \text{radians} = 90 \times \frac{\pi}{180} \)
  3. Simplify: \( \text{radians} = \frac{90\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{2} \approx 1.5708 \)

Result: 90° = π/2 ≈ 1.5708 radians

Glossary of Terms

  • Degrees (°): A measure of angle where 360 degrees form a full circle. Commonly used in everyday applications, navigation, and geometry.
  • Radians (rad): The standard unit of angular measure in mathematics. One radian is the angle at the center of a circle whose arc length equals the radius. A full circle is 2π radians.
  • Pi (π): A mathematical constant approximately equal to 3.14159, representing the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is a radian?

A radian is a unit of angular measure used in mathematics. It is equal to the angle at the center of a circle whose arc length is equal to the radius. One complete circle equals 2π radians (approximately 6.28318 radians).

Why use radians instead of degrees?

Radians are often used in mathematical calculations because they provide a direct relationship between the angle and the arc length, simplifying many formulas in calculus, trigonometry, and physics.

How do I convert radians back to degrees?

To convert radians to degrees, use the formula: \( \text{degrees} = \text{radians} \times \frac{180}{\pi} \)

What are some common degree-to-radian conversions?

Common conversions include: 0° = 0 rad, 30° = π/6 rad, 45° = π/4 rad, 60° = π/3 rad, 90° = π/2 rad, 180° = π rad, and 360° = 2π rad.
