The formula explained

\[ \text{Range} = \max\{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\} \;-\; \min\{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\} \]

Glossary of variables

  • \(x_i\): the \(i\)-th observation in your dataset.
  • \(\min\): the smallest observed value.
  • \(\max\): the largest observed value.
  • Range: the difference between max and min.
  • Count \(n\): number of valid numeric observations after cleaning.

How it works: a step-by-step example

Suppose your measurements are: 7, 2, 9, 4, 4.5, -3.

  1. Clean & parse the list to obtain numeric values: \([-3,\;2,\;4,\;4.5,\;7,\;9]\).
  2. Compute minimum and maximum: \(\min=-3\), \(\max=9\).
  3. Apply the formula: \(\text{Range} = 9 - (-3) = 12\).
  4. Report count \(n=6\) and show the span in the range bar.

FAQ

What does “range” tell me about my data?

Range shows the total spread (span) between the smallest and largest values. It’s quick to compute but sensitive to outliers.

How is range different from interquartile range (IQR)?

Range uses only min and max. IQR uses the 25th and 75th percentiles and is more robust to outliers.

Do I need to sort my data first?

No. Sorting helps visualization, but the calculator finds min and max without sorting.

How are non-numeric tokens handled?

If “Ignore non-numeric” is checked, tokens that can’t be parsed into real numbers are discarded. Otherwise, an error prompts you to fix the input.

What number formats are supported?

Integers, decimals, negatives, and scientific notation (e.g., 1e-3).

Is rounding applied to calculations?

Min, max, and range are computed at full precision, then displayed using your rounding preference.

Can I copy the cleaned numbers?

Yes—use “Copy cleaned numbers” to put the normalized, optionally sorted list on your clipboard.
