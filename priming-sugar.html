Data Source and Methodology

Authoritative data and equations are adapted from:

  • John J. Palmer, How to Brew (4th ed., Brewers Publications, 2017), Bottling and Carbonation.
  • Empirical residual CO₂ vs. temperature equation used in brewing software:
    V_CO₂ = 3.0378 − 0.050062·T + 0.00026555·T² with T in °F.

All calculations follow the above sources and industry practice.

The Formula Explained

Residual CO₂ (volumes) at temperature (°F):
\( V_{\text{res}}(T) = 3.0378 - 0.050062\,T + 0.00026555\,T^2 \)

Priming sugar mass:
\( m_s = k_s \cdot V_{\text{beer,L}} \cdot \max(0, V_{\text{target}} - V_{\text{res}}) \)

  • \( m_s \) = sugar mass (g)
  • \( k_s \) = sugar yield constant (g·L⁻¹·vol⁻¹)
  • \( V_{\text{beer,L}} \) = batch volume (L)
  • \( V_{\text{target}} \) = target carbonation (vol CO₂)

Sugar Yield Constants

  • Corn sugar (dextrose): 3.86 g·L⁻¹·vol⁻¹
  • Table sugar (sucrose): 3.65 g·L⁻¹·vol⁻¹
  • DME (dry malt extract): 6.86 g·L⁻¹·vol⁻¹
  • Honey: 4.16 g·L⁻¹·vol⁻¹

How It Works: Step-by-Step Example

Scenario: 5.0 US gallon American pale ale, bottling at 68°F, target 2.5 vol CO₂, using corn sugar.

  1. Convert volume: 5.0 gal × 3.78541 = 18.93 L
  2. Residual CO₂ at 68°F: \( V_{\text{res}} = 3.0378 − 0.050062·68 + 0.00026555·68^2 \approx 0.85 \) vol
  3. CO₂ to add: 2.5 − 0.85 = 1.65 vol
  4. Sugar mass (ks=3.86): \( m = 3.86 \times 18.93 \times 1.65 \approx 120.4 \) g
  5. In ounces: 120.4 g ÷ 28.3495 ≈ 4.25 oz

Method: Boil 200–300 mL water, dissolve sugar, cool, gently mix with beer, bottle promptly.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Which temperature should I use?

Use the highest temperature after fermentation to avoid over-carbonation.

Why do different sugars need different amounts?

Fermentability and water content differ by sugar type, altering CO₂ yield per gram.

Can I prime with DME?

Yes, select DME; you’ll need more by weight than simple sugars.

What if the result is zero or negative?

Your beer already meets or exceeds your target carbonation; no sugar needed.

Glossary

  • Batch size: Total beer volume to bottle
  • Residual CO₂: CO₂ dissolved at the beer’s highest post-fermentation temperature
  • Target carbonation: Desired CO₂ volumes (L CO₂ per L beer at STP)
  • Priming sugar: Fermentable sugar added for carbonation in sealed bottles
